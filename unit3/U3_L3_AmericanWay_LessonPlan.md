## Lesson Objective

Students learn about the Era of Good Feelings under James Monroe, the transportation revolution, the Second Great Awakening, the cotton gin's role in entrenching slavery, the Missouri Compromise, and the beginning of the Adams–Jackson rivalry. They see an America growing confident and creative — and simultaneously deepening the fracture that will define the coming decades.

## Stories for the Heart

1. **The Erie Canal — digging the "Big Ditch."** In 1817 New York State began digging a canal 363 miles long from Albany on the Hudson River to Buffalo on Lake Erie. There were no steam shovels. It was dug mostly by Irish immigrant laborers, by hand, through swamp and rock, at a dollar a day. When it opened in 1825, Governor DeWitt Clinton poured a barrel of Lake Erie water into New York Harbor to join the waters. Within ten years, New York City was the largest city in North America. Tell this story with the physical hardship and the triumph — the canal as an act of imagination, daring, and sheer human labor.

2. **The deaths of Thomas Jefferson and John Adams on July 4, 1826.** July 4, 1826 — the fiftieth anniversary of the Declaration of Independence. In Monticello, Virginia, Thomas Jefferson — 83 years old, deeply ill, deeply in debt — asked his doctor: "Is it the Fourth?" Yes. He died that afternoon. In Quincy, Massachusetts, John Adams — 90 years old — spoke his final words. His last thought was of his old rival and old friend. "Thomas Jefferson still survives." He was wrong by a few hours. Both men died on Independence Day, fifty years to the day after they had pledged their lives, fortunes, and sacred honor. No novelist could have written it.

3. **The "corrupt bargain" election of 1824.** Four candidates for president. Jackson wins the most popular votes and the most electoral votes — but not a majority. The House of Representatives decides the election. Henry Clay, the Speaker of the House, throws his support to John Quincy Adams. Adams wins. Adams names Clay Secretary of State. Jackson is furious. He calls it a "corrupt bargain" and immediately begins campaigning for the next election. This is the birth of the Democratic Party — and the election of 1828 will settle the score.

## Day-by-Day Lesson Plan

### Class 1 — A Nation Growing Up: The Era of Good Feelings

**Focus:** Post-war prosperity; the Virginia Dynasty ends; immigration; Tocqueville and American democracy

**Teacher Narration (15–20 min):**
After the War of 1812, something unexpected happened in America: peace broke out — not just with Britain, but at home. There was only one political party. Newspapers were less vicious than they had been. James Monroe, the last president of the Virginia Dynasty, won re-election in 1820 nearly unopposed. One newspaper in Boston called it "The Era of Good Feelings," and the name stuck.

It was not entirely accurate. Underneath the calm, serious tensions were building. But in the late 1810s, the surface of American life was genuinely optimistic. The country was growing fast. Immigrants were arriving from Ireland, Germany, and other parts of Europe — most of them settling first in New York and New England. Cities were expanding. New states were joining the Union: Indiana, Mississippi, Illinois, Alabama, Maine, Missouri.

The French traveler Alexis de Tocqueville visited America in 1831 and wrote the most penetrating description of American democracy ever written. He noticed things no American could see because they were too close. He saw that Americans were restless and always on the move. He saw that Americans believed in equality in a way Europeans did not — not just legal equality, but a social equality, a refusal to bow to anyone. He saw that Americans formed voluntary associations for everything — churches, schools, fire brigades, reform societies. He saw both the greatness and the dangers of democratic life.

*Ask: What does it mean to be "equal" — not just in law, but in the way people treat each other? Can you think of examples from your own life?*

**Read-Aloud:**
Guerber, *The Story of the Thirteen Colonies and the Great Republic*, pp. 133–138 — teacher reads aloud or reads together.

### Class 2 — The Erie Canal and the Transportation Revolution

**Focus:** The Erie Canal; steamships; railroads; why technology reshapes nations

**Teacher Narration (15–20 min):**
In 1807, Robert Fulton's steamship, the *Clermont*, chugged up the Hudson River from New York to Albany — 150 miles in 32 hours. People called him a madman for building it. When it worked, they called it a miracle.

The Erie Canal was a more deliberate miracle. Governor DeWitt Clinton of New York proposed it, and people laughed. They called it "Clinton's Ditch." It would be 363 miles long — the longest canal in the world. It would be dug through mosquito-infested swamps and solid rock, mostly by Irish immigrant laborers who had just arrived in America. It would require 83 locks to climb over Niagara Falls without going over Niagara Falls.

It took eight years. It opened in 1825. The effect was immediate and enormous. Before the Erie Canal, it cost $100 to ship a ton of goods from Buffalo to New York City. After: $9. Farms in Ohio could now sell wheat in New York. Cities along the canal — Syracuse, Rochester, Buffalo — exploded in size. New York City became the commercial capital of the continent.

By the 1830s, railroads were beginning to be built. The first American steam locomotive ran in South Carolina in 1830. Within twenty years, thousands of miles of rail would crisscross the eastern states, shrinking the country still further.

**Read-Aloud and Sing:**
"The Erie Canal" — read the full text aloud and sing together. Work toward having students memorize the first verse and chorus by the end of the lesson.

*Ask: The Erie Canal was "Clinton's Ditch" before it worked and a marvel after. What does that tell us about how people respond to new ideas? What is one big project in our time that people thought was impossible?*

**Map Work:**
Trace the Erie Canal on a map — Albany to Buffalo, the Hudson River to Lake Erie. Mark the cities that grew along the route.

### Class 3 — The Second Great Awakening and Reform

**Focus:** Camp meetings; evangelical revival; temperance, abolitionism, women's rights — all rooted in the Second Great Awakening

**Teacher Narration (15–20 min):**
In the 1790s, something began happening on the American frontier that no one entirely planned. In the forests of Kentucky and Tennessee, people gathered in enormous outdoor camp meetings — sometimes thousands of people, camping for days. Preachers would speak for hours. People wept, cried out, fell down. This was the Second Great Awakening.

It was not primarily an eastern, educated, respectable Christianity. It was frontier Christianity — loud, physical, emotional, and democratic. Anyone could preach. Anyone could be saved. The message was urgent: God is calling you, right now, and the choice is yours.

Out of this religious fire came something unexpected: reform movements. If individual souls could be transformed, why not sinful institutions? If individuals could choose to be saved, why not abolish slavery? Why not help the poor? Why not stop alcohol from destroying families? The connections between the Second Great Awakening and every major reform movement of the 1830s and 1840s — temperance, women's rights, abolitionism — are direct and deep.

William Lloyd Garrison began publishing *The Liberator*, his abolitionist newspaper, in Boston in 1831. His first issue famously said: "I will be as harsh as truth and as uncompromising as justice... I will not equivocate — I will not excuse — I will not retreat a single inch — AND I WILL BE HEARD."

New religious movements also emerged. Joseph Smith founded the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in 1830 in upstate New York, claiming new revelation. The Mormons, as they were called, would eventually migrate west to Utah under Brigham Young, driven out of Missouri and Illinois by persecution.

*Ask: Why might religious conviction lead people to work for political and social change? What is the connection between believing something is wrong before God and deciding to do something about it?*

### Class 4 — The Cotton Gin and King Cotton

**Focus:** Eli Whitney's invention; cotton's transformation of the Deep South; the domestic slave trade; the moral paradox of technology

**Teacher Narration (15–20 min):**
Eli Whitney was a young man from Massachusetts who visited a Georgia plantation in 1793 and noticed a problem. Separating the seeds from short-staple cotton — the kind that grew almost everywhere in the South — was agonizingly slow work. It took a person most of a day to clean a single pound of cotton by hand.

Whitney went to work. Within ten days, he had built a machine that could clean fifty pounds of cotton in a single day. He called it the cotton gin — "gin" being short for "engine."

The consequences were not what anyone predicted. Whitney may have thought his invention would reduce the need for enslaved labor, since the most tedious part of the work was now mechanized. Instead, the cotton gin made cotton so profitable that planters wanted to grow as much of it as possible — and cotton was still planted and picked by human hands. The demand for enslaved people *increased*, not decreased. Cotton spread westward through Alabama, Mississippi, and Louisiana. The Deep South was transformed. By 1840, the United States was producing half the world's cotton. The trade name for this economic reality was "King Cotton."

The domestic slave trade — the buying and selling of enslaved people within the United States — expanded dramatically. Families were separated. The brutality of the plantation system intensified as more cotton was grown, more profit was demanded, and more human beings were treated as property.

**Teacher Note on Historical Proportion:** This is a moment of genuine moral darkness in the story, and it should be taught honestly. The cotton gin story is especially useful for older students because it shows how technological innovation can make evil more entrenched rather than less. It resists easy moralizing and invites genuine thought. Do not dwell sensationally; do not minimize. Teach the full picture: a creative, expanding nation whose creativity was partly built on and extended an institution of profound injustice.

*Ask: Eli Whitney probably thought his invention would help everyone. What went wrong? What does this story tell us about the relationship between technology and human choices?*

### Class 5 — The Missouri Compromise

**Focus:** Missouri's statehood crisis; Henry Clay and the compromise; Jefferson's warning; the 36° 30' line

**Teacher Narration (15–20 min):**
For thirty years, the United States had managed — barely — to avoid a direct confrontation over slavery at the national level. The Constitution had papered over the question. The Northwest Ordinance had banned slavery north of the Ohio River. And as long as free states and slave states entered the Union in rough balance, the balance in the Senate was maintained.

Then Missouri applied for statehood in 1819, and everything nearly fell apart.

Missouri was a slave territory. If it entered as a slave state, the balance would tip. A congressman from New York named James Tallmadge proposed an amendment: Missouri could enter the Union, but slavery must be gradually abolished there and no new slaves brought in. The South erupted. The vote in the House fell almost entirely along sectional lines — North versus South.

Henry Clay, the Great Compromiser, found the solution. Missouri would enter as a slave state. Maine, which had been part of Massachusetts, would enter as a free state. And slavery would be forever banned from all territory north of the 36° 30' line — the southern border of Missouri extended westward.

For a moment, the crisis was over. The nation exhaled. But Thomas Jefferson — now 76 years old, watching from Monticello — wrote to a friend: "This momentous question, like a fire bell in the night, awakened and filled me with terror. I considered it at once as the knell of the Union."

He was right that it was a fire bell. He was wrong that the Union was already dying. The Missouri Compromise held for thirty years. But it resolved nothing. It only postponed.

**Map Work:**
Draw the 36° 30' line on a map of the United States. Shade slave territory and free territory. Mark Missouri and Maine.

*Ask: Why was Henry Clay called "the Great Compromiser"? Is compromise always a good thing? When does compromise solve a problem and when does it only delay it?*

### Class 6 — The Monroe Doctrine and the "Corrupt Bargain"

**Focus:** Monroe Doctrine; the Adams–Jackson rivalry; the election of 1824; birth of the Democratic Party

**Teacher Narration (15–20 min):**
While Congress was fighting over Missouri, President Monroe's Secretary of State — John Quincy Adams, son of President John Adams — was quietly doing important work in foreign policy.

In the early 1820s, Spain's colonies in Latin America were winning their independence. Monroe and Adams worried that European powers — especially France and Russia — might try to step into the vacuum, reconquering the colonies or establishing new ones. In December 1823, Monroe delivered a message to Congress that Adams had largely written. It said: the Western Hemisphere is closed to new European colonization. If European powers try to extend their system into the Americas, the United States will regard it as a threat to its own peace and safety.

This became the Monroe Doctrine. It was a bold statement for a young, not-very-powerful nation to make. The British Navy — which also didn't want European rivals in the Americas — helped make it credible. But the doctrine established a principle that would shape American foreign policy for the next century.

Then came the election of 1824. Four men ran for president: John Quincy Adams, Andrew Jackson, William Crawford, and Henry Clay. Jackson won the most popular votes and the most electoral votes — but not a majority. The House of Representatives had to decide. Clay threw his support to Adams. Adams won. Adams named Clay his Secretary of State — the usual stepping stone to the presidency.

Jackson was furious. He said Adams and Clay had made a "corrupt bargain" — trading the presidency for a cabinet post. He began campaigning immediately for 1828. His supporters formed a new political organization: the Democratic Party. The battle lines were drawn.

*Ask: Was the election of 1824 really a "corrupt bargain"? What would you call it? Is it wrong to support someone in exchange for a political appointment?*

## Answer Key Notes

*(Full answers in U3_AnswerKey.md)*

**Virginia Dynasty:** The first five presidents included four from Virginia — Washington, Jefferson, Madison, and Monroe. Monroe was the last; after him the presidency passed to John Quincy Adams of Massachusetts, ending the Virginia monopoly.

**Era of Good Feelings:** The years under President Monroe (1817–1825), roughly, characterized by one-party politics, postwar prosperity, and relative national harmony. The name was coined by a Boston newspaper in 1817. It was partially accurate but masked growing tensions over slavery and sectionalism.

**49th Parallel:** The border established in 1818 between the United States and British Canada west of the Great Lakes, running from the Lake of the Woods to the Rocky Mountains. A practical compromise.

**Erie Canal:** A man-made waterway 363 miles long connecting the Hudson River (Albany, New York) to Lake Erie (Buffalo, New York). Opened 1825. Dramatically reduced shipping costs and enabled the economic development of the interior. New York City's commercial dominance followed.

**Cotton gin:** Invented by Eli Whitney in 1793. Mechanized the separation of cotton seeds from fiber. Made cotton farming so profitable that demand for enslaved labor in the Deep South increased dramatically.

**King Cotton:** The economic reality of the antebellum Deep South. By 1840, the U.S. produced roughly half the world's cotton; the plantation economy was almost entirely dependent on enslaved labor.

**Second Great Awakening:** A widespread Protestant religious revival, beginning c. 1795–1800, reaching its peak in the 1820s–1840s. Characterized by camp meetings, evangelical preaching, and emotional conversion. Generated the temperance, women's rights, and abolitionist reform movements.

**Missouri Compromise (1820):** Henry Clay's solution to the crisis over Missouri's statehood. Missouri entered as a slave state; Maine entered as a free state; slavery was banned in all remaining territory north of 36° 30' (Missouri's southern border extended west). Held for thirty years until the Kansas-Nebraska Act (1854) overturned it.

**36° 30' line:** The geographic boundary established by the Missouri Compromise above which slavery was prohibited in the territories (except for Missouri itself).

**Monroe Doctrine:** President Monroe's 1823 statement (largely written by Secretary of State John Quincy Adams) warning European powers against new colonization or intervention in the Western Hemisphere. Foundational to U.S. foreign policy.

**Deaths of Jefferson and Adams (July 4, 1826):** Both the second and third presidents died on the fiftieth anniversary of the Declaration of Independence — Jefferson at Monticello in the afternoon, Adams at Quincy a few hours later. Adams's last words, reportedly: "Thomas Jefferson still survives." He was wrong by hours.

**"Corrupt bargain" of 1824:** Jackson's charge that Henry Clay had supported John Quincy Adams in the House election in exchange for appointment as Secretary of State. Whether it was corrupt or merely political calculation is debated. It drove Jackson to form the Democratic Party and campaign relentlessly for 1828.
