# *The New World Made Ready* — Unit 3, Lesson 2
## Prospects, Uncertainties, and War
### 1801–1815 | 6–7 Classes

---

## Lesson Objective

Students learn about events during the presidencies of Thomas Jefferson and James Madison, including the Louisiana Purchase, the Corps of Discovery, the Barbary Pirates, and America's conflict with the British in the War of 1812. They understand Jefferson's contradictions — the man who wrote "all men are created equal" while holding enslaved people — and Madison's hard test in the war that nearly destroyed the young republic.

## Stories for the Heart

**1. Jefferson Walks to His Own Inauguration**
March 4, 1801. Thomas Jefferson walks from his boarding house to the Capitol — no carriage, no ceremony, wearing plain clothes. He gives a short, quiet speech calling for unity: *"We are all Republicans, we are all Federalists."* Then he walks back to his boarding house and sits down to dinner at the common table. No one makes way for him. He waits his turn. This is what Jefferson thought a republic should look like.

**2. The Louisiana Purchase — A Constitutional Problem**
Napoleon Bonaparte needs money for his European wars and offers to sell France's vast Louisiana Territory to the United States for $15 million. Jefferson desperately wants it — it would double the size of America and control the Mississippi River. But he has always insisted that the president can only do what the Constitution explicitly permits. The Constitution says nothing about buying foreign territory. Jefferson agonizes. Then he buys it anyway, and asks Congress to ratify the deal after the fact. His critics call it hypocritical. Jefferson says the opportunity was too great to pass up. He never fully resolved the contradiction.

**3. The Corps of Discovery**
May 1804 – September 1806. Jefferson sends Meriwether Lewis and William Clark west to explore the new territory. They travel from St. Louis to the Pacific Ocean and back — 8,000 miles. They encounter over 50 Native American nations. Sacagawea, a Shoshone woman, joins the expedition near present-day North Dakota and proves indispensable as a guide and interpreter. Lewis's journals describe landscapes, animals, and peoples no American had ever seen. Read entries aloud: the first sighting of the Rocky Mountains, the first view of the Pacific. Students should feel the scale of the continent opening before them.

**4. The Barbary Pirates — Americans Enslaved**
For years, the Barbary States of North Africa (Tripoli, Algiers, Tunis, Morocco) had seized European and American merchant ships and enslaved their crews. Sailors were chained, forced into labor, ransomed for enormous sums, or simply never returned. The United States had been paying tribute — essentially protection money — to keep its ships safe. Jefferson refused. In 1801 he sent the Navy to the Mediterranean. The Marines landed on the shores of Tripoli. After four years of war, Tripoli agreed to stop taking American ships. The phrase "to the shores of Tripoli" in the Marines' Hymn commemorates this campaign.

*Teacher note: Tell this as a story of Americans enslaved and Americans freed — not merely a trade dispute. The captives had names, families, and long years of suffering. This connects directly to the broader theme of liberty and its violation that runs through the whole curriculum.*

**5. Aaron Burr Kills Alexander Hamilton**
July 11, 1804. Aaron Burr — Vice President of the United States — challenges Alexander Hamilton to a duel at Weehawken, New Jersey, over a political insult. Hamilton fires into the air or deliberately misses. Burr does not. Hamilton is mortally wounded and dies the next morning at age 49. He was arguably the most brilliant financial mind the founding generation produced. Burr, charged with murder in two states, flees south and eventually is tried for treason (acquitted) for a separate scheme to carve out a western empire. He lives another 30 years in disgrace. Hamilton lives in the history books.

**6. Dolley Madison and the Portrait of Washington**
August 24, 1814. British troops march on Washington, DC. Madison has already fled. Dolley Madison is still in the Executive Mansion, supervising the removal of important documents. She refuses to leave until the great Gilbert Stuart portrait of Washington is taken down from the wall and saved. She cuts it from its frame herself. The British burn the Executive Mansion, the Capitol, and much of the city. When the Madisons return, the walls are blackened but still standing. The portrait survives.

**7. The Defense of Fort McHenry and "The Star-Spangled Banner"**
September 13–14, 1814. British warships bombard Fort McHenry outside Baltimore for 25 hours. Francis Scott Key, a Washington lawyer, watches from a British prisoner ship where he has gone to negotiate a release. Through the night he cannot see the fort. At dawn — over the rockets' red glare, the bombs bursting in air — the American flag is still flying. He takes a letter from his pocket and writes a poem. It is set to a popular drinking tune. Within weeks it is being sung across America.

**8. The Battle of New Orleans**
January 8, 1815. Andrew Jackson leads a ragged army of Tennessee militia, free Black soldiers, Choctaw warriors, and Jean Lafitte's pirates against 8,000 British veterans. The British charge in formation across open ground; Jackson's men fire from behind earthworks. In thirty minutes, over 2,000 British are killed or wounded. American casualties: 13 dead. Jackson becomes a national hero overnight.

One detail worth noting: the war had officially ended two weeks earlier with the Treaty of Ghent, signed December 24, 1814. Word hadn't reached Louisiana yet. The battle was fought after the peace was signed — neither side knew it.

## Day-by-Day Lesson Plan

### DAY 1 — Context: Jefferson Takes Office

**Focus:** Jefferson's vision; the Louisiana Purchase; the constitutional problem

**Teacher Narration:** Tell Story for the Heart #1 (Jefferson walks to his inauguration). Describe Jefferson's vision of America — a nation of independent farmers, limited government, states' rights, no entangling foreign alliances. Then tell #2 (Louisiana Purchase) — including the constitutional problem honestly. Jefferson was not a hypocrite by nature; he was a man who chose an enormous opportunity over a principle he held dear. Students should be able to hold the complexity.

Introduce Marbury v. Madison briefly: Jefferson's conflict with Chief Justice John Marshall, and Marshall's masterstroke — establishing judicial review while appearing to rule against himself. The Supreme Court will decide what the Constitution means. This lasts.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What was the Louisiana Purchase? Why was Jefferson unsure whether to buy it?"
*Form IIA:* "Jefferson believed the president should only do what the Constitution explicitly allowed. Why then did he buy the Louisiana Territory? Was he right to do it? What does this tell us about the tension between principles and opportunities?"

---

### DAY 2 — Story Day: Lewis and Clark

**Focus:** The Corps of Discovery; Sacagawea; the continent opens

**Narration:** Tell Story for the Heart #3 in full. Read aloud Lewis's journal entry describing the first sight of the Rocky Mountains, or Clark's description of reaching the Pacific. Show maps. Let students feel the scale — this was American territory, but no American had seen it. The expedition was also a diplomatic mission: Lewis and Clark met with 50 Native nations. Some encounters were friendly. Some were tense. Some of the peoples they met would later face removal.

**Student Living Book:** *Meet Thomas Jefferson*, pp. 29–62.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "Who was Sacagawea? How did she help Lewis and Clark?"
*Form IIA:* "Lewis and Clark's expedition was scientific, military, diplomatic, and geographic all at once. What was Jefferson hoping to accomplish by sending them? Did they succeed?"

---

### DAY 3 — Story Day: The Barbary Pirates

**Focus:** Americans enslaved; American naval power; liberty for all

**Narration:** Tell Story for the Heart #4 (Barbary Pirates) in full — including the captivity. This is not merely a military adventure story. American sailors were enslaved — chained, forced to labor, some dying, some ransomed after years. The parallel to the slavery already existing in America was noticed and named at the time, including by some of the enslaved sailors themselves. Jefferson had spent years insisting the United States should not pay tribute. When he became president, he finally had the power to stop it.

Also: introduce the Act Prohibiting Importation of Slaves (1807). Congress and Jefferson outlawed the importation of enslaved people from abroad. The domestic slave trade, however, continued — and expanded.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "Who were the Barbary Pirates? What did they do to American sailors?"
*Form IIA:* "Americans were being enslaved by the Barbary Pirates at the same time slavery existed in America. Did anyone notice the contradiction? What does this tell us about the difficulty of living up to the principles in the Declaration?"

### DAY 5 — Story Day: Hamilton, Burr, and the War of 1812

**Focus:** The duel; impressment; Madison's war

**Narration:** Tell Story for the Heart #5 (Hamilton-Burr duel) — brief but significant. Hamilton was at the height of his influence when he died. His death ended an era of the founding generation.

Then introduce the War of 1812: impressment of American sailors, British interference with American trade, Tecumseh's alliance with the British on the frontier. Madison asks Congress to declare war. It is not a popular war. The early campaigns are disasters. The British burn Washington.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What was impressment? Why did it lead to war with Britain?"
*Form IIA:* "Why did America go to war with Britain in 1812? Was it a good idea? What were the risks?"

---

### DAY 6 — Story Day: Fort McHenry and the Star-Spangled Banner

**Focus:** Dolley Madison; the burning of Washington; the flag

**Narration:** Tell Story for the Heart #6 (Dolley Madison and the portrait) and #7 (Fort McHenry) together. The burning of Washington is a low point; the dawn flag over Fort McHenry is the turning point. Read "The Star-Spangled Banner" aloud — all the way through. The fourth stanza names freedom explicitly: *"Then conquer we must, when our cause it is just / And this be our motto: 'In God is our trust.'"*

Then tell Story for the Heart #8 (Battle of New Orleans) — including the detail about the peace treaty already being signed. History sometimes ends with accidental victories.

**Read Aloud:** "The Star-Spangled Banner" full text. Then listen to or sing stanza 1 together.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What happened at Fort McHenry? Who wrote 'The Star-Spangled Banner'?"
*Form IIA:* "Francis Scott Key wrote his poem watching from a British prison ship. What made him write it? What do you think he felt when he saw the flag still flying at dawn?"

## Jefferson's Contradictions — A Teacher Note

Thomas Jefferson held enslaved people while writing "all men are created equal." He knew this was wrong — he wrote about it agonizingly in *Notes on the State of Virginia* — and he could not bring himself to act. His estate at Monticello was built and maintained by enslaved labor. He freed only a handful of people in his will, and only after his death, when they could no longer cost him anything.

This does not make Jefferson a villain and nothing else. He was one of the most brilliant political thinkers who ever lived, and the Declaration he wrote has inspired liberation movements across the world and across two centuries. But the contradiction is real, it was known to him, and students should know it too. The goal is not to reduce Jefferson to his failures, nor to excuse them — it is to understand how a man can be genuinely great and genuinely wrong at the same time, and what that means for how we read his words.
