# *The New World Made Ready*
## A Family History & Civics Curriculum
### Unit 3 — The Early Republic, 1789–1848
**Teacher Overview & Guide**

---

> *"The United States of America is an 'experiment' in republican government. The experiment had seemed to be on the verge of failure by 1787, but the Constitution gave it a second chance. This is the story of the beginning decades of that second chance."*
> — Hillsdale College K-12 Curriculum

## Unit 3 at a Glance

**Period Covered:** 1789–1848
**Central Question:** How did the new American republic grow, test its principles, and begin to fracture under the weight of its contradictions?
**Weeks:** ~9 weeks (36 classes at 30–45 minutes each)
**Lessons:** 4 lessons + unit summative

| Weeks | Lesson | Content |
|-------|--------|---------|
| 1–2 | **Lesson 1: The New Government** | Washington and Adams presidencies; precedents; first political parties |
| 3–4 | **Lesson 2: Prospects, Uncertainties, and War** | Jefferson and Madison; Louisiana Purchase; Barbary Pirates; War of 1812 |
| 5–6 | **Lesson 3: The American Way** | Era of Good Feelings; Erie Canal; cotton gin; Missouri Compromise |
| 7–9 | **Lesson 4: Manifest Destiny** | Jackson; Trail of Tears; Texas; Mexican-American War; abolitionists |
| 9 | **Unit Summative** | Midunit test (after Lesson 2) · Final test · Writing assignment |

*Midunit Test covers Lessons 1–2. Final Unit Test covers Lessons 3–4.*

## Unit 3 Enduring Ideas

Students who complete this unit will carry these ideas forward:

1. The presidency of George Washington was indispensable in establishing precedents for free self-government and keeping America out of what would have been a disastrous foreign war.
2. The opportunities available to American citizens in these decades were exceedingly rare in the history of nations — land, self-governance, economic freedom, and religious liberty on an unprecedented scale.
3. Although the American nation made great political and economic strides in these early decades, the fractures over slavery widened and deepened, setting the course toward civil war.
4. American democracy expressed itself in unique ways and had a deep effect on the habits, thoughts, and character of Americans — observed most clearly by Alexis de Tocqueville.

---

## A Note on Historical Proportion

Unit 3 contains some of the most morally complex content in the curriculum. The cotton gin's role in entrenching slavery, the Trail of Tears, and the growing abolitionist movement all appear here. These must be taught honestly and with appropriate weight. At the same time, they should be taught in proportion — embedded in the full narrative of an expanding, creative, self-governing nation, not made the single organizing lens for the era. The Missouri Compromise is both a political failure (it delayed but did not solve the slavery question) and a demonstration that the republic was trying, however inadequately, to manage an impossible tension without violence. Students should hold both truths.

The Barbary Pirates episode (Lesson 2) must include the full story of American captivity and enslavement — not merely a trade dispute. See standing instruction in project memory.

|-----|-------|
