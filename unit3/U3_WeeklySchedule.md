## WEEK 1
**Lesson 1 — The New Government**
*Focus: Washington's presidency; precedents; Hamilton vs. Jefferson*

| Day | Activity | Resources |
|-----|----------|-----------|
| **Day 1** | **Context: The Experiment Begins.** Teacher narrates: the nation has a Constitution — now what? Washington is inaugurated in New York City. Every action he takes will set a precedent. What does it mean to be the first? | Lesson 1 plan; image of Federal Hall inauguration |
| **Day 2** | **Story Day: Washington's Presidency.** The cabinet. The Whiskey Rebellion. The French Revolution and neutrality. The Farewell Address. Washington's two-term precedent. | Lesson 1 plan; *Meet George Washington*, pp. 55–63 |
| **Day 3** | **Hamilton vs. Jefferson: Two Visions of America.** Teacher narrates the debate: Hamilton's industrial nation of banks and tariffs vs. Jefferson's republic of independent farmers. Both were right about something. Both were wrong about something. | Lesson 1 plan; Farewell Address excerpt |
| **Day 4** | **Narration + Copywork.** Oral (Form IA): "What traditions did George Washington give us as president?" Written (Form IIA): "What did Washington warn about in his Farewell Address? Why do those warnings still matter?" | Narration prompts U3-L1 |

## WEEK 3
**Lesson 2 — Prospects, Uncertainties, and War**
*Focus: Jefferson's presidency; Louisiana Purchase; Barbary Pirates; Lewis and Clark*

| Day | Activity | Resources |
|-----|----------|-----------|
| **Day 1** | **Context: Jefferson Takes Office.** Teacher narrates Jefferson's walk to his own inauguration — no carriage, no ceremony, just a man walking to work. His vision: a republic of yeoman farmers, limited government, westward expansion. Then: the Louisiana Purchase. Napoleon needs money. Jefferson has no constitutional authority — but he does it anyway. America doubles in size overnight. | Lesson 2 plan; *Meet Thomas Jefferson*, pp. 29–45 |

The birth of the U.S. Marine Corps' role in North Africa. | Lesson 2 plan — **include full captivity story per standing instruction** |
| **Day 4** | **Narration + Notebooking.** Oral (Form IA): "Tell me about Lewis and Clark's expedition." Written (Form IIA): "What was the Louisiana Purchase and why did Jefferson struggle with the decision to buy it?" Notebooking page: Lewis and Clark. | Narration prompts U3-L2; NB_U3_02_CorpsOfDiscovery |

## WEEK 5
**Lesson 3 — The American Way**
*Focus: Era of Good Feelings; immigration; transportation revolution; Erie Canal*

| Day | Activity | Resources |
|-----|----------|-----------|
| **Day 1** | **Context: A Nation Growing Up.** Teacher narrates: after the War of 1812, America enters a period of prosperity and expansion. The "Era of Good Feelings" under James Monroe. The Virginia Dynasty ends. New immigrants arrive from Europe. The transportation revolution begins: canals, steamboats, railroads. | Lesson 3 plan |
| **Day 2** | **Story Day: The Erie Canal.** 363 miles from Albany to Buffalo — dug largely by Irish immigrant laborers. It connects the Hudson River to the Great Lakes and opens the interior of the continent. Cities boom. Goods flow. Read and sing "The Erie Canal." | Lesson 3 plan; "The Erie Canal" (song text) |
| **Day 3** | **Story Day: The Second Great Awakening.** Camp meetings, revival preachers, emotional conversions across the frontier. New denominations. The beginning of the great reform movements — temperance, women's rights, abolition — all rooted in evangelical Christianity's conviction that sinful institutions can be changed. | Lesson 3 plan |
| **Day 4** | **Narration + Copywork.** Oral (Form IA): "What was the Erie Canal and why was it important?" Written (Form IIA): "How did the Second Great Awakening connect to the reform movements of the 1830s and 1840s?" | Narration prompts U3-L3 |

**Read-Aloud This Week:** Guerber, pp. 133–138; "The Erie Canal" (song)

## WEEK 6
**Lesson 3 — The American Way, continued**
*Focus: Cotton, slavery, King Cotton; Missouri Compromise*

| Day | Activity | Resources |
|-----|----------|-----------|
| **Day 1** | **Story Day: The Cotton Gin and King Cotton.** Eli Whitney's invention was supposed to reduce the need for slave labor — it did the opposite. Cotton became vastly more profitable; planters demanded more land and more enslaved people to work it. The Deep South transforms. The domestic slave trade expands. | Lesson 3 plan |
| **Day 2** | **Story Day: Life Under Slavery.** Honest, age-appropriate account of daily life — field work, family separation, the slave trade, culture and faith within slave communities. Use a slave narrative excerpt if available. Proportion: do not dwell sensationally; do not minimize. | Lesson 3 plan — *Historical Proportion principle applies* |
| **Day 3** | **Story Day: The Missouri Compromise.** Missouri wants to enter the Union as a slave state. The nation nearly splits. Henry Clay brokers the deal: Missouri slave, Maine free, slavery banned above 36°30'. Jefferson, now old and watching from Monticello, calls it "a fire bell in the night." | Lesson 3 plan; map of Missouri Compromise line |
| **Day 4** | **Narration + Notebooking.** Oral (Form IA): "What was the Missouri Compromise?" Written (Form IIA): "Why did the cotton gin make the slavery problem worse, not better?
