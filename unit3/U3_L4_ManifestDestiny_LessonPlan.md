# *The New World Made Ready* — Unit 3, Lesson 4
## Manifest Destiny
### 1829–1848 | 8–9 Classes

## Lesson Objective

Students learn about the presidency of Andrew Jackson — his democratic appeal, his war on the Bank of the United States, and his removal of Native Americans in defiance of the Supreme Court. They follow American expansion to Texas and the Pacific through the Mexican-American War. They meet the abolitionists — Frederick Douglass, Harriet Tubman, William Lloyd Garrison — whose voices were growing louder as the contradiction at the heart of the republic deepened. They understand what "manifest destiny" meant: a national conviction of providential purpose, and the genuine costs that conviction imposed on others.

## Stories for the Heart

1. **Andrew Jackson — "Old Hickory."** Born in the Carolina backcountry to Scots-Irish immigrant parents. Orphaned at fourteen. Prisoner of war at thirteen — a British officer demanded he clean his boots; Jackson refused; the officer slashed him across the face with a sword, leaving a scar he carried all his life. He became a lawyer, a judge, a congressman, a senator, a planter, a duelist who killed a man who insulted his wife. He became the Hero of New Orleans. He was the first president who had not come from the Virginia or Massachusetts gentry — the first president of the common man. Tell this story fully and honestly: Jackson was capable of great courage, genuine loyalty, and profound injustice.

2. **The Trail of Tears.** The Cherokee Nation was not a primitive people. They had a written language, developed by Sequoyah — one of the few people in history to single-handedly create a writing system for a language that had none. They had a constitution, a newspaper, schools, churches, farms. They sued the state of Georgia in the Supreme Court — and won. Chief Justice John Marshall ruled that Georgia had no authority over Cherokee lands. Andrew Jackson, President of the United States, ignored the ruling. He is reported to have said, "John Marshall has made his decision; now let him enforce it." In the winter of 1838–1839, the U.S. Army forced roughly 16,000 Cherokee men, women, and children to march from Georgia to Indian Territory in present-day Oklahoma. About 4,000 died of cold, hunger, and disease along the way. The Cherokee called it *Nunna daul Tsuny* — "the place where they cried." We call it the Trail of Tears.

3. **Frederick Douglass's account of the slave breaker.** Frederick Bailey was born into slavery in Maryland — he never knew the exact year. His mother was enslaved; he believed his father was a white man. As a boy he was sent to Baltimore and taught himself to read. When he was sixteen, his master sent him to a man named Edward Covey, known as a "slave breaker" — someone who specialized in breaking the spirit of enslaved people who showed too much independence. Covey beat Douglas regularly. One morning, Douglass fought back. They struggled for two hours. Covey never beat him again. Douglass later wrote that the fight was "the turning point in my career as a slave." Read the relevant passage from the *Narrative* aloud. It is available free at the Internet Archive. Few pieces of American prose have as much compressed moral weight.

4. **The Battle of the Alamo.** In 1836, a Mexican army of several thousand soldiers under General Antonio López de Santa Anna surrounded a former Spanish mission in San Antonio called the Alamo, where about 189 Texan defenders had taken shelter. Among them was Davy Crockett, the famous frontiersman from Tennessee. For thirteen days, the defenders held out. On March 6, the Mexican army broke through the walls. Every defender was killed. The news reached Sam Houston's Texan army as they were retreating east. It stopped the retreat. "Remember the Alamo!" became the battle cry. Six weeks later, Houston's army surprised Santa Anna's forces at the Battle of San Jacinto. They attacked shouting "Remember the Alamo! Remember Goliad!" The battle lasted eighteen minutes. Texas was free.

5. **The Spot Resolutions and a young congressman named Lincoln.** When President James Polk asked Congress for a declaration of war against Mexico in May 1846, he claimed that Mexico had shed "American blood upon American soil." A young first-term congressman from Illinois named Abraham Lincoln was not convinced. He introduced a series of resolutions demanding that Polk show Congress the precise "spot" of ground where the first blood had been shed — and whether that spot was really American soil or Mexican territory. The "Spot Resolutions" went nowhere; Lincoln was largely ignored. But he was asking the right question: how did this war begin, and did the President tell the truth about it? Lincoln did not stop asking hard questions.

## Day-by-Day Lesson Plan

### Class 1 — Andrew Jackson: The Common Man's President

**Focus:** Jackson's biography; the Nullification Crisis; Jacksonian democracy; the Bank War

**Teacher Narration (15–20 min):**
Andrew Jackson was unlike any president America had seen. Every president before him had been born into privilege — Virginia planters or Massachusetts lawyers. Jackson was born in a log cabin on the Carolina frontier, the son of Irish immigrants. He was a fighter, a duelist, a general, and a man who inspired fierce loyalty and fierce hatred in roughly equal measure.

His democratic appeal was genuine. He believed the government had been run by and for the elite — the merchants, the bankers, the educated gentry. He intended to change that. He opened the White House to ordinary people at his inauguration party. (The result was something close to a riot.) He believed in the common man's instincts and common man's rights.

But his presidency also revealed the limits of democratic instinct without moral principle. He vetoed the recharter of the Bank of the United States, arguing that it favored wealthy Eastern merchants and European investors over ordinary Americans. His supporters cheered. Economists were more skeptical — the resulting financial instability helped cause the Panic of 1837, just after he left office.

He faced a direct challenge from his own Vice President, John C. Calhoun of South Carolina, over the Nullification Crisis. South Carolina claimed it could "nullify" — refuse to enforce — a federal tariff it considered unconstitutional. Jackson's response was direct: no state has the right to nullify federal law. "Our Federal Union — it must be preserved." He threatened to send troops. South Carolina backed down.

He was capable of holding the Union together against secession — and simultaneously willing to destroy the legal rights of an entire people.

*Ask: What did Jackson mean by "the common man"? Did his policies actually help ordinary people?*

### Class 2 — The Trail of Tears

**Focus:** Indian Removal Act; the Cherokee Nation; Sequoyah; Worcester v. Georgia; the forced march

**Teacher Narration (15–20 min):**
Before Andrew Jackson was elected president, Sequoyah — a Cherokee man who may or may not have been able to read English — spent twelve years single-handedly creating a writing system for the Cherokee language. He had no model for how to do this. He invented it from first principles: a syllabary of 86 characters, one for each syllable in the Cherokee language. When he presented it to the Cherokee leaders in 1821, they tested it. Messengers were sent miles away; Sequoyah's daughter wrote down what they dictated; the messages were sent back to Sequoyah, who read them aloud. The leaders were astonished. Within months, thousands of Cherokee had learned to read and write their own language. The Cherokee Phoenix — a bilingual newspaper — began publication in 1828.

This was the Cherokee Nation that Andrew Jackson decided to remove from their homeland.

The Indian Removal Act of 1830 authorized the federal government to negotiate treaties with Native American tribes to exchange their eastern lands for lands west of the Mississippi. "Negotiate" was a polite word. The pressure was immense, and many of the treaties that resulted were signed by small groups of Cherokees who had no authority to speak for the Nation.

The Cherokee sued the state of Georgia and took their case to the Supreme Court. In 1832, Chief Justice John Marshall ruled in *Worcester v. Georgia* that Georgia had no authority over Cherokee territory — that the Cherokee Nation was a distinct political community with its own sovereignty. Jackson is reported to have dismissed the ruling. The removal proceeded.

In 1838–1839, U.S. soldiers rounded up the Cherokee — men, women, children, elderly — at gunpoint and marched them westward in the dead of winter. About 4,000 died.

**Teacher Note on Historical Proportion:** The Trail of Tears must be taught honestly, without minimization. It was a moral catastrophe, compounded by the fact that it was done in defiance of a Supreme Court ruling — meaning Jackson did not merely do wrong, but did wrong while ignoring the legal mechanism designed to stop him. Students should understand both the injustice and the system of law that failed to prevent it. This is not a reason to despair of law and government; it is a reason to take both seriously.

*Ask: Sequoyah created a writing system for his people. What does that tell us about his love for his people? How does the Trail of Tears change how you think about Andrew Jackson?*

**Map Work:**
Trace the Trail of Tears on a map — from Georgia and the Southeast to Indian Territory in present-day Oklahoma.

### Class 3 — The Texas Revolution and the Alamo

**Focus:** Americans in Tejas; the Texas Revolution; the Alamo; the Republic of Texas

**Teacher Narration (15–20 min):**
In 1820, Mexico was still a Spanish colony. In 1821, it won independence and became the Republic of Mexico. Among the territories it inherited from Spain was a thinly populated region far to the north called Tejas.

The Mexican government had a problem: Tejas was so remote and sparsely settled that it was nearly impossible to defend or govern. Their solution was to invite American settlers in. Stephen F. Austin, a Missouri-born businessman, negotiated a contract with the Mexican government to settle 300 families in Texas. The settlers came. By 1830, there were 30,000 Americans in Texas — and fewer than 8,000 Mexicans.

The Americans brought their ways with them, including slavery — which Mexico had abolished. Tensions mounted. Mexico tried to restrict further immigration and enforce its laws more tightly. The American settlers resisted. In 1835, fighting began. In early 1836, the Texan provisional government declared independence.

Meanwhile, a small force of Texan volunteers occupied the Alamo, a former Spanish mission in San Antonio. On February 23, 1836, Santa Anna's army arrived — thousands of soldiers against fewer than 200 defenders. The siege lasted thirteen days. On March 6, the army breached the walls. Every defender was killed.

The Alamo fell. But it gave Sam Houston's retreating army time to organize. On April 21, Houston's forces attacked Santa Anna's camp at San Jacinto at midday — the Mexicans were resting. Texans shouted "Remember the Alamo!" The battle lasted eighteen minutes. Santa Anna was captured. Texas was free.

Texas became the Republic of Texas in 1836. It petitioned immediately to join the United States. The petition was delayed — admitting Texas would mean admitting a new slave state, and the slavery question made Congress reluctant. Texas remained an independent republic for ten years.

*Ask: Was the Texas Revolution a story of heroic freedom fighters, or of Americans who went into a foreign country, didn't follow its laws, and then fought to leave? Can it be both?*

### Class 4 — The Abolitionists

**Focus:** William Lloyd Garrison; Frederick Douglass; Harriet Tubman; the Underground Railroad; Sojourner Truth; Elizabeth Cady Stanton

**Teacher Narration (15–20 min):**
While Andrew Jackson was reshaping politics on one side of the country, a different kind of reshaping was happening in the North.

William Lloyd Garrison began publishing *The Liberator* in Boston in 1831 — an abolitionist newspaper that did not compromise and did not apologize. Garrison called for the immediate, unconditional abolition of slavery. He burned a copy of the Constitution at public meetings, calling it "a covenant with death and an agreement with hell" because it tolerated slavery. His tactics were extreme; his argument was simple: slavery is wrong and must end now.

Sojourner Truth was born enslaved in New York around 1797. She walked away from her master in 1826, just before New York State abolished slavery, saying that God had given her a new name and a new mission. She became one of the most powerful speakers for abolition and, later, for women's rights.

Elizabeth Cady Stanton organized the Seneca Falls Convention in 1848 — the first women's rights convention in American history. The women there produced a document modeled deliberately on the Declaration of Independence, beginning: "We hold these truths to be self-evident: that all men and women are created equal."

Levi and Catharine Coffin were Quakers in Newport, Indiana, who transformed their home into a station on the Underground Railroad. They helped more than 2,000 enslaved people escape north over twenty years.

Harriet Tubman was born enslaved in Maryland around 1822. She escaped in 1849, walking alone through the night, following the North Star. She went back. Nineteen times. She led more than 300 people to freedom and never lost a passenger. She carried a pistol and used it to encourage those who wanted to turn back: "Dead folks tell no tales." She was called "Moses" by the people she freed.

Frederick Douglass escaped slavery in 1838 and taught himself to speak in public. His *Narrative*, published in 1845, was so vivid and so powerful that many readers refused to believe an enslaved man had written it. He lectured across America and in Britain, wrote three autobiographies, published his own newspaper, and became one of the most influential voices in American public life. His account of his encounter with the slave breaker Edward Covey — read it aloud in Class 5.

*Ask: The abolitionists used very different methods — Garrison burned the Constitution; Tubman carried a pistol; Douglass wrote and spoke. What do their different approaches tell us about how moral change happens?*

**Read-Aloud (15–20 min):**

- What did Douglass say the fight changed in him?
- He said it gave him "a renewed determination to be a *free man*." What do you think he meant by freedom — legal freedom, or something more?
- Why do you think Douglass chose to write this account down and publish it?
- What does this passage tell us about what slavery really was — not just as a legal status, but as a system of power over human beings?

### Class 6 — Manifest Destiny and the Mexican-American War

**Focus:** Texas annexation; Polk; the war; Abraham Lincoln's Spot Resolutions; the Mexican Cession; Oregon

**Teacher Narration (15–20 min):**
By the 1840s, a phrase was circulating in American politics and newspapers: "manifest destiny." A journalist named John O'Sullivan coined it in 1845. He wrote that it was America's "manifest destiny to overspread and to possess the whole of the continent which Providence has given us."

It expressed something real about how many Americans felt: that the continent lay open before them, that freedom and democracy were good things worth spreading, that God or Providence had arranged things so that America would stretch from sea to sea. The sentiment was optimistic, expansionist, and sincerely held.

It also had costs. Manifest destiny required moving Native Americans off their land — often violently. It required fighting a war with Mexico. It required the question of whether slavery would spread into the new territories, a question that would tear the country apart within fifteen years.

Texas had been waiting for annexation since 1836. In 1845, Congress voted to admit Texas to the Union. Mexico, which had never recognized Texas independence, considered this an act of war. President James Polk was not unhappy about the confrontation. He believed the United States should also acquire California and the Southwest from Mexico. He sent troops to the disputed border region between the Rio Grande (the American claim) and the Nueces River (the Mexican claim). Fighting broke out in April 1846.

A young Illinois congressman named Abraham Lincoln demanded to know exactly where on American soil the first blood had been shed — his "Spot Resolutions." He suspected the first blood had been shed on contested or Mexican territory. He was largely ignored. The war was popular.

The U.S. Army performed brilliantly. Generals Zachary Taylor in the north and Winfield Scott in the south defeated larger Mexican armies. Scott's army marched all the way to Mexico City — the "Halls of Montezuma." The war ended with the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo in 1848. Mexico ceded an enormous territory to the United States: present-day California, Nevada, Utah, most of Arizona, and parts of New Mexico, Colorado, and Wyoming. America had reached the Pacific.

Meanwhile, in 1846, the U.S. and Britain had settled the border of Oregon Country — the 49th Parallel extended to the Pacific. America now stretched from the Atlantic to the Pacific, from the 49th Parallel to the Rio Grande.

**Teacher Note:** The Mexican-American War is a genuine case of a more powerful nation provoking a war with a weaker neighbor to take its territory. Lincoln was right to question it. At the same time, the territories gained — California especially — were transformed by American settlers and became among the most productive regions in the world. Students can hold both truths. Manifest destiny is neither simply heroic nor simply villainous; it is complicated in exactly the way American history often is.

*Ask: Lincoln asked where exactly the first blood was shed. Why does that question matter? What is President Polk's responsibility for starting the war?*

**Map Work:**
Mark the Mexican Cession on a map. Mark Oregon Country and the 49th Parallel. Show what the United States looked like before 1845 and after 1848.

### Class 7 — The Oregon Trail and the Great Migration

**Focus:** Why people went west; the Oregon Trail; Brigham Young and the Mormons; the cost of the journey

**Teacher Narration (15–20 min):**
While soldiers were fighting in Mexico, thousands of ordinary American families were loading their belongings into covered wagons and setting out on a 2,000-mile journey west to Oregon Country. They followed a route called the Oregon Trail, which ran from Independence, Missouri, through the Great Plains, over the Rocky Mountains, and down to the Willamette Valley in Oregon.

Why did they go? Some went for land — free land, fertile land, more land than most of them had ever imagined owning. Some went because they were failing where they were — in debt, sick, without prospects. Some went because of what historians call "Oregon fever" — a kind of contagion of optimism. The journey took five to six months. Oxen pulled the wagons. Families walked most of the way to spare the animals. Cholera, dysentery, drowning at river crossings, getting lost in early mountain snow — these were real dangers, and thousands died on the trail.

But thousands more made it. And when they arrived in Oregon, they found what they had hoped for: good land, a mild climate, and the possibility of a new start.

Brigham Young led a different kind of westward migration. After Joseph Smith's murder in Illinois in 1844, Young led the Mormon community west — not to Oregon, but to the remote Great Salt Lake Valley, then part of Mexico. They arrived in 1847. Young looked out over the salt flats and mountains and said, "This is the right place." He was an organizational genius. Within a few years, thousands of Mormons had settled the valley, built cities, and established an irrigation system that made the desert farmable. They called their territory Deseret; it eventually became the state of Utah.

*Ask: What would make a family leave everything they knew and walk 2,000 miles with no guarantee of success? What do you think they were hoping for?*

## Answer Key Notes

*(Full answers in U3_AnswerKey.md)*

**Andrew Jackson:** Seventh president (1829–1837). Born on the Carolina frontier, Scots-Irish descent. Hero of the Battle of New Orleans (1815). First president not from the Virginia or Massachusetts gentry; champion of the "common man." Opposed the Bank of the United States; dealt with the Nullification Crisis by threatening force. Signed the Indian Removal Act (1830); defied the Supreme Court ruling in *Worcester v. Georgia* to force Cherokee removal.

**Nullification Crisis (1832–1833):** South Carolina claimed the right to nullify — refuse to enforce — a federal tariff. Jackson threatened military force. South Carolina backed down. Calhoun resigned as Vice President. Jackson's position: federal law is supreme; no state may nullify it.

**Indian Removal Act (1830):** Authorized the President to negotiate treaties exchanging Native American lands east of the Mississippi for lands in Indian Territory to the west. Led to forced removals of multiple tribes, most notably the Cherokee.

**Sequoyah:** Cherokee scholar who single-handedly created the Cherokee syllabary — a writing system of 86 characters — in 1821. Led to widespread Cherokee literacy and the publication of the Cherokee Phoenix newspaper.

**Trail of Tears:** The forced relocation of approximately 16,000 Cherokee people from their homeland in the southeastern U.S. to Indian Territory (present-day Oklahoma) in 1838–1839. Approximately 4,000 died. The relocation was ordered despite the Supreme Court ruling in *Worcester v. Georgia* that Georgia had no jurisdiction over Cherokee lands.

**The Alamo (February–March 1836):** A thirteen-day siege of approximately 189 Texan defenders against Santa Anna's army of several thousand at a former mission in San Antonio. All defenders were killed. The battle delayed the Mexican advance and galvanized Texan resistance. "Remember the Alamo!" became the rallying cry at the Battle of San Jacinto (April 21, 1836), where Sam Houston's army defeated Santa Anna and secured Texas independence.

**Republic of Texas (1836–1845):** The independent nation formed after the Texas Revolution. Existed as a republic for nearly ten years before U.S. annexation in 1845.

**William Lloyd Garrison:** Abolitionist newspaper publisher (*The Liberator*, founded 1831). Advocated immediate, unconditional emancipation. Called the Constitution "a covenant with death" for tolerating slavery.

**Frederick Douglass:** Born into slavery in Maryland c. 1818. Escaped in 1838. Taught himself to read; became the most powerful abolitionist orator in America. Published *Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass* (1845). His account of the slave breaker Covey remains one of the most powerful passages in American literature.

**Harriet Tubman:** Born enslaved in Maryland c. 1822. Escaped in 1849. Returned nineteen times to lead more than 300 people to freedom via the Underground Railroad. Never lost a passenger. Later served as a Union spy and nurse during the Civil War.

**Underground Railroad:** A network of safe houses and guides — "conductors" — that helped enslaved people escape to free states and Canada. Not an actual railroad; the name was metaphorical. Levi and Catharine Coffin of Newport, Indiana, helped approximately 2,000 people escape.

**Manifest destiny:** The 19th-century belief that American territorial expansion across the continent was both inevitable and divinely ordained. Coined by journalist John O'Sullivan in 1845. Used to justify westward expansion, including conflicts with Native Americans and Mexico.

**Mexican-American War (1846–1848):** Triggered by disputed Texas annexation and the boundary question between the Rio Grande and the Nueces River. U.S. forces under Generals Taylor and Scott defeated Mexican armies. Ended with the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo (1848), which ceded California, Nevada, Utah, and parts of Arizona, New Mexico, Colorado, and Wyoming to the United States ("the Mexican Cession").

**Spot Resolutions:** A series of resolutions introduced in the House by Abraham Lincoln (1848) demanding that President Polk identify the precise location ("spot") where Mexican forces first shed "American blood on American soil." Lincoln questioned whether the first blood had actually been shed on American or contested/Mexican soil, challenging the administration's justification for the war.

**Mexican Cession:** The territory acquired from Mexico in the Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo — present-day California, Nevada, Utah, most of Arizona, and parts of New Mexico, Colorado, and Wyoming. Together with Texas annexation and the Oregon settlement (49th Parallel), it completed the continental United States.
