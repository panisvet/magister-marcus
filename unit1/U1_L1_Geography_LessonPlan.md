# Unit 1, Lesson 1 — The Lands, Waters, and Peoples of America
## Teacher Lesson Plan

**Hillsdale content source:** Unit 1, Lesson 1 (3–5 PDF), pp. 28–31  
**Time:** 6–7 classes, 30–40 minutes each  
**Forms:** IA (grades 3–4) and IIA (grades 5–6)

---

## Lesson Objective

Students learn the physical and political geography of what would become the United States, including its major landforms, waterways, climate advantages, and Native American inhabitants.

| Term | Lesson context |
|------|----------------|
| terrain | The physical features of the land |
| climate | The pattern of weather in a region over time |
| natural resources | Materials from the land useful for human life |

## Lesson Sequence (6–7 Classes)

### Class 1 — Open with a Story, Begin Topography

**Open with this story (teacher tells aloud, 10 minutes):**
Begin in the middle of the ocean. Paint the scene for your students: it is 1492. Three small Spanish ships are sailing west across the Atlantic. The crew has been at sea for weeks. The ship is cramped, the food is salt meat and hard biscuit, and no one knows what — if anything — lies ahead. Then, in the early morning hours, a lookout sees something on the horizon. He shouts: *¡Tierra! ¡Tierra!* — "Land! Land!" What does the crew do?

Tell this opening as vividly as you can. You are setting the stage for the entire year of history. The Hillsdale guide says this well: "Every story has a setting, and the true story of history is no different." This geography lesson establishes the habit — students will use their imaginations to enter every place and period they study.

**Transition:** "Before we follow Columbus ashore, let's learn the land he found — and the land that stretches far beyond that little island."

**Begin topographic map work (20 minutes):**
Hand out blank physical maps. Begin at the Atlantic coast and move west together. Label each feature as you introduce it. Use vivid sensory language: describe the sound of Niagara Falls, the silence of the Great Plains, the heat of the Mojave. Ask simple observation questions as you go:
- "What would be different about traveling through the Appalachian Mountains compared to the Great Plains?"
- "Why do you think so many major cities in America grew up near rivers or bays?"

**Close:** Oral narration prompt.

> **Oral Narration Prompt (both forms):**
> "Tell me about America's landscape — start at the Atlantic and head west. What would you see? What changes?"

---

### Class 2 — Finish Topography, Begin Political Map

**Review (5 min):** Point to unmarked features on the projected map. Students identify them.

**Continue map labeling (15 min):** Complete the topographic map through the Pacific. Emphasize: two oceans border this land. This will matter enormously for the history we'll study all year.

**Advantages of the land (10 min):** Discuss with your students why America was a good place to live and build a civilization:
- Rich soils, long growing seasons, mild climate in most regions
- Abundant fresh water and navigable rivers
- Vast forests for fuel and building
- Natural barriers (oceans, mountains) providing security

Ask: "If you were starting a new settlement from scratch, what would you want the land to offer? Does America have those things?"

**Begin political map (10 min):** Introduce the 13 original colonies in founding order. Use the second blank map. You will return to this in Lesson 2 when you study each colony in depth.

---

### Classes 3–4 — Native Peoples

**Teacher introduction (15 min):**
Tell students: America was not empty when Columbus arrived. Long before 1492, people had lived here for thousands of years. Introduce the idea of the land bridge across the Bering Strait during the last Ice Age — glaciers lowered sea levels, creating a walkable corridor between Asia and North America.

Introduce the four civilizations from the Hillsdale content:

**Mayas** — Central America; built great stone cities; advanced astronomy and mathematics; by 1492, their peak civilization had long passed, but millions of their descendants still lived in the region.

**Aztecs** — Mexico; built Tenochtitlán, one of the largest cities in the world; a warrior empire with vast trade networks.

**Incas** — South America; built roads through the Andes mountains; a great mountain empire stretching along the Pacific coast.

**Hopewell** — Eastern North America; built enormous earthen mounds; extensive trade routes connecting tribes from the Gulf of Mexico to the Great Lakes. These are among the ancestors of the tribes the first English settlers would meet.

Place these groups on the physical map together. Use images if available (see Hillsdale lesson's image list: photos of Maya, Aztec, Inca, and Ancestral Pueblo ruins).

**Discussion questions (10 min):**
- "Where did the first peoples of America come from? How do we think they got here?"
- "How did different groups of Native Americans live? Were they all the same?"
- "What are some ways in which Native Americans and Europeans lived differently?"

**Narration (10 min):**

> **Oral Narration Prompt:**
> "Tell me about the first peoples of America — where they came from and what kinds of civilizations they built."

---

### Class 5 — Finish Native Peoples, Connect to Columbus

**Review and connect (20 min):**
Return to the map. Point to each region where the major civilizations lived. Note: the native peoples of North America were enormously diverse — some in small hunter-gatherer groups, some in large cities, some farming, some fishing, some trading across hundreds of miles.

Then say: "Now return to the Atlantic, to the horizon. It is 1492. To Columbus and all the people of Europe, Africa, and Asia — none of this was known. This entire half of the world was invisible to them. That is about to change."

**Important note for teachers:** Present the relationships between Native Americans and European explorers honestly. They were not uniformly hostile, nor uniformly peaceful. They varied widely — by group, by time, by circumstance. Students should avoid broad generalizations in either direction.

### Classes 6–7 — Map Assessment and Notebooking

**Map work (20 min):** Students label maps from memory (teacher-created assessment). Both forms complete the same physical and political map tasks.

**Notebooking (15 min):** Students complete the Lesson 1 notebooking page. They choose one scene to illustrate from the lesson — a landscape feature, a native civilization's city or mound, or the crew of Columbus sighting land — and write a caption below.

**Copywork:**

> **Form IA copywork:** Copy the following sentence into your notebook.  
> *"Every story has a setting, and the true story of history is no different."*

> **Form IIA dictation:** Prepare to write from dictation:  
> *"America had excellent and untouched soils, temperature and rainfall averages were ideal, and timber was plentiful. Waterways were plentiful and mostly navigable."*

## Post-Lesson Narration Prompts

Use one or more of the following after each class reading.

**Form IA (1–3 complete sentences):**
- "Describe two different landforms in America and what each one looks like."
- "Write 2–3 sentences about the Mayas, Aztecs, or Hopewell — who they were and where they lived."
- "Why is America a good land for people to live? Write two reasons."

**Form IIA (full paragraph, 5–8 sentences):**
- "Write a paragraph describing the American landscape from east to west. Use at least four specific geographic features."
- "Write a paragraph explaining how the first peoples came to North America, how they spread across the continent, and what kinds of civilizations they built."
- "Write a paragraph about why America's geography was an advantage for people who came to live here. Include at least three specific advantages."

## Story for the American Heart

**Story 1:** Columbus's crew sighting land (Hillsdale SH #1 for this lesson)

Tell this story in your own words, drawing on the following details: Columbus's three ships had been sailing for over a month. His crew was frightened and increasingly mutinous. On October 12, 1492, at 2 a.m., a sailor named Rodrigo de Triana spotted a white cliff in the moonlight from the Pinta's bow. He shouted "Tierra!" A cannon was fired to signal the fleet. Columbus himself claimed he had seen a light the previous evening — a matter that became a dispute about who should receive the reward the Spanish crown had promised to the first man to sight land.

The land was a small island in the Bahamas that Columbus called San Salvador ("Holy Savior"). He waded ashore carrying the royal banner of Spain. He knelt and prayed.

## Questions for the American Mind

(Hillsdale formative discussion questions for this lesson)

1. What are some of the different kinds of terrain in the American landscape?
2. Why is the American landscape a good place for people to live?
3. How is the terrain different between different regions?
4. Where did the native peoples of North and South America come from? How do we believe they came?
5. How did different groups of Native Americans live?
6. What are some ways in which Native Americans and Europeans lived differently?

---

## What to Look For in Narrations

A strong narration from this lesson will include:
- At least 3–4 specific geographic features named and described
- A basic explanation of the land bridge theory
- At least one of the four native civilizations named with a specific detail
- The connection to Columbus at the end (Europe didn't know this world existed)

A narration that mentions only vague generalities ("America is big and has mountains and rivers") needs more reading and more practice, not more questions.
