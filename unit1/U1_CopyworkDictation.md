# U1 Copywork and Dictation
## *Magister Marcus: The New World Made Ready*
**Unit 1 — All Lessons | Both Forms**

---

## How to Use This Document

**Form IA (Grades 3–4):** Student copies the passage by hand into their notebook. Take as many sessions as needed. Neatness counts. Do not correct spelling while copying — that defeats the purpose.

**Form IIA (Grades 5–6):** Student reads the passage three times, sets it aside, and writes it from memory (dictation). Parent reads it aloud once if needed. Student checks their own work and corrects in a different color.

**Pace:** One passage per 1–2 lessons. No need to rush. A single well-copied sentence is worth more than a page done carelessly.

## Passage 1 — The Mayflower Compact (1620)
**Lesson 2 — Exploration and Settlement**

**Source:** Mayflower Compact, 1620 (public domain, full text at avalon.law.yale.edu)

**Passage:**
> "We whose names are underwritten, having undertaken, for the Glory of God and advancement of the Christian Faith and Honour of our King and Country, a voyage to plant the first colony in the northern parts of Virginia, do by these presents solemnly and mutually in the presence of God and one of another, covenant and combine ourselves together into a civil Body Politick, for our better ordering and preservation."

**Teacher note:** This is the core sentence of the Compact. Read it aloud before students copy. Point out: they wrote a *covenant* — a solemn agreement — because they needed a government and had no king's charter for where they landed. This is the spirit of American self-government: when no one is in charge, free people make their own law.

**Form IA copywork:** Copy the passage above in your best handwriting. (May take 2–3 sessions.)

**Form IIA dictation:** After reading three times and setting aside — write from memory. Expected to capture the key phrases: "Glory of God," "advancement of the Christian Faith," "civil Body Politick," "covenant and combine."

## Passage 2 — John Winthrop, "A Modell of Christian Charity" (1630)
**Lesson 3 — The Colonies in Profile**

**Source:** John Winthrop, sermon delivered aboard the *Arbella*, 1630 (public domain)

**Passage:**
> "We shall be as a city upon a hill. The eyes of all people are upon us. So that if we shall deal falsely with our God in this work we have undertaken, and so cause him to withdraw his present help from us, we shall be made a story and a byword through the world."

**Teacher note:** Winthrop spoke these words to the Puritans before they landed in Massachusetts. He believed they were building something the world was watching — a community that would prove what a godly people could do. The phrase "city upon a hill" comes from Jesus's Sermon on the Mount. It has been quoted by American leaders ever since, most famously by Ronald Reagan. Read this passage in its original seriousness before having students copy it.

**Form IA copywork:** Copy the first two sentences only: *"We shall be as a city upon a hill. The eyes of all people are upon us."*

**Form IIA dictation:** Full passage from memory. Expected to capture key phrases exactly.

## Passage 3 — Edward Winslow, Letter on the First Thanksgiving (1621)
**Lesson 2 — Exploration and Settlement**

**Source:** Edward Winslow, letter to a friend in England, December 1621 (public domain, full text at plimoth.org)

**Passage:**
> "Our harvest being gotten in, our governor sent four men on fowling, that so we might after a special manner rejoice together after we had gathered the fruit of our labors. Many of the Indians coming amongst us, and among the rest their greatest king Massasoit, with some ninety men, whom for three days we entertained and feasted, and they went out and killed five deer."

**Teacher note:** This is the *entire* eyewitness account of the First Thanksgiving. Students are usually surprised at how short it is. Point out what Winslow does not mention — and what he does: the harvest, the fowling, the rejoicing, the Wampanoag guests, Massasoit, the deer. The passage is humble and matter-of-fact. They were grateful. They feasted. That's the whole story.

**Form IA copywork:** Copy the passage in full. (May take 2 sessions.)

**Form IIA dictation:** Full passage from memory.

## Passage 4 — George Washington, Rules of Civility (c. 1745)
**Lesson 4 — Major Events in the Colonies, Day 4**

**Source:** George Washington's handwritten copy of *Rules of Civility & Decent Behavior in Company and Conversation* (based on a 16th-century French Jesuit work; Washington copied all 110 rules as a boy; full text at founders.archives.gov)

**Passage A:**
> "Be not apt to relate news if you know not the truth thereof."

**Passage B:**
> "Speak not evil of the absent, for it is unjust."

**Passage C:**
> "Use no reproachful language against any one, neither curse nor revile."

**Teacher note:** Washington copied all 110 rules by hand as a teenager. These rules taught him restraint, honor, and courtesy. They shaped a man who never lost his temper publicly, never slandered his enemies in print, and maintained his dignity under conditions of extreme pressure. Ask students: *Which of these rules is hardest to follow? Why?*

**Form IA copywork:** Copy Passages A and B. Write one sentence after each explaining what it means in your own words.

**Form IIA copywork/dictation:** Copy all three passages (A, B, C). Then write Passage A and B from dictation. Write two sentences after each: one explaining the meaning, one describing a situation where the rule would be hard to follow.

## Passage 5 — Benjamin Franklin, Autobiography (c. 1771)
**Lesson 4 — Major Events in the Colonies, Day 7**

**Source:** Benjamin Franklin, *The Autobiography of Benjamin Franklin*, Part I (Project Gutenberg, free: gutenberg.org/ebooks/20203)

**Passage:**
> "I have always thought that one man of tolerable abilities may work great changes, and accomplish great affairs among mankind, if he first forms a good plan, and, cutting off all amusements or other employments that would divert his attention, makes the execution of that same plan his sole study and business."

**Teacher note:** Franklin wrote this in his 60s, looking back on how he had lived. He is describing his own method. Read this passage to students and ask: *Do you think this is true? What "amusements" might Franklin have cut off? What was his "plan"?* This passage encapsulates the American ideal of deliberate self-improvement — Franklin's gift to future generations.

**Form IA copywork:** Copy the passage in full. (May take 2–3 sessions.)

**Form IIA dictation:** After three readings, write from memory. Expected to capture "one man of tolerable abilities," "great changes," "forms a good plan," "sole study and business."

## Optional Extension — A Rule of Your Own
*(For any student who finishes early or wants a challenge)*

**Form IA:** Choose your favorite of Washington's 110 Rules of Civility (view the full list at founders.archives.gov). Copy it in your notebook and illustrate it.

**Form IIA:** Write your own "Rule of Civility" in the style of Washington — one sentence, formal language, describing a behavior that makes a person worthy of trust. Then write a short paragraph explaining why you chose that virtue.
