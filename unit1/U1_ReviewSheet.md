## PERSONS — Know who each person is and what they did

**Lesson 2 — Exploration and Settlement**
- **Christopher Columbus** — Italian explorer sailing for Spain; first voyage to San Salvador, 1492
- **John Smith** — Captain who saved Jamestown; required colonists to work for food
- **Pocahontas** — Powhatan girl who helped at Jamestown; later married John Rolfe
- **Lord De La Warr** — Royal governor who arrived in time to save Jamestown
- **John Rolfe** — Grew tobacco in Jamestown; married Pocahontas
- **William Bradford** — Pilgrim governor; kept a journal of Plymouth's founding
- **Massasoit** — Wampanoag leader who made peace with the Pilgrims
- **John Winthrop** — Puritan leader; said Massachusetts Bay would be "a city upon a hill"

**Lesson 3 — The Colonies in Profile**
- **Roger Williams** — Expelled from Massachusetts; founded Rhode Island; champion of religious toleration
- **Lord Baltimore** — Founded Maryland as a haven for Catholics
- **William Penn** — Quaker; founded Pennsylvania on principles of fairness and religious freedom
- **James Oglethorpe** — Founded Georgia as a fresh start for English debtors

**Lesson 4 — Major Events in the Colonies**
- **Jonathan Edwards** — Puritan preacher; his sermons helped spark the Great Awakening
- **George Washington** — Virginia militia officer; survived Fort Duquesne; later America's first president
- **Benjamin Franklin** — Printer, inventor, writer; proposed the Albany Plan of Union
- **George III** — King of England during the French and Indian War; later taxed the colonies

## TERMS AND TOPICS — Know what each term means

**Lesson 2**
- *Niña*, *Pinta*, and *Santa María* — Columbus's three ships
- Taíno — the Native people Columbus first met at San Salvador
- Columbian Exchange — transfer of peoples, plants, animals, and diseases between Old and New Worlds
- smallpox — European disease that devastated Native American populations
- Virginia Company — English trading company that founded Jamestown
- indentured servants — workers who traded years of labor for passage to America
- "Starving Time" — terrible winter at Jamestown when many colonists died
- tobacco — cash crop that saved Virginia
- Pilgrims — Separatists who sailed on the *Mayflower* seeking religious freedom
- *Mayflower* — ship that brought the Pilgrims to Plymouth in 1620
- Mayflower Compact — agreement to govern themselves by just laws; early self-government
- House of Burgesses — first elected assembly in the colonies (Virginia, 1619)
- religious freedom — the right to worship as one chooses
- self-government — the people having a say in the laws they live under
- Wampanoag — Native people of the Plymouth region; made peace with the Pilgrims
- Puritans — English Protestants who founded Massachusetts Bay Colony

**Lesson 3**
- Quakers — peaceful religious group; founded Pennsylvania with William Penn
- colonial assemblies — elected bodies that made local laws in each colony
- colonial governors — appointed by the Crown to oversee each colony
- militia — armed citizens ready to defend their community
- Triangle Trade — trade route connecting Europe, West Africa, and the Americas
- indentured servitude — working without wages for a set number of years
- chattel slavery — owning a person as property for life, passed to children
- Middle Passage — ocean route on which enslaved Africans were shipped to the Americas

**Lesson 4**
- King Philip's War — 1675–1676 conflict between colonists and Native Americans in New England
- English Civil War — conflict in England (1640s) that taught colonists about the limits of royal power
- Glorious Revolution — 1688 change in English monarchy; produced the English Bill of Rights
- English Bill of Rights — limited the king's power; gave rights to Parliament and the people
- "salutary neglect" — England's policy of leaving the colonies mostly alone
- representation — the idea that people should have a voice in the laws they live under
- The Great Awakening — religious revival of the 1730s–1740s; gave all colonies a shared experience
- French and Indian War — 1754–1763 war between Britain and France in North America
- Iroquois Confederacy — alliance of six Native nations in the Northeast; played both sides in the war
- Albany Congress — 1754 meeting where Franklin proposed a plan for colonial unity
- Fort Duquesne — French fort in Pennsylvania; site of Washington's first battles
- Treaty of Paris — 1763 treaty that ended the French and Indian War; Britain gained most of North America east of the Mississippi
