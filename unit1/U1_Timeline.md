### 1492
**Columbus Lands on San Salvador**
Christopher Columbus and his crew — sailing for Spain aboard the *Niña*, *Pinta*, and *Santa María* — landed on an island in the Caribbean. Columbus called the people he met "Indians" because he believed he had reached Asia. He had found something far greater: a continent entirely unknown to Europe.

---

### 1585–1590
**Roanoke: The Lost Colony**
England's first attempt to settle North America ended in mystery. The colonists on Roanoke Island in present-day North Carolina vanished without explanation. The only clue left behind was the word *CROATOAN* carved on a post.

---

### 1607
**Jamestown Founded**
English settlers established the first permanent English colony in North America at Jamestown, Virginia. The early years brought terrible hardship — disease, starvation, and conflict with the Powhatan people. John Smith, John Rolfe, and Lord De La Warr were among the figures who shaped the colony's survival.

---

### 1619
**First Africans Arrive in Virginia**
The first Africans brought to the English colonies arrived at Point Comfort, Virginia, aboard a Dutch ship. They were among the first of many who would be enslaved in British North America. Their labor would shape the colonies — and their suffering would shape the American conscience for centuries.

---

### 1620
**The Mayflower and Plymouth**
Separatist Pilgrims and other settlers crossed the Atlantic aboard the *Mayflower*. Before landing, they signed the *Mayflower Compact* — an agreement to govern themselves by the consent of those who signed it. This was one of the first acts of self-government in America. The Wampanoag leader Massasoit helped the Pilgrims survive their first winter.

---

### 1621
**The First Thanksgiving**
After a successful harvest, the Plymouth colonists held a feast with their Wampanoag neighbors. Edward Winslow's brief letter — the only first-hand account — describes the gathering. The Pilgrims gave thanks to God for their survival.

---

### 1630
**Massachusetts Bay Colony — "A City upon a Hill"**
Puritan leader John Winthrop led a large group of settlers to Massachusetts Bay. In his sermon aboard the *Arbella*, he told his fellow Puritans: *"We must consider that we shall be as a city upon a hill. The eyes of all people are upon us."* Massachusetts Bay became the largest and most influential of the New England colonies.

---

### 1636
**Roger Williams Founds Rhode Island**
Banished from Massachusetts Bay for his views on religious freedom and the rights of Native people, Roger Williams founded Providence — later Rhode Island. It became a colony where people of different faiths could live together.

---

### 1681
**William Penn Founds Pennsylvania**
King Charles II granted William Penn — a Quaker — a large tract of land in North America as payment of a debt. Penn founded a colony based on religious toleration and fair treatment of Native peoples. Philadelphia became one of the most prosperous cities in colonial America.

---

### 1688
**Glorious Revolution; English Bill of Rights**
England's Parliament removed King James II and replaced him with William and Mary, who agreed to rule under a new *Bill of Rights*. The colonists watched. They learned: even kings must follow the law. These English rights were their rights too.

---

### 1732
**Georgia Founded — The Last Colony**
James Oglethorpe founded Georgia as a colony where English debtors could start fresh, and as a buffer against Spanish Florida. Georgia was the thirteenth and last of Britain's original North American colonies.

---

### 1730s–1740s
**The Great Awakening**
A wave of religious revival swept through all thirteen colonies. Preachers like Jonathan Edwards and George Whitefield drew enormous crowds. For the first time, colonists from New Hampshire to Georgia shared a common emotional and spiritual experience — and began to feel like one people.

---

### 1754
**Albany Congress — Franklin's Plan of Union**
As war with France loomed, representatives of seven colonies met in Albany, New York. Benjamin Franklin proposed a "Plan of Union" — a single government to coordinate colonial defense. The plan was rejected, but the idea that the colonies might govern themselves together had been planted.

---

### 1754–1763
**The French and Indian War**
France and Britain fought for control of North America. The young Virginia militiaman George Washington fought in some of the war's first engagements. The war ended with a British victory and the *Treaty of Paris (1763)* — France lost nearly all its North American territory. But the war left Britain deep in debt. Parliament decided the colonists should help pay. That decision would lead directly to revolution.

---

### 1755
**Braddock's Defeat — Washington Survives**
British General Braddock led an army toward Fort Duquesne. The French and their Native allies attacked from the forest. The British were routed. Braddock was killed. George Washington had two horses shot from under him and four bullets pass through his coat — and survived without a wound. He organized the retreat.

---

### 1763
**Treaty of Paris — Unit 1 Ends Here**
Britain gained Canada and all French territory east of the Mississippi River. The thirteen colonies were now hemmed in from the west by British proclamation — and about to push back.
