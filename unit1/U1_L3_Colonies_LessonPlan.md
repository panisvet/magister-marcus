## *Magister Marcus: The New World Made Ready*

## Lesson Overview

**Period:** 1630–1732
**Big Idea:** The thirteen English colonies were not built by a master plan — they were built by individuals seeking land, freedom, and a better life. From this scattered beginning, a distinctly American character began to form.

**Key to the Lesson:** England's approach to colonization — decentralized, private, built on individual initiative — produced something no one planned: a people who expected freedom as their birthright.

**Persons:** Lord Baltimore, Roger Williams, Peter Stuyvesant, William Penn, James Oglethorpe
**Terms:** religious freedom, township, Quakers, self-government, colonial assemblies, colonial governors, militia, Triangle Trade, indentured servitude, chattel slavery, slave ships, Middle Passage, individualism
**Civics connections ⚖️:** self-government, religious freedom, U.S. Civics Q.73, Q.75

## Day-by-Day Lesson Plan

### DAY 1 — Map Day: The Thirteen Colonies

**Focus:** Geography of the three colonial regions; why place shapes people

**Teacher Narration:**
Open a labeled 13-colonies map. Tell the story of how England colonized differently from Spain and France — not with government soldiers but with private people, families, and joint-stock companies. Each colony had its own founding story.

Walk through the three regions with the map:
- **New England** (Massachusetts, Connecticut, Rhode Island, New Hampshire): rocky soil, long winters, fishing and trade; founded mostly for religious reasons
- **Middle Colonies** (New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Delaware): fertile farmland, mixed peoples from many countries, religious diversity
- **Southern Colonies** (Virginia, Maryland, North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia): warm climate, long growing season, large plantations, cash crops

**Student Activity:**
Students label and color-code a 13-colonies outline map with three colors (one per region). Label at least one city per region: Boston, Philadelphia, Williamsburg.

*Form IA:* Oral narration after map work — "Tell me which region each of these places belongs to: Boston, Philadelphia, Williamsburg."
*Form IIA:* After labeling, write 2–3 sentences in notebook describing the geographic differences between the regions.

**Resources:** PCL outline map (lib.utexas.edu/maps) or generate with Grok; Lesson 3 map from Hillsdale PDF

### DAY 2 — Story Day: New England and Middle Colonies

**Focus:** Founding stories; religious freedom; Roger Williams, William Penn

**Key Stories for the Heart:**
1. **Roger Williams** — Banished from Massachusetts for preaching that government cannot command a man's conscience. Walked alone into the winter wilderness and founded Rhode Island as a place where all could worship freely. Williams was the first colonial leader to insist on religious toleration as a matter of principle.
2. **William Penn** — A Quaker nobleman who received a charter from the king as repayment of a debt. Penn saw Pennsylvania as a "holy experiment" — a place where Quakers and others could live in peace. He treated the Lenape people with unusual fairness and set up a government with an elected assembly.

**Teacher Narration Notes:**
- John Winthrop's "city upon a hill" vision: the Massachusetts Bay Puritans believed they were building a community that would serve as an example to the world — not a place of tolerance, but a place of visible godliness
- Contrast: Williams and Penn expanded freedom; Winthrop and the Massachusetts leadership expected conformity
- Mention the Salem Witch Trials (1692) briefly — even in godly communities, fear can produce great injustice
- Middle Colonies: New York came from the Dutch colony New Amsterdam; Peter Stuyvesant was the last Dutch governor, famously stubborn; Lord Baltimore founded Maryland as a haven for Catholics

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "Why did Roger Williams get in trouble in Massachusetts? What did he do about it?"
*Form IIA:* "How did the founding of Rhode Island and Pennsylvania show that the idea of religious freedom was growing in the colonies? What does this tell us about human conscience and government?"

**Copywork prompt** (see U1_CopyworkDictation.md, Passage 2):
*"We shall be as a city upon a hill, the eyes of all people are upon us."* — John Winthrop, 1630
*Form IA:* Copy the passage.
*Form IIA:* Copy from memory (dictation) after three readings.

### DAY 3 — Story Day: Southern Colonies, Colonial Life, and Hard History

**Focus:** Southern colonies; daily life; slavery and the Triangle Trade

**Key Stories for the Heart:**
1. **James Oglethorpe and Georgia** — Founded 1733 as a haven for debtors and the poor. Oglethorpe envisioned a colony of small farmers, no slavery, no large plantations. The dream didn't last — Georgia eventually allowed slavery — but Oglethorpe's founding vision shows how much individuals shape history.
2. **Hard History: The Middle Passage** — The Triangle Trade connected England, Africa, and the Americas. From Africa came enslaved people, captured in wars or sold by rival tribes, packed onto ships in conditions of terrible suffering. Roughly 350,000 Africans were brought to the North American colonies. The first Africans arrived at Jamestown in 1619. Slavery hardened into law slowly: in 1662, Virginia declared that children born to enslaved mothers were also enslaved for life.

**Teacher Narration Notes:**
- Indentured servitude vs. chattel slavery: indentured servants were mostly white Europeans who traded years of labor for passage; after their contract they were free. Chattel slavery was permanent and hereditary — the person was property.
- The Quakers were the first organized group to oppose slavery in the colonies. This contrast matters: even in the colonial period, Americans debated whether slavery was right.
- Tell stories of enslaved people with dignity: their names where known, their resistance, those who won freedom
- Don't moralize as narrator — let the facts and stories do the work

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What was the Middle Passage? Why was it such a terrible experience?"
*Form IIA:* "What is the difference between indentured servitude and chattel slavery? Why did chattel slavery grow in the Southern colonies? Who first spoke out against it?"

## Lesson 3 Answer Key Notes
*(Full answers in U1_AnswerKey.md)*

**Lord Baltimore:** Catholic proprietor who founded Maryland as a haven for Catholics; established early religious toleration law (Act Concerning Religion, 1649)
**Roger Williams:** Puritan minister banished from Massachusetts; founded Rhode Island; first American to argue for separation of church and state and religious freedom for all
**William Penn:** Quaker; founded Pennsylvania as a "holy experiment" of peaceful, tolerant governance; made fair treaties with the Lenape
**James Oglethorpe:** Founded Georgia (1733) as a haven for debtors; opposed slavery in its early charter; served as military leader defending the colony

**Chattel slavery** = permanent, hereditary ownership of a person as property; distinguished from indentured servitude (temporary, contracted labor)
**Middle Passage** = the ocean voyage from Africa to the Americas endured by enslaved Africans; named for the middle leg of the Triangle Trade
**Salutary neglect** = England's policy of loose oversight of the colonies; gave colonists room to develop self-governing habits ⚖️

## Civics Connections ⚖️

- **U.S. Civics Q.73:** "Why did colonists come to America?" — economic opportunity, religious freedom, to escape oppression
- **U.S. Civics Q.75:** "What group of people was taken and sold as slaves?" — Africans
- **Self-government:** Colonial assemblies developed because England wasn't paying close attention — this habit became central to American identity
