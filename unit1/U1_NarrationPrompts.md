# U1 Narration Prompts — All Lessons
## *Magister Marcus: The New World Made Ready*
**Unit 1 | Both Forms | All 4 Lessons**

---

## How to Use This Document

Narration is the primary student assessment in this curriculum. It replaces comprehension worksheets.

**Form IA (Grades 3–4) — Oral Narration:**
After a read-aloud or story lesson, ask one prompt. Student speaks; parent listens. Do not interrupt or correct mid-narration. When finished, ask: "Is there anything you want to add?" For written narration, Form IA writes 1–3 complete sentences.

**Form IIA (Grades 5–6) — Written Narration:**
Student writes independently after hearing the prompt. Target: 5–8 complete sentences forming a coherent paragraph. Narration is not a summary list — it should read like a well-told story or a thoughtful explanation.

**Narration is not tested.** It is practice for thinking and speaking clearly. The goal is to tell the story back in the student's own words — not to recite the teacher's words.

## LESSON 1 — The Lands, Waters, and Peoples of America

### Oral Narration Prompts (Form IA)

1. "Tell me about the geography of North America. Describe the land — what does it look like from the Atlantic coast to the Mississippi River?"
2. "Tell me about one Native people we studied. Where did they live, and how did they live?"
3. "Describe a day in the life of a Powhatan villager. What would you see, hear, and do?"
4. "What are some ways that Native peoples and European explorers were different from each other? What did each group think when they first met?"
5. "Tell me about the Iroquois Confederacy. Who were they, and what was unusual about how they governed themselves?"

### Written Narration Prompts (Form IIA)

1. "Describe the geography of North America as a newcomer sailing up the Atlantic coast for the first time would have seen it. Move from the coast inland. What major features would shape where people settled and how they lived?"
2. "Choose one Native civilization from the pre-contact period. Describe their way of life, their territory, and what made them distinctive. What did they have that Europeans did not? What did Europeans have that they did not?"
3. "What did the arrival of Europeans mean for Native peoples? Think about disease, trade, alliances, and conflict. Was the outcome the same for every Native group?"
4. "The Iroquois Confederacy governed five (later six) nations through a council that required consensus. What does this system suggest about how Native peoples thought about government? How might Franklin have been influenced by it?"

## LESSON 2 — Exploration and Settlement, 1492–1630

### Oral Narration Prompts (Form IA)

1. "Tell me the story of Columbus's first voyage. Start with leaving Spain and end with sighting land. What happened when he arrived?"
2. "What was the Columbian Exchange? Name two things that went from the Americas to Europe, and two things that went from Europe to the Americas."
3. "Tell me the story of the Starving Time at Jamestown. What happened? Why did it happen? How did the colony survive?"
4. "Tell me the story of the Mayflower voyage. Who was on the ship, where were they going, and what happened when they arrived?"
5. "What was the Mayflower Compact? Why did the Pilgrims write it? What did it say?"
6. "Tell me about the First Thanksgiving. Who was there? What happened? How do you picture it?"

### Written Narration Prompts (Form IIA)

1. "Tell the story of Christopher Columbus's first voyage from beginning to end. Include his crew's fear, the long weeks at sea, the sighting of land, and his first encounters with the Taíno. What did Columbus think he had found?"
2. "What was the 'Starving Time' at Jamestown? Why did it happen, and what saved the colony? How did tobacco change Jamestown's future — for better and for worse?"
3. "Why did the Pilgrims write the Mayflower Compact? What does the document say, and why does it matter in American history? Connect it to the idea of self-government."
4. "Describe the First Thanksgiving using Winslow's account as your starting point. What does his brief description tell us about the Pilgrims' relationship with the Wampanoag? What does it leave out?"
5. "Compare Jamestown and Plymouth: why were they founded, who came, what challenges did they face, and what did each settlement contribute to the American story?"

## LESSON 3 — The Colonies in Profile, 1630–1732

### Oral Narration Prompts (Form IA)

1. "Tell me the founding story of one colony. Who founded it? Why? What was life like there?"
2. "Why did Roger Williams get in trouble in Massachusetts? What did he do about it?"
3. "Who was William Penn? What was Pennsylvania? What made it unusual?"
4. "What was indentured servitude? What was chattel slavery? How were they different?"
5. "What was the Middle Passage? Tell me what you know about what that journey was like."
6. "Who were the Quakers? What did they believe? Where did they find safety?"

### Written Narration Prompts (Form IIA)

1. "Compare the founding of at least three colonies — choose from Massachusetts Bay, Rhode Island, Pennsylvania, Maryland, and Georgia. What was each founder trying to build? What did they succeed in? What did they fail in?"
2. "What did the English approach to colonization produce that was unusual in the world? Think about land ownership, class, religious variety, and self-government. Why did it matter that England colonized through private ventures and joint-stock companies rather than through royal officials?"
3. "Describe the Triangle Trade and explain how it worked. What did chattel slavery do to the colonies? How did it begin, how did it grow, and who first resisted it?"
4. "What kind of character did the colonial period produce? Use the term 'individualism' and give at least three specific examples from colonial life to support your answer."
5. "Roger Williams believed civil government had no authority over a person's conscience. William Penn believed a community of different faiths could live in peace. How did these ideas compare to the Massachusetts Bay approach? What do you think? Which approach was right?"

## LESSON 4 — Major Events in the Colonies, 1607–1763

### Oral Narration Prompts (Form IA)

1. "What is the Magna Carta? Why does it matter that a king once had to sign a document limiting his own power?"
2. "What was salutary neglect? Why was England's inattention actually good for the colonists?"
3. "Tell me about the Great Awakening. What was it? What did it do for the colonies?"
4. "Tell me the story of young George Washington in the French and Indian War. What happened at Fort Duquesne?"
5. "What was the Albany Congress? What did Benjamin Franklin propose there?"
6. "What did the Treaty of Paris say? What happened to France's land in North America?"

### Written Narration Prompts (Form IIA)

1. "Trace the tradition of limited government from the Magna Carta (1215) through the English Bill of Rights (1689). How did each document or event reduce the king's power? Why did colonists care about this tradition?"
2. "What is 'salutary neglect,' and what did it produce in the colonies? How did 150 years of self-government change what colonists expected from their relationship with England?"
3. "Tell the story of the Great Awakening. Who were the key figures? What did people experience? Why did it matter politically, not just religiously? How did it give colonists something in common for the first time?"
4. "Tell the story of George Washington in the French and Indian War from beginning to end. Include Jumonville Glen, Braddock's defeat, and Washington's survival. What kind of man did these experiences begin to form?"
5. "How did the French and Indian War set the stage for the American Revolution? Consider: who fought it, who won, what it cost, and what Parliament decided to do afterward."
6. "Benjamin Franklin is described as 'distinctly American.' What does that mean? Use specific details from his biography to argue that Franklin could only have come from America — not from England, France, or any other country."
