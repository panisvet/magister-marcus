# U1 Lesson 4 — Major Events in the Colonies
## *Magister Marcus: The New World Made Ready*
**Unit 1 | Weeks 7–8 | 9–10 class days**

## Lesson Overview

**Period:** 1607–1763
**Big Idea:** By 1763, the colonists had fought wars, wrestled with big ideas, experienced religious revival, and built habits of self-government — all without realizing they were becoming a new kind of people.

**Key to the Lesson:** The major events of the colonial period — English Civil War, Glorious Revolution, King Philip's War, the Great Awakening, the French and Indian War — each pushed the colonists toward a greater sense of shared identity and greater independence from England. They did not know it yet, but they were becoming Americans.

**Persons:** Jonathan Edwards, George Washington, Benjamin Franklin, George III
**Geography:** Appalachian Mountains, Allegheny Mountains, Ohio River Valley, Great Lakes, Canada, Quebec, Montreal, Fort Duquesne (Pittsburgh)
**Terms:** King Philip's War, English Civil War, Bacon's Rebellion, Glorious Revolution, English Bill of Rights, "salutary neglect," representation, self-government, The Great Awakening, French and Indian War, Iroquois Confederacy, Battle of Jumonville Glen, Albany Congress, Fort Duquesne, Treaty of Paris
**Civics connections ⚖️:** Magna Carta, English Bill of Rights, representation, self-government, U.S. Civics Q.85, Q.86

## Day-by-Day Lesson Plan

### DAY 1 — English Political Heritage: Magna Carta to Glorious Revolution

**Focus:** The tradition of limited government that colonists inherited from England ⚖️

**Teacher Narration:**
Begin with a short question: "Has England always had a king who could do anything he wanted?" The answer is no — and the colonists knew it.

Tell the story of English political tradition in brief:
- **Magna Carta (1215):** King John was forced by his nobles to sign a document limiting his power. For the first time in England, a king was bound by law. Ordinary people couldn't vote, but the idea that *even a king must follow rules* was planted.
- **English Civil War (1640s):** Parliament fought King Charles I over taxation and power; Charles was ultimately executed. The colonists watched. What they learned: a king can be held accountable.
- **Glorious Revolution (1688):** King James II was removed by Parliament without bloodshed; William and Mary took the throne and signed the **English Bill of Rights** — guaranteeing Parliament's authority, free elections, and freedom from arbitrary imprisonment. These were rights the colonists considered their inheritance as Englishmen.

**Key idea:** When Parliament tried to tax the colonists later, colonists didn't see it as politics — they saw it as a violation of their birthright as Englishmen.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What did the Magna Carta say a king must do? Why did that matter?"
*Form IIA:* "How did the Magna Carta, the English Civil War, and the Glorious Revolution each chip away at the idea that a king could do whatever he wanted? What did colonists learn from watching these events?"

### DAY 2 — Self-Government in the Colonies ⚖️

**Focus:** Colonial assemblies; salutary neglect; representation

**Teacher Narration:**
The Virginia House of Burgesses was established in 1619 — the same year the first Africans arrived at Jamestown. Two stories happening at once: the first representative government and the first enslaved people in English America.

**Key Idea — "Salutary Neglect":** For most of the 1600s and early 1700s, England was too busy with its own troubles to pay close attention to the colonies. The result: colonists governed themselves. They collected taxes, made laws, ran courts, and managed their own affairs through elected assemblies. By 1763, they had been doing this for over a hundred years. They weren't used to being told what to do by Parliament — because Parliament hadn't been telling them what to do.

**Key Idea — Representation ⚖️:** In each colony, the assembly represented the people — men who owned property could vote for their representatives. This was rare in the world. The assembly could refuse to fund a governor's salary, refuse to pass laws, and generally make life difficult for royal authority. Colonists were proud of this.

**Story to Tell:** Describe a session of the Virginia House of Burgesses — men in wigs and breeches debating crop prices and land grants. Point out: these were planters and merchants, not professional politicians. They governed themselves because that was simply what Englishmen did.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What was salutary neglect? Why was England's inattention actually good for the colonists?"
*Form IIA:* "What is representation? How did the colonial assemblies give colonists experience with self-government? Why would colonists resist when England tried to take that away?"

### DAY 3 — The Great Awakening and King Philip's War

**Focus:** Shared colonial experience; religious revival; Jonathan Edwards

**Teacher Narration:**

**King Philip's War (1675–1676):**
Brief but important: Metacom (called "King Philip" by colonists) led a broad coalition of Native tribes against New England settlements. It was the bloodiest war, per capita, in American history. Nearly half the towns in New England were attacked; thousands on both sides died. The colonists won, but barely. The lesson: they had to defend themselves — and they could. Metacom was killed; the Wampanoag and other tribes were devastated.

**The Great Awakening (1730s–1740s):**
Jonathan Edwards and George Whitefield traveled from colony to colony preaching about personal faith, conversion, and the need for each person to stand before God on their own — no priest required, no church hierarchy required. Thousands gathered in fields to hear them preach.

Why it mattered for American history: For the first time, colonists from Massachusetts to Georgia experienced something *together* — the same preachers, the same ideas, the same emotional revivals. The Great Awakening gave them a shared culture that crossed colony lines. It also planted the seed that every person, not just the powerful, stood on equal footing before God — a radical idea with political consequences.

**Story for the Heart — Jonathan Edwards:**
Read or paraphrase the famous opening image from *Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God* (1741): a spider dangling on a thread over a fire. Edwards wanted his congregation to feel their fragility before God. The sermon terrified, but it also called people to take their souls seriously. Whatever students think of Edwards, his preaching shook the colonies awake.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What was the Great Awakening? Who were some of its leaders?"
*Form IIA:* "How did the Great Awakening help colonists feel like they had more in common than they thought? What was the connection between the idea that every person stands equally before God and the idea of equality in government?"

### DAY 4 — Biography: Young George Washington

**Focus:** Washington's early life; Rules of Civility; Virginia militia

**Teacher Narration:**
George Washington was born in 1732 in Virginia. His father died when George was 11. He never went to college. As a young man he worked as a surveyor — mapping the wild lands of the Virginia frontier. He learned to read the land and endure hardship.

At 14, he copied out by hand all 110 of George Washington's "Rules of Civility & Decent Behavior in Company and Conversation" — a 16th-century French guide to honorable conduct. These rules shaped him: treat others with respect, control your emotions, be punctual, don't speak ill of absent people.

**Read:** *Meet George Washington* (Heilbroner), chapters on his boyhood.

**Student Copywork:**
See U1_CopyworkDictation.md, Passages 4–5 (Washington's Rules).
*Form IA:* Copy two rules by hand.
*Form IIA:* Copy two rules, then write one sentence explaining what each rule means in your own words.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What are two of Washington's Rules of Civility? How do they describe a good person?"
*Form IIA:* "What kind of man did Washington become? What experiences shaped his character before he was famous?"

### DAY 5 — Map Day: The French and Indian War

**Focus:** Geography of the conflict; rival claims in North America

**Teacher Narration:**
By 1750, three empires claimed North America: Spain in the south and west, France in Canada and the Mississippi River valley, England along the Atlantic coast. The Ohio River Valley — the land between the Appalachians and the Mississippi — was the flashpoint.

Show students a map of French and British territorial claims. France had built a chain of forts from Canada down to the Gulf of Mexico, encircling the English colonies. The British colonists were boxed in.

**Student Map Activity:**
Label French and British territorial claims on an outline map of North America. Mark: Ohio River Valley, Great Lakes, Fort Duquesne (Pittsburgh), Quebec, Montreal, Appalachian Mountains.

*Form IA:* Oral narration after map: "Who was fighting, and why?"
*Form IIA:* Write 3–4 sentences explaining why France and Britain went to war over North America.

### DAY 6 — Story Day: The French and Indian War

**Focus:** Young Washington at Fort Duquesne; Albany Congress; Franklin's Plan of Union

**Key Stories for the Heart:**

**Washington at Fort Duquesne (1755):**
George Washington was only 23 when he led Virginia militiamen against a French scouting party at Jumonville Glen — and accidentally started a world war. Later, he rode with General Braddock's British army toward Fort Duquesne. Braddock marched his troops in neat European formations through the Pennsylvania forest; the French and their Native allies attacked from cover. The British army was routed. Braddock was killed. Washington had two horses shot from under him; four bullets passed through his coat. He survived without a wound and organized the retreat. He was 23 years old.

Washington wrote to his brother: "I heard the bullets whistle, and, believe me, there is something charming in the sound." Later, when he read this, he remarked he had been young and foolish. He learned: European tactics don't work in American forests, and providential survival is not something to take lightly.

**Albany Congress and Franklin's Plan of Union (1754):**
As war loomed, representatives of seven colonies met in Albany, New York. Benjamin Franklin proposed a "Plan of Union" — a single colonial government to coordinate defense. The colonies rejected it (too much power to the center). Parliament rejected it (too much power to the colonies). But the idea was planted: the colonies might govern themselves together.

**Teacher note:** The French and Indian War ended with British victory and the **Treaty of Paris (1763)**. France lost nearly all its North American territory. Britain now controlled the continent east of the Mississippi. But the war left Britain deeply in debt — and Parliament decided the colonists should help pay for the war that had defended them. That decision set the stage for Unit 2.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What happened to George Washington at Fort Duquesne? What did he learn?"
*Form IIA:* "Why was the French and Indian War important for understanding the American Revolution? Think about debt, colonial cooperation, and what the colonists learned about themselves."

### DAY 7 — Primary Source: Franklin's Autobiography

**Focus:** Benjamin Franklin as a distinctly American story

**Read Aloud:** Excerpt from Franklin's *Autobiography*, Part I (Project Gutenberg — free). Choose a passage describing his early life, his self-education, or his arrival in Philadelphia as a young man with nothing but a few coins.

**Teacher Narration:**
Franklin was born in Boston in 1706, the 15th of 17 children. His father couldn't afford to keep him in school past age 10. He taught himself by reading everything he could find, writing letters to newspapers under a fake name, and arguing with anyone who would argue with him. By 40 he had retired from business, wealthy enough to devote his life to science and public affairs.

Franklin represents something distinctly American: a man who rose entirely by his own intelligence and hard work, without family connections, titles, or inherited wealth. He is not the exception — he is the American story.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What was Benjamin Franklin famous for? Name two things."
*Form IIA:* "What does Franklin's life tell us about what made America different from England? How does the idea of 'salutary neglect' connect to stories like Franklin's?"

**Civics connection ⚖️:** U.S. Civics Q.85 — Franklin is famous for discovering electricity, being a diplomat, serving as postmaster, writing *Poor Richard's Almanack*, and many other things.

## Lesson 4 Answer Key Notes
*(Full answers in U1_AnswerKey.md)*

**Jonathan Edwards:** Puritan minister and theologian; central preacher of the Great Awakening; *Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God* (1741) is the famous sermon; Yale president; argued for the sovereignty of God and the need for individual conversion

**George Washington:** Virginia-born planter and surveyor; led Virginia militia in French and Indian War; survived Fort Duquesne ambush; rose to command the Continental Army in Unit 2; first president

**Benjamin Franklin:** Born Boston 1706; self-educated printer, writer, scientist, diplomat; proposed Albany Plan of Union; served as colonial representative in London; represented U.S. in France during Revolution; Constitutional Convention delegate

**George III:** King of England 1760–1820; his decision to tax the colonies after the French and Indian War set the course toward revolution

**Salutary neglect:** England's practical policy of loose oversight of the colonies; gave colonists room to develop self-governing habits; ended when Parliament tried to raise colonial revenue post-1763

**Great Awakening:** Religious revival movement of 1730s–1740s; preachers like Edwards and Whitefield gave colonists a shared experience crossing colonial lines; reinforced the idea of individual spiritual equality

**French and Indian War:** 1754–1763; Britain vs. France (and Native allies) for control of North America; Britain won; Treaty of Paris transferred French territory to Britain; war debt led to colonial taxation and revolution

**Albany Congress:** 1754 meeting of seven colonies; Franklin's Plan of Union proposed and rejected; first attempt at colonial unity

**Iroquois Confederacy:** League of six Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) nations; sided largely with British in French and Indian War; Franklin studied their confederate structure when drafting his Plan of Union

**Treaty of Paris (1763):** Ended the French and Indian War; France ceded Canada and territory east of the Mississippi River to Britain; Spain received Louisiana Territory
