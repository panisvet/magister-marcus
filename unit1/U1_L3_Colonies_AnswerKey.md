## Persons

**Lord Baltimore** (George Calvert)
Maryland's Catholic founder; granted a royal charter in 1632; died before settlement began; his son Cecil carried the work forward. Maryland's Act Concerning Religion (1649) was one of the first laws granting religious toleration in the English colonies.

**Roger Williams**
Puritan minister who arrived in Massachusetts Bay in 1631 and immediately challenged the colony's authority to compel religious conformity. Banished in 1636, he walked into the wilderness in winter and founded Providence, Rhode Island. Williams was the first American leader to argue consistently that civil government has no authority over matters of conscience and that land must be purchased — not seized — from Native peoples.

**Peter Stuyvesant**
Last Dutch governor of New Amsterdam (later New York). Known for authoritarian governance; refused to surrender gracefully to the English in 1664. New York's diversity (Dutch, English, Jewish, African) was already established before the English takeover.

**William Penn**
English Quaker nobleman; received Pennsylvania as a royal land grant in 1681. Founded Philadelphia ("City of Brotherly Love") and governed through elected assemblies. Made fair treaties with the Lenape people. Pennsylvania attracted settlers of many faiths and nationalities, becoming the most religiously diverse colony.

**James Oglethorpe**
English philanthropist and military leader; founded Georgia in 1733 as a haven for the poor and for debtors who would otherwise go to prison. Originally prohibited slavery and large landholdings; these restrictions were later removed as Georgia's plantation economy grew. Defended the colony against Spanish attack from Florida.

## Terms

**Religious freedom / toleration** — The principle that government cannot compel a person to hold particular religious beliefs or attend a particular church. Rare in the 17th-century world; practiced imperfectly but increasingly in several colonies.

**Township** — A unit of local self-government used especially in New England; landowners governed local affairs together, set up schools, and maintained roads. An early form of grassroots democracy.

**Quakers** — Members of the Religious Society of Friends; believed in direct personal experience of God without clergy or sacraments; opposed war and slavery; faced persecution in England and some colonies; found refuge in Pennsylvania and Rhode Island.

**Self-government ⚖️** — The principle that people have the right to govern their own community's internal affairs through elected representatives. Practiced in colonial assemblies; foundational to American democracy.

**Colonial assemblies ⚖️** — Elected legislative bodies in each colony; made laws, controlled taxation, and managed local affairs. The Virginia House of Burgesses (1619) was the first. Their existence meant colonists had decades of experience governing themselves before independence.

**Colonial governors** — Appointed officials (usually by the king or proprietor) who represented royal or proprietorial authority in each colony. Often in tension with elected assemblies.

**Militia ⚖️** — A citizens' defense force, not a standing professional army; men of a community who trained together and could be called to defend it. Colonial militias were a critical institution for self-defense in the absence of a strong central government.

**Triangle Trade** — The three-legged trade pattern connecting England, West Africa, and the American colonies: manufactured goods went to Africa; enslaved Africans went to the Americas; raw materials (tobacco, sugar, rice) went to England.

**Indentured servitude** — A labor arrangement in which a person contracted to work for a master for a fixed term (usually 4–7 years) in exchange for passage to America and shelter. The servant was bound but not owned; at contract's end, they were free.

**Chattel slavery** — The permanent, hereditary ownership of a human being as property. Unlike indentured servitude, enslavement was for life and passed to children. Grew in law in Virginia from 1662 onward.

**Slave ships** — Vessels used in the Atlantic slave trade; enslaved Africans were packed in horrific conditions for the 6–8 week voyage; death rates were very high.

**Middle Passage** — The ocean voyage from West Africa to the Americas; the "middle" leg of the Triangle Trade. The most lethal part of the slave trade.

**Individualism** — The American character trait of self-reliance, personal initiative, and independence from centralized authority. Hillsdale's Key to the Lesson 15: emerged from the demands of frontier life combined with English traditions of individual rights.

**Short Answer Key:**
1. European settlers saw the New World as a place of opportunity because land was available, legal class distinctions from the Old World did not carry over, and individuals could own property, practice their religion, and better their station in life.
2. It was important for colonial children to read and think because self-government requires educated citizens who can evaluate laws, read contracts, and make decisions. Literacy was rare among common people in Europe; in the colonies it was widespread.
3. In the colonies, almost any free man who owned property could vote — a remarkable contrast to Europe, where voting rights were limited to the nobility and wealthy landowners.
4. Slavery existed throughout human history. The transatlantic slave trade grew especially from the sugar cane plantations of the Caribbean in the early 1500s. Africans were first enslaved by Portuguese traders, then sold by rival African tribes, then transported to the Americas.
5. Slavery expanded in the American colonies slowly: the first Africans arrived at Jamestown in 1619 as something between servants and slaves; in 1662 Virginia decreed that children of enslaved mothers were enslaved for life; laws permitting corporal punishment of enslaved people followed. The practice grew as the demand for plantation labor increased.
