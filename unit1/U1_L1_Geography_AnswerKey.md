# Unit 1, Lesson 1 — The Lands, Waters, and Peoples of America
## Teacher Answer Key — Narration Prompts

**Teacher use only. Do not distribute to students.**

This key lists the content checkpoints for each narration prompt in the Lesson 1 plan. A strong narration need not hit every point — use this list to listen for understanding, not to grade like a test. If a student misses 2–3 key points consistently across multiple narrations, return to those sections in the reading rather than drilling with questions.

---

## Oral Narration Prompts

---

### Prompt: "Tell me about America's landscape — start at the Atlantic and head west. What would you see? What changes?"
*(Class 1 closing prompt — both forms)*

**Strong narration includes:**
- Starts at the Atlantic coast (ocean, coastline, harbors/bays)
- Names the Appalachian Mountains as the first major barrier heading west
- Notes a flat or open middle region (Great Plains)
- Names the Rocky Mountains as a second, larger range in the west
- Reaches the Pacific Ocean
- Notes at least one water feature (Mississippi River, Great Lakes, Gulf of Mexico, Chesapeake Bay)
- Conveys that the land changes dramatically from east to west

**Acceptable for Form IA:** Names 3+ features in rough west-to-east order; conveys that the land is varied.

**Expected for Form IIA:** Names 5+ specific features; describes differences between regions (flat vs. mountainous, wet vs. dry, etc.); may mention specific advantages of the land.

---

### Prompt: "Tell me about the first peoples of America — where they came from and what kinds of civilizations they built."
*(Classes 3–4 prompt — both forms)*

**Strong narration includes:**
- First peoples came from Asia (northeast Asia / Siberia)
- They crossed a land bridge across the Bering Strait (glaciers lowered sea levels, exposing land)
- Over thousands of years they spread across North and South America
- Names at least two of the four civilizations: Mayas, Aztecs, Incas, Hopewell
- Gives at least one specific detail per civilization named:
  - Mayas: stone cities, astronomy, Central America
  - Aztecs: Tenochtitlán, large empire, Mexico
  - Incas: Andes mountains, roads, South America
  - Hopewell: burial mounds, trade networks, eastern North America
- Conveys that native peoples were diverse — not all one group

**Acceptable for Form IA:** Land bridge + one civilization named with one detail.

**Expected for Form IIA:** Land bridge explained with mechanism (glaciers/sea level); two or more civilizations named with specific details; diversity of native peoples noted.

## Written Narration Prompts — Form IA

---

### Prompt: "Describe two different landforms in America and what each one looks like."

**Strong narration includes:**
- Two distinct landforms named (e.g., Appalachian Mountains and Great Plains; Rocky Mountains and Mississippi River; Grand Canyon and Great Lakes)
- A descriptive detail for each — not just the name
- Complete sentences

**Watch for:** Students who name two things but give no description ("The Rocky Mountains and the Mississippi River"). Prompt: "Can you tell me what the Rocky Mountains look like? What would you see there?"

---

### Prompt: "Write 2–3 sentences about the Mayas, Aztecs, or Hopewell — who they were and where they lived."

**Strong narration includes:**
- Correct geographic location (Central America, Mexico, or eastern North America)
- At least one specific detail about how they lived or what they built
- Complete sentences that go beyond "they were a Native American tribe"

**Sample strong Form IA response (Aztecs):**
*"The Aztecs lived in Mexico and built a huge city called Tenochtitlán. They were a powerful empire. They lived before Columbus came to America."*

---

### Prompt: "Why is America a good land for people to live? Write two reasons."

**Strong narration includes:**
- Two distinct reasons drawn from lesson content:
  - Good soil / long growing seasons
  - Abundant fresh water / navigable rivers
  - Vast forests for building and fuel
  - Mild climate in most regions
  - Natural barriers (oceans, mountains) providing security from foreign powers
- Complete sentences

**Watch for:** Vague answers ("because it has lots of land"). Push for specific reasons tied to what they can grow, build, or use.

---

## Written Narration Prompts — Form IIA

---

### Prompt: "Write a paragraph describing the American landscape from east to west. Use at least four specific geographic features."

**Strong narration includes:**
- Organized east-to-west structure
- At least four named geographic features with brief description of each:
  - Atlantic Ocean / coastline / Chesapeake Bay
  - Appalachian Mountains
  - Mississippi River / Great Lakes / Gulf of Mexico
  - Great Plains
  - Rocky Mountains / Grand Canyon / Mojave Desert / Death Valley
  - Pacific Ocean
- Conveys the sense of variety and scale — America is a vast and diverse land
- 5–8 complete sentences in logical sequence

**Watch for:** Students who list features without describing them or connecting them. A strong paragraph moves through the landscape, not just names it.

---

### Prompt: "Write a paragraph explaining how the first peoples came to North America, how they spread across the continent, and what kinds of civilizations they built."

**Strong narration includes:**
- Origin: northeast Asia
- Mechanism: land bridge across Bering Strait; glaciers lowered sea levels
- Migration: spread south and east over thousands of years
- At least two civilizations named with specific details (see oral prompt key above)
- Sense of diversity — not all native peoples were the same
- 5–8 complete sentences

---

### Prompt: "Write a paragraph about why America's geography was an advantage for people who came to live here. Include at least three specific advantages."

**Strong narration includes:**
Three or more of the following specific advantages from lesson content:
- Rich, untouched soils suitable for cultivation
- Temperature and rainfall averages ideal for farming
- Timber plentiful for fuel, building, heating
- Native plants and animals suitable for food
- Waterways plentiful and mostly navigable (rivers as highways)
- Mild winters, long growing seasons in most regions
- Two oceans providing natural security from foreign powers

**Watch for:** Students who give general statements ("the land was good"). A strong IIA response names specific advantages and explains why each one mattered to settlers or civilizations.

## Map Assessment Checkpoints

When students label maps from memory (Classes 6–7), a complete map includes:

**Physical map — minimum acceptable (Form IA):**
- Atlantic Ocean, Pacific Ocean
- Appalachian Mountains, Rocky Mountains
- Mississippi River, Great Lakes (labeled as a group)
- Gulf of Mexico

**Physical map — full credit (Form IIA):**
All of the above plus: San Salvador, Niagara Falls, Chesapeake Bay, Lake Ontario/Erie/Huron/Michigan/Superior (individual labels), Great Plains, Grand Canyon, Death Valley, Mojave Desert, Bering Strait, Hawaiian Islands

**Political map — both forms:**
All 13 original colonies labeled correctly; Washington, D.C. marked; capital cities of at least 5 colonies labeled
