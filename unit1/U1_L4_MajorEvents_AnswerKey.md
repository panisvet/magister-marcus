## Persons

**Jonathan Edwards (1703–1758)**
Congregationalist minister; Yale president; leader of the Great Awakening in New England. His 1741 sermon *Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God* is the most famous sermon in American history. Edwards argued that each person must experience genuine conversion — not merely churchgoing. His emphasis on individual spiritual standing contributed to a culture that valued the individual's direct relationship with God and truth.

**George Washington (1732–1799)**
Born in Westmoreland County, Virginia; father died when George was 11; largely self-educated; worked as a surveyor on Virginia's frontier. In 1754, as a 22-year-old Virginia militia officer, he fired the first shots of the French and Indian War at Jumonville Glen. In 1755 he rode with General Braddock's ill-fated march on Fort Duquesne; Braddock was killed and Washington led the retreat despite four bullet holes in his coat. He became a hero in Virginia. He would later command the Continental Army and become the first president.

**Benjamin Franklin (1706–1790)**
Born in Boston, the 15th of 17 children; apprenticed to his brother's print shop; ran away to Philadelphia at 17; became wealthy as a printer and writer (*Poor Richard's Almanack*); retired from business at 42 to pursue science (electricity experiments; invented the lightning rod, bifocals, the Franklin stove); proposed the Albany Plan of Union in 1754; served as colonial agent in London; represented the United States in France during the Revolution; oldest delegate at the Constitutional Convention in 1787. U.S. Civics Q.85.

**George III (1738–1820)**
King of Great Britain 1760–1820; his decision to tax the colonies to pay for the French and Indian War set the course toward the American Revolution. He was not a tyrant by the standards of his time, but his inflexibility and his ministers' policies destroyed the relationship between Britain and the colonies.

## Terms

**King Philip's War (1675–1676)** — War between New England colonists and a broad coalition of Native tribes led by Metacom (King Philip), the Wampanoag sachem. One of the bloodiest per-capita wars in American history; destroyed dozens of colonial towns; ultimately ended with Metacom's death and the virtual elimination of coastal Native tribes in New England.

**English Civil War (1640s)** — Conflict between Parliament and King Charles I over taxation and royal authority; Charles I was executed in 1649; England was briefly a republic under Oliver Cromwell. Colonists followed these events closely; they confirmed that kings could be held accountable.

**Bacon's Rebellion (1676)** — Armed uprising in Virginia led by Nathaniel Bacon against Governor Berkeley; settlers demanded more aggressive action against Native tribes on the frontier; ended with Jamestown burned and Bacon's sudden death. Showed tensions between frontier settlers and the colonial establishment.

**Glorious Revolution (1688)** — Parliament removed King James II without violence and installed William and Mary; they signed the English Bill of Rights, limiting royal power and guaranteeing Parliament's authority. Colonists considered these rights their inheritance as Englishmen.

**English Bill of Rights (1689)** — Document signed by William and Mary; guaranteed free elections, parliamentary control of taxation, and freedom from arbitrary arrest. Directly influenced the U.S. Bill of Rights a century later.

**"Salutary neglect"** — England's practical policy of loose oversight of the colonies during most of the 1600s and early 1700s. The colonies developed self-governing habits precisely because no one in London was paying close attention. When Parliament tried to tighten control after 1763, colonists resisted — they had governed themselves for over a century.

**Representation ⚖️** — The principle that those who are taxed must have a voice in the body that taxes them. "No taxation without representation" — the rallying cry of the Revolution — was simply an application of this principle that colonists had practiced in their assemblies for generations.

**The Great Awakening (1730s–1740s)** — Religious revival movement that swept the colonies; preachers Jonathan Edwards and George Whitefield drew thousands with emotional preaching emphasizing personal conversion and direct experience of God. It gave colonists a shared emotional and cultural experience crossing colonial lines for the first time; reinforced the idea of every person's direct individual standing before God.

**French and Indian War (1754–1763)** — North American theater of the Seven Years' War; Britain vs. France for control of North America; Native tribes fought on both sides. Britain won decisively; the Treaty of Paris (1763) transferred all French territory east of the Mississippi to Britain. War debt led directly to colonial taxation and then revolution.

**Iroquois Confederacy** — League of six Haudenosaunee nations (Mohawk, Onondaga, Cayuga, Oneida, Seneca, Tuscarora); sided largely with Britain in the French and Indian War; Franklin studied their federal structure when drafting the Albany Plan of Union.

**Battle of Jumonville Glen (1754)** — Washington's Virginia militia ambushed a French scouting party; began the French and Indian War.

**Albany Congress (1754)** — Meeting of seven colonies in Albany, New York; Franklin proposed a "Plan of Union" for colonial self-governance and common defense; rejected by the colonies (feared it) and by Parliament (feared it); planted the idea that the colonies could act together.

**Fort Duquesne** — French fort at the forks of the Ohio River (present-day Pittsburgh); site of Braddock's defeat (1755); captured by British and renamed Fort Pitt in 1758.

**Treaty of Paris (1763)** — Ended the French and Indian War; France ceded Canada and all territory east of the Mississippi to Britain; Spain received Louisiana Territory; Britain now controlled the continent east of the Mississippi but was deeply in debt.

## Stories for the Heart — Answer Guides

**Washington at Fort Duquesne:**
Full story: Washington led Virginia militia; rode with Braddock; Braddock was ambushed and killed; Washington had two horses shot from under him and four bullet holes in his coat; he organized the retreat; he was 23. Lesson: providential survival; frontier warfare is different from European tactics; character is formed by trial.

**Benjamin Franklin's biography:**
Franklin born in Boston, 1706; 15th of 17 children; self-educated; printer by trade; retired wealthy at 42; scientist (electricity, lightning rod); diplomat; proposed Albany Plan of Union; wrote *Poor Richard's Almanack*; oldest delegate at Constitutional Convention. Lesson: distinctly American — rose by merit, not birth.
