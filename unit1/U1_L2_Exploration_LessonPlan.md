# Unit 1, Lesson 2 — Exploration and Settlement
## Teacher Lesson Plan

**Hillsdale content source:** Unit 1, Lesson 2 (3–5 PDF), pp. 32–35  
**Time period:** 1492–1630  
**Time:** 9–10 classes, 30–40 minutes each  
**Forms:** IA (grades 3–4) and IIA (grades 5–6)

---

## Lesson Objective

Students learn about the European exploration of North America and the first English settlement efforts at Roanoke, Jamestown, Plymouth, and Massachusetts Bay.

## Primary Source

**The Mayflower Compact (1620)**

**The sourcing habit:** Before reading any primary source aloud, ask three questions orally: *Who wrote this? When? Why?* (Here: 41 Pilgrim men, November 1620, because they had landed outside their patent and needed to agree on how to govern themselves.) Make this a standing habit for every primary source in the curriculum — it takes one minute and teaches students that a source is something a real person made for a reason.

Both forms will read and discuss the Mayflower Compact. Form IA hears it read aloud and narrates its meaning. Form IIA reads it independently (or with the teacher) and writes a narration about its significance.

Key passage to discuss:
> *"We...do by these Presents, solemnly and mutually, in the Presence of God and one another, covenant and combine ourselves together into a civil Body Politick..."*

The Mayflower Compact is the first example of self-government in American history — a group of ordinary people agreeing together to govern themselves by just laws for the common good. This is a seed of what will become the American republic.

**To Know by Heart:**
> *"We must consider that we shall be as a city upon a hill. The eyes of all people are upon us."* — John Winthrop

Both forms should memorize this quote by the end of the lesson sequence.

## Lesson Sequence (9–10 Classes)

### Classes 1–2 — Before Columbus / Columbus's Voyages

**Student prep before Class 1:** Look at images of Spanish or Portuguese caravels. Ask students to describe the challenges of crossing the Atlantic on such a ship.

**Class 1 — Why did Europeans explore? (30–40 min)**

Begin by helping students gain historical perspective. Before the age of exploration, most people never traveled more than a few miles from where they were born. There was no internet, no printing press for most people, no fast travel. Ask students to think about what was different about their own lives vs. life in 1490.

Introduce the reasons Europeans began exploring: trade desire (especially routes to Asia), competition between emerging kingdoms, new maritime technology (the caravel, improved navigation instruments), and growing curiosity in European culture after the Renaissance.

Review briefly: Leif Erikson reached North America around 1000 AD — nearly 500 years before Columbus. Why didn't that change the world? (Answer: it was isolated; no lasting connection was made; Europe didn't know.)

**Class 2 — Columbus: Four Voyages (30–40 min)**

Tell the story of Columbus: his theory that the world was smaller than others believed (not that it was flat — correct this misconception explicitly), his petition to Portugal and then Spain, the sponsorship of Ferdinand and Isabella.

Cover all four voyages, noting the arc of decline: the first voyage was triumphant; by the fourth, Columbus was shipwrecked on Jamaica, aging and ignored. He died without understanding that he had discovered an entire half of the world. He never found a passage to Asia.

Discuss the Columbian Exchange: plants, animals, people, and diseases moved in both directions between the hemispheres. The most devastating transfer was disease — especially smallpox, which killed millions of Native Americans who had no immunity. This must be taught honestly and with appropriate weight.

> **Oral Narration Prompt:**
> "Tell me the story of Columbus — who he was, what he believed about the world, what he found, and what happened to him in the end."

### Classes 3–4 — Jamestown: Founding and Crisis

**Living book assignment:**
- Teacher reads aloud from Guerber, *Story of the Thirteen Colonies*, pp. 11–25 (Jamestown section)
- Use images of the Jamestown fort and illustrated map

**Class 3 — Jamestown Founded (30–40 min)**

In 1607, the Virginia Company sent 104 settlers to found Jamestown — named for King James I — in what is now Virginia. They came primarily for profit: the Virginia Company expected gold. They found swamp, disease, and conflict with the powerful Powhatan Confederacy.

Tell the stories:
- John Smith's leadership: "He who shall not work shall not eat"
- The relationship with Pocahontas — complicated, important, not a fairy tale
- Lord De La Warr's arrival (1610) rescuing the collapsing colony

**Class 4 — The Starving Time and Tobacco (30–40 min)**

The winter of 1609–1610: most of the 500 settlers died. They ate horses, dogs, rats, leather. This is called the "Starving Time." This is history that should not be softened — the story of Jamestown is a story of extraordinary suffering and near-failure.

What saved Virginia:
- John Rolfe's cultivation of tobacco (a cash crop Europe wanted)
- The House of Burgesses, 1619: Virginia settlers chose representatives to make laws — the first elected assembly in the Americas
- The arrival of women and families, making Jamestown a permanent home, not just an outpost

> **Oral Narration Prompt:**
> "Tell me the story of the 'Starving Time' at Jamestown. What happened? What saved the colony?"

> **Written Narration — Form IA (2–3 sentences):**
> "Write about what made Jamestown finally succeed after years of failure."

> **Written Narration — Form IIA (paragraph):**
> "Write a paragraph about the founding of Jamestown — who funded it, who settled it, what they suffered, and what finally made it succeed. Include the House of Burgesses."

### Classes 5–6 — The Pilgrims and the *Mayflower*

**Living book:** Begin reading *If You Sailed on the Mayflower* — Ann McGovern. Read 2–3 chapters per class, alternating reading aloud with student reading (Form IIA reads independently).

**Student prep before Class 5:** Look at "The First Thanksgiving 1621" painting by Jean Leon Gerome Ferris. Discuss: who is in the painting? What is happening? What do you notice about the relationship between the Pilgrims and the Native Americans in this scene?

Then ask the sourcing questions: *Who made this? When? Why?* Ferris painted it in 1912 — almost 300 years after the event. Discuss: is this painting evidence of what happened in 1621, or evidence of what Americans in 1912 imagined about it? (It is a secondary source. Compare it with the true primary sources: the accounts of Edward Winslow and William Bradford, who were there. Winslow says the Wampanoag outnumbered the Pilgrims nearly two to one — does the painting show that?)

**Class 5 — The Voyage (30–40 min)**

The Pilgrims were Separatists — English Protestants who believed the Church of England was beyond reforming and wanted to separate from it entirely. After years in Holland, they arranged a crossing on the *Mayflower* in September 1620. 102 passengers, a 90-foot ship, 66 days at sea.

Tell the story of the voyage: storms, cramped conditions, the ship nearly turning back when a main beam cracked, the improvised repair. One person died at sea; one baby was born.

On November 11, they anchored off what is now Provincetown, Massachusetts. They had overshot their patent — they were outside the Virginia Company's territory. This created a crisis: did they have any legal right to govern themselves here?

**Class 6 — Plymouth Founded (30–40 min)**

The Mayflower Compact: before going ashore, 41 men signed an agreement to govern themselves justly for the common good. Read it aloud together. Discuss: this is a community agreeing to make its own laws. This is self-government.

Tell of the first winter: half the Pilgrim settlers died of cold and disease. Those who survived did not leave. In the spring, Squanto — a Patuxent man who had been enslaved, taken to England, and returned — came to their camp and spoke to them in English. He taught them to plant corn. Massasoit, the Wampanoag chief, made a treaty of mutual defense with the Pilgrims that held for fifty years.

The First Thanksgiving: in the fall of 1621, the Pilgrims held a harvest festival. Massasoit and ninety of his people came and stayed for three days. They brought five deer. The Pilgrims had survived.

> **Oral Narration Prompt:**
> "Tell me the story of the Mayflower voyage and the first winter at Plymouth. What did the Pilgrims hope for? What did they find?"

**Copywork — Mayflower Compact:**

> **Form IA copywork:** Copy this sentence:  
> *"We covenant and combine ourselves together into a civil Body Politick, for our better Ordering and Preservation."*

> **Form IIA dictation:** Prepare to take dictation from the opening of the Mayflower Compact (teacher selects 2–3 sentences).

### Classes 7–8 — Massachusetts Bay and Winthrop's Vision

**Class 7 — The Puritans (30–40 min)**

In 1630, ten years after the Pilgrims landed, a much larger group of Puritans — not Separatists, but reformers within the Church of England — sailed for Massachusetts Bay. John Winthrop led them. They came in a fleet of eleven ships, 700 settlers.

On the ship *Arbella*, before landing, Winthrop preached a sermon. He said: "We shall be as a city upon a hill. The eyes of all people are upon us." He meant: we are building something the world is watching. We cannot fail each other or God.

Discuss: What did Winthrop mean by "city upon a hill"? (Answer: A visible example — if they succeeded in building a just, godly community, the world would see it. If they failed, that too would be seen.) Why might this idea still matter? (It became a recurring theme in how Americans have understood their nation's purpose.)

**Class 8 — Review and Narration Day (30–40 min)**

Use this class to consolidate the lesson through oral narration, map review, and notebooking.

**Review map:** Mark the key settlements: San Salvador, St. Augustine, Roanoke, Jamestown, Plymouth, Massachusetts Bay (Boston). Label Columbus's route.

> **Oral Narration Prompt:**
> "Tell me about one of these: Roanoke, Jamestown, Plymouth, or Massachusetts Bay — what was it, who settled it, and why does it matter?"

## Questions for the American Mind

(Hillsdale formative discussion questions for this lesson)

1. Why did Europeans begin exploring the ocean in the 1400s?
2. What was Columbus's theory about the fastest route to Asia?
3. Why was ocean exploration so dangerous?
4. How did Columbus's voyages change the course of world history?
5. Was Columbus successful? Why or why not?
6. How did England settle the New World? How was it different from other countries (Spain, France)?
7. Why did settlers want to establish Jamestown? What problems did they face?

10. Why did the Pilgrims want to establish Plymouth?

12. How did the First Thanksgiving come about?

## What to Look For in Narrations

A strong narration from this lesson will include:
- At least 3 of the 10 key persons named with accurate detail
- At least one of the four settlements (Roanoke, Jamestown, Plymouth, Massachusetts Bay) narrated with specific events
- The Mayflower Compact identified as an act of self-government
- Winthrop's "city upon a hill" quote recognized or paraphrased

A narration that blurs the four settlements together or confuses the Pilgrims with the Puritans needs more read-aloud time with the living book, not more questioning.
