# Schola Cantorum Domestica
## A Two-Year Sacred Music and Solfège Course for the Christian Home

---

### Course Identity

**Spine:** Ladukhin, Nikolay — *Elementary Course in Solfège* (c. 1896, P. Jurgenson). Public domain. Free PDF via IMSLP.
**Supplementary:** *Collection of Monophonic Solfeggi* and *60 Solfeggi for 2 Voices* (Ladukhin, IMSLP); *Liber Usualis* (Internet Archive); Byzantine chant recordings.
**Duration:** Two school years, 2 sessions/week, ~25–30 minutes per session (~144 sessions total).
**Students:** Ages 8 and 11 (Grammar stage), pitch-matched, no prior formal training.
**Teacher:** Parent with cantor/choir director background and Ladukhin training. Fixed-do natural.
**System:** Fixed-do (Do = C, always) in Year 1. Moveable-do introduced developmentally in Year 2. See *Solfège Philosophy* below.

---

### Solfège Philosophy: Fixed-Do as Foundation, Moveable-Do as Destination

This course uses a two-stage approach to solfège that reflects how a skilled church singer actually functions — and how this course's teacher actually sings.

**Year 1: Fixed-Do**

Do = C, always. The piano keyboard is the anchor. Every solfège syllable names a specific, findable pitch. This concreteness serves children at the Grammar stage: they can see Do on the keyboard, hear it, match it, and build a library of intervals from a stable foundation. Ladukhin's exercises are designed for this system. The teacher is fluent in it. There is no translation cost.

Throughout Year 1, however, the ear is being trained in something deeper than pitch labels. Every time the teacher asks *"where does this settle?"* — every listening game, every drone exercise, every major/minor identification — the children are developing the moveable-Do instinct before it has a name. They are learning to hear *home*, wherever home happens to be.

**Year 2: The Bridge**

Year 2 opens with one explicit session that names what the children already do. The teacher demonstrates her own practice:

*"When I need to sing something in F major, I find Do on the piano — that's C — and I count up to Fa. Then I hold Fa in my ear, and I let it become my new Do. I have to feel it, and hear it in my mind's ear, before I can sing it out loud. Once Fa becomes home, I can read the whole melody from there."*

This is not a new skill. It is a name for the pitch-location operation the children have watched their teacher perform every session. Fixed-do got her to the right pitch. The moveable-Do shift let her read the melody once she arrived.

After the bridge lesson, Year 2 runs dual-track:
- **Ladukhin exercises** continue in fixed-do (the printed syllables are fixed-do; no translation needed).
- **Chant analysis and the Eight Tones** use moveable-do, following the AOCS form analysis vocabulary. Do means *tonic* — wherever the tone centers. The solfège tables in this project (C Major, G Major, F Major, D Major, A minor, E minor, D minor, G minor) become usable reference materials once the children hold both frames.

**What this produces**

A singer who can do what their teacher does: use fixed-do as a pitch-location system and moveable-do as a melodic reading system, simultaneously and without friction. This is how Orthodox church singers have always functioned — the Byzantine *ni-pa-vou-ga-di-ke-zo* system is itself a moveable system, and the great Russian choral tradition (from which Ladukhin comes) trained singers to navigate both. The children are not learning two competing systems. They are learning one skill in two stages.

**Note on the AOCS materials**

The *Basic Sight-Singing and Ear Training for Church Singers* course (OCA) uses moveable-do throughout and explicitly recommends it for Orthodox choral music. Its pitch supplements, solfège tables, and form analysis exercises are included in this project. In Year 1, these are teacher reference only — the syllable labels are moveable-do and will confuse a fixed-do student. In Year 2, after the bridge lesson, they become active instructional materials. The teacher must translate pitch names to fixed-do absolute pitches when presenting any AOCS exercise to the children (e.g., in G major, AOCS Do = G; in fixed-do, that is Sol).

### Philosophical Orientation

This course is built on the conviction that music is not a subject to be studied but a gift to be received and offered back. The child's voice is the first and finest instrument; it was given before any lesson began. Our task is to train it in truth, beauty, and service.

**Charlotte Mason principles applied:**
- Short, attentive lessons (15–25 minutes of focused work per session).
- Sound before sight: pitch is internalized before notation is read.
- Atmospheric learning: the mother singing fixed-do casually around the house is pre-education. Begin this before Lesson 1.
- Narration adapted for music: the child sings back, identifies intervals, finds Do on a page, copies a line of notation in their music journal.
- Habit training named explicitly: attention (no wandering during exercises), truthfulness (sing what you actually hear, not what you think you should hear), wonder (sacred music is not homework).

**Classical Grammar stage:**
- Age 8: pure imitation and pattern-absorption. Echo-singing is primary. Wonder before analysis.
- Age 11: pattern + emerging logic. Begins asking *why*. Ready for interval names, key structure, simple theory. Sacred music history as story.

**Orthodox Christian formation:**
- The voice in service of worship, not performance.
- The living tradition: children are not learning an ancient artifact — they are joining a choir that has never stopped singing.
- St. Romanos the Melodist as patron of this course (feast: October 1). His story belongs in Lesson 1.
- Liturgical calendar awareness: the eight Byzantine tones rotate every eight weeks. Year 2 modal introduction syncs with the tone of the week.
- Scope: Orthodox Byzantine chant, Gregorian chant, Latin hymns. The breadth is intentional — the Church's song is larger than any one tradition, and all of it belongs to these children.

### Two-Year Arc

**Year 1 — Foundation: The Voice Finds Its Home**
Diatonic solfège in C major and closely related keys. Staff notation introduced. Sacred music listening (Byzantine chant, simple Gregorian). Music journaling begins. First simple sacred melody prepared a cappella.

**Year 2 — Deepening: The Voice Enters the Tradition**
Chromatic and modal solfège. Moveable-do introduced as a second frame alongside fixed-do. Introduction to the eight church tones (Byzantine octoechos) using AOCS form analysis vocabulary (macrostructure, microstructure, intonation/reciting tone/cadence). Gregorian chant notation introduced. Two-part singing with teacher. Latin hymns. Cumulative synthesis: children prepare and sing a cappella at one or two liturgical services.

---

### Cumulative Synthesis Goal

By the end of Year 2, the children will prepare a specific piece for a specific occasion:

**Suggested synthesis piece:** The troparion *"When Thou wast baptized in the Jordan"* (Theophany, Tone 1) — or a simple *Lord have mercy* in a tone the children know from their home parish. The piece should already live in their liturgical memory; the course teaches them to *own* it musically.

**Occasion:** Theophany (January 6, Year 2) or another feast day chosen by the family. Count backward from the feast to plan preparation.

This is not a performance. It is an act of worship. That distinction should be named clearly to the children before they begin preparation.

---

### What This Course Does Not Do

This course does not teach piano, music composition, or Byzantine neume notation in depth. It does not replace choir participation — it prepares children to participate more fully. It is not a self-teaching course; it requires a teacher who can demonstrate and respond to what the children actually sing.

---

### A Note for Published Use

This course was designed by and for an Orthodox cantor/choir director teaching her own children. The teacher-facing notes assume Ladukhin familiarity. A published version for general audiences would need a supplementary *Teacher Orientation Guide* covering: how to use the Ladukhin PDF, what fixed-do means and why it matters, and a decision tree for the two-child teaching dynamic.
