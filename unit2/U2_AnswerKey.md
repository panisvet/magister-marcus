# QUIZ 1 — Lesson 1: Self-Government or Tyranny

## Form IA Answer Key

**MATCHING:**
- militia → D (an armed body of citizens prepared for military service)
- massacre → C (a violent, unjust killing of innocent people)
- petition → A (a formal written request signed by several people)
- Minutemen → E (colonists ready to fight at a minute's notice)
- Congress → B (a group that made laws for the colonies)

**NARRATION — Accept any response that demonstrates these key facts:**

*Boston Tea Party:* Colonists were angry about the Tea Act (taxed tea, gave monopoly to British East India Company). Samuel Adams and the Sons of Liberty, dressed as Mohawk Indians, dumped 342 chests of tea into Boston Harbor on December 16, 1773. Britain responded with the Intolerable Acts.

*Paul Revere's Ride:* British General Gage sent 700 soldiers to Concord to seize colonial weapons. Paul Revere and William Dawes rode through the night on April 18–19, 1775 to warn the countryside. Two lanterns hung in the Old North Church signaled the British were coming by sea.

**QUESTIONS FOR THE MIND:**
1. Colonial weapons and military supplies stored in Concord.
2. The colonists believed it was wrong to be taxed by Parliament because they had no elected representatives in Parliament — they had no vote on the laws being passed about them.
3. Minutemen were colonial militia who pledged to be ready to fight at a minute's notice — very short warning.

## Form IIA Answer Key

**MATCHING:**
- militia → D
- massacre → C
- petition → A
- Minutemen → E
- Continental Army → F
- Sons of Liberty → G
- salutary neglect → B

**SHORT ANSWER — Model answers:**

1. British soldiers were seeking colonial weapons stored in Concord. At Lexington, 77 Minutemen faced a column of 700 Redcoats. Someone fired. Eight colonists died. The British marched on to Concord, but the weapons had been mostly moved.

2. "No taxation without representation" meant Parliament could not justly tax the colonists because the colonists had no elected members in Parliament. These taxes were especially offensive because the colonists had been largely self-governing for 150 years, and Britain was now interfering in that tradition without their consent.

3. Adams believed that even hated men deserved a fair trial and competent legal defense — that is what the rule of law means. He knew his defense would make him deeply unpopular. He took the case anyway. This reveals extraordinary moral courage and commitment to principle over popularity.

4. Ralph Waldo Emerson called it the "shot heard 'round the world" because the Battle of Lexington and Concord in April 1775 began the first successful democratic revolution in modern history, one whose ideas would inspire people around the world.

5. Patrick Henry was saying that the old colonial identities no longer mattered — Virginians, New Yorkers, New Englanders were all Americans now, united by a common cause. This was the birth of an American national identity.

# QUIZ 2 — Lesson 2: The Declaration of Independence

## Form IA Answer Key

**MATCHING:**
- natural rights → C
- self-evident → B
- unalienable → A
- treason → E
- consent of the governed → D

**NARRATION — Accept any response demonstrating these key facts:**

*The Declaration's writing and signing:* Thomas Jefferson wrote the Declaration in a rented room in Philadelphia; John Adams nominated him. The Second Continental Congress voted for independence July 2; the Declaration was adopted July 4, 1776. The men who signed it risked execution for treason.

*Natural rights:* The Declaration says all people have rights — life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness — simply because they are human beings, given by God/Creator. Governments exist to protect these rights. If government fails, the people may alter or abolish it.

**QUESTIONS FOR THE MIND:**
1. Thomas Jefferson wrote it. John Adams nominated Jefferson, saying Jefferson was the better writer and the more appropriate choice (Virginia, the largest colony, needed to be represented).
2. July 4, 1776: the Second Continental Congress formally adopted the Declaration of Independence. The thirteen colonies announced themselves free and independent states.
3. Accept honest responses. The expected answer: Yes, it is a genuine contradiction. Jefferson wrote "all men are created equal" while enslaving over 100 people. However, Lincoln, Douglass, and King cited the Declaration's true words as a standard America must live up to. The contradiction does not erase what is true in the Declaration — it shows what work remained to be done.

## Form IIA Answer Key

**MATCHING:**
- natural rights → C
- self-evident → B
- unalienable → A
- treason → E
- consent of the governed → D
- equality → F
- pursuit of happiness → G

**SHORT ANSWER — Model answers:**

1. Thomas Jefferson wrote it. Adams nominated Jefferson because Jefferson was a better prose stylist, Virginia (the largest colony) needed to be the visible author, and Adams recognized that Jefferson would give the document more legitimacy with Southern delegates.

2. Jefferson owned more than 100 enslaved people while writing "all men are created equal." His original draft condemned the slave trade; Congress removed the passage to keep Southern delegates. The contradiction is genuine and has driven American history ever since. However, Lincoln, Douglass, and King showed that the Declaration's true words — rightly understood — provided the standard against which slavery could be condemned and equality demanded. True things outlast the hypocrisy of those who state them imperfectly.

3. According to the Declaration, people create governments to secure their natural rights — life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness. Governments derive their just powers from the consent of the governed. If government becomes destructive of these ends, the people have the right to alter or abolish it.

4. The signers risked execution for treason. Several lost their fortunes during the war. Some died. All pledged "our lives, our fortunes, and our sacred honor." They signed because they believed the cause was just — that self-government was worth risking everything for.

5. America was founded on ideas — the equal natural rights of all people — not on a royal dynasty, ethnic identity, or conquered territory. This means the Declaration can still be cited in argument today. Any person, anywhere, can appeal to its principles. That is why it remains a living document.

# QUIZ 3 — Lessons 3–4: War of Independence & Constitution

## Form IA Answer Key

**MULTIPLE CHOICE:**
1. B
2. C
3. C (Delaware River)
4. B (France)
5. C (James Madison)

**NARRATION:**

*Washington's Crossing:* On Christmas night 1776, Washington led his exhausted army across the ice-choked Delaware River in a desperate surprise attack. They surprised the Hessian garrison at Trenton the next morning and won a complete victory. It revived American morale at the war's darkest moment.

*The Constitution:* The Constitution is the supreme law of the United States, written in 1787 in Philadelphia. It creates the structure of the national government (three branches: legislative, executive, judicial) and protects the rights of the people. The Founders wrote it because the Articles of Confederation had left the country too weak.

**QUESTION FOR THE MIND:**
The Bill of Rights protects the rights of the people from government interference. It guarantees freedom of religion, freedom of speech, freedom of the press, the right to bear arms, the right to a fair trial, and others. (Accept any one freedom correctly identified.)

---

## Form IIA Answer Key

**MULTIPLE CHOICE:**
1. B
2. A
3. B (Battle of Saratoga convinced France to ally with America)
4. C (Washington, who voluntarily gave up power)
5. B (a plan by some officers to make Washington king or overthrow Congress)

**SHORT ANSWER — Model answers:**

1. Soldiers in the War of Independence typically fought in lines, firing volleys on command. Americans also used guerrilla warfare — hiding behind walls, trees, and rocks to fire on British soldiers who were trained for open-field European warfare. This asymmetry allowed smaller, less-equipped American forces to inflict heavy casualties.

2. On Christmas night, 1776, Washington led his troops across the icy Delaware River in the dark. They marched through snow and surprise-attacked the Hessian garrison at Trenton at dawn. The victory was complete, and it transformed American morale at the lowest point of the war. Without it, the Continental Army might have collapsed.

3. Valley Forge (winter 1777–78) was a catastrophe: soldiers lacked shoes, food, and warm clothing; thousands died of cold and disease. Washington visited the sick, prayed, encouraged the men personally. Baron von Steuben arrived and drilled the army into a professional fighting force. The army that emerged in spring was transformed.

4. The Founders divided power among three branches because they believed no person should hold unchecked authority — power corrupts. If one branch abuses its power, the other two can check it. James Madison said "If men were angels, no government would be necessary." Since they are not angels, government must be designed to manage the worst in human nature while still permitting self-government.

5. The Bill of Rights — the first ten amendments — was added because Anti-Federalists feared the new Constitution gave too much power to the national government. It explicitly guarantees freedom of religion, speech, press, assembly, the right to bear arms, protection from unreasonable search and seizure, and the right to a fair trial. It protects the people from government overreach.

**PART 4 — STORIES FOR THE HEART:**

*Boston Massacre:* March 5, 1770 — tension between colonists and British soldiers in Boston erupted into violence. A crowd jeered soldiers; someone threw something; soldiers fired. Five colonists died, including Crispus Attucks, a freed slave. John Adams then agreed to defend the soldiers at trial — believing even hated men deserved a fair hearing under the law.

*Boston Tea Party:* December 16, 1773 — Samuel Adams and the Sons of Liberty, disguised as Mohawk Indians, dumped 342 chests of British East India Company tea into Boston Harbor to protest the Tea Act. It was a political act, not a mob — disciplined, organized, targeted. Britain responded with the Intolerable Acts, which united the colonies.

*Paul Revere's Ride:* April 18–19, 1775 — two lanterns hung in the Old North Church steeple: the British were coming by sea. Paul Revere and William Dawes rode through the night to warn the countryside. The Minutemen assembled. At Lexington, eight colonists died. At Concord, the British were turned back. The war had begun.

**PART 5 — QUESTIONS:**
11. The colonists believed Parliament had no right to tax people who had no representation in Parliament; they had been self-governing for over 150 years and viewed the new taxes as a violation of their rights as Englishmen.
12. The Intolerable Acts were five Parliamentary acts passed in 1774 in response to the Boston Tea Party. They closed Boston Harbor (economic strangulation), stripped Massachusetts of self-government, and required colonists to house British soldiers. The colonists called them "intolerable" because they violated the basic principles of self-government and rule of law.
13. *Common Sense* by Thomas Paine argued that monarchy was absurd, that Britain had exploited rather than protected the colonies, and that independence was not just possible but necessary. It sold 100,000 copies in three months and shifted public opinion decisively toward independence.
14. Natural rights are freedoms that belong to all people simply because they are human beings — not because governments grant them. According to Jefferson, they come from the Creator (God). Because they come from God/nature, no government can rightfully take them away.

**PART 4 — SHORT ANSWER:**

6. Salutary neglect was Britain's practical policy of loosely overseeing the colonies, allowing them to develop habits of self-government through elected assemblies, local courts, and town meetings. It ended in 1763 after the French and Indian War, when Britain began taxing the colonies to pay war debts. The sudden interference in colonists' lives — after 150 years of relative freedom — felt like a violation of their rights as Englishmen.

7. Moderate Patriots used petitions, boycotts, and appeals to Parliament; radical Patriots used confrontation, propaganda, and eventually armed resistance. Both were responses to the same grievances. Debate over which was wiser: accept either position with supporting reasons.

8. Jefferson wrote "all men are created equal" while owning more than 100 enslaved people. This is a genuine, not a technical, contradiction. Lincoln cited the Declaration at Gettysburg; Frederick Douglass cited it to demand America live up to its promise; Martin Luther King Jr. called it "a promissory note." The contradiction has driven American history ever since and shows that the founding left unfinished work.

9. Governments are created to secure natural rights — life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness. Their just power comes from the consent of the governed. If a government becomes destructive of these ends, the people have the right to alter or abolish it and institute a new government.

At dawn they attacked the Hessian garrison at Trenton and won completely. It was the turning point of the early war, rescuing American morale at its lowest point.

*Valley Forge:* Winter 1777–78 — thousands of soldiers lacked shoes, food, and shelter; hundreds died of cold and disease. Washington visited the sick and prayed. Baron von Steuben drilled the army into a professional force. The army that emerged in spring was transformed.

*Franklin's Rising Sun:* At the close of the Constitutional Convention, Benjamin Franklin pointed to the sun carved on Washington's chair and said he had wondered during the debates whether it was a rising or setting sun — and now he knew it was a rising sun. Franklin also told a woman outside the hall who asked what they had created: "A republic, if you can keep it." Both stories capture the hope and fragility of the founding moment.

**PART 5 — SHORT ANSWER:**

7. Washington's strategy was to keep the army alive and fighting rather than to seek a decisive pitched battle he might lose. Each engagement proved Americans could hold the field. This gradually built confidence in the Continental Army, proved to France that the Americans were worth supporting, and exhausted British willingness to continue the war.

8. Baron von Steuben (a Prussian officer) arrived at Valley Forge in February 1778 and drilled the Continental Army in professional tactics — how to march, wheel, load, and fire in formation. He transformed an undisciplined militia into a professional fighting force. The army that won Yorktown was the army Steuben trained.

9. The Founders believed that human nature was not angelic — people in positions of power tend to abuse it. To prevent tyranny, they divided power among three branches (separation of powers), each of which can check the others. They also divided power between the national government and the states (federalism). As Madison wrote in *Federalist* No. 51: "If men were angels, no government would be necessary." The design of the Constitution reflects deep realism about human nature.
