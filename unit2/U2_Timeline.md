## The Timeline: 1763–1789

```
1763 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
     Proclamation of 1763
     (Britain tells colonists: no settlement west of the Appalachians)

1765 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
     Stamp Act
     (Britain taxes the colonies; colonists cry "no taxation without
     representation!")

1770 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
     Boston Massacre
     (British soldiers shoot five colonists in Boston; Crispus
     Attucks is the first to fall)

1773 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
     Boston Tea Party
     (Sons of Liberty dump 342 chests of British tea into
     Boston Harbor)

1774 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
     Intolerable Acts / First Continental Congress
     (Britain punishes Boston; colonists unite in response)

April 19, 1775 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
     Battles of Lexington and Concord
     ("The shot heard 'round the world" — the war begins)

1775–1783 ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
     War of Independence

July 4, 1776 ───────────────────────────────────────────────────
     Declaration of Independence signed
     (Thomas Jefferson's words: "all men are created equal")

December 25–26, 1776 ───────────────────────────────────────────
     Crossing the Delaware / Battle of Trenton
     (Washington leads a desperate night crossing; a great American
     victory turns the tide)

Winter 1777–1778 ───────────────────────────────────────────────
     Valley Forge
     (The Continental Army suffers through the winter; Washington
     holds them together)

1777 ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
     Battle of Saratoga
     (American victory brings France into the war as an ally)

1781 ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
     Battle of Yorktown
     (Cornwallis surrenders; the war is effectively over)

1783 ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
     Treaty of Paris
     (Britain officially recognizes American independence)

September 17, 1787 ─────────────────────────────────────────────
     Constitutional Convention concludes — Constitution Day
     ("A republic, if you can keep it." — Benjamin Franklin)

1788 ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
     Constitution ratified
     (Nine states approve the new plan of government)

1789 ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
     George Washington inaugurated as first President
     New government begins

1791 ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
     Bill of Rights ratified
     (First ten amendments to the Constitution)
```

## Words to Know

**unalienable rights** — rights that cannot be taken away; given by God, not by government

**self-government** — the right of a people to make their own laws

**representation** — having a voice in the laws that govern you

**tyranny** — when a ruler or government uses power unjustly and without the consent of the people

**militia** — a group of ordinary citizens trained to fight as soldiers

**Minutemen** — colonial militiamen ready to fight at a minute's notice

**Continental Army** — the army created by the Second Continental Congress to fight the British

**Declaration of Independence** — the document signed on July 4, 1776, stating that the American colonies were free from Britain and that all people are created equal with unalienable rights

**Constitution** — the document written in 1787 that established the laws and structure of the American government

**Bill of Rights** — the first ten amendments to the Constitution, protecting the freedoms of American citizens

**checks and balances** — the system by which the three branches of government limit each other's power

**separation of powers** — dividing the government into three parts: legislative (makes laws), executive (carries out laws), judicial (decides what the laws mean)

**republic** — a government in which citizens elect representatives to govern for them
