# *The New World Made Ready* — Unit 2, Lesson 1
## Self-Government or Tyranny
### 1763–1776 | 9–10 Classes

---

## Lesson Objective

Students learn how new British exertions of authority over the colonists — after 150 years of relative freedom — led to resistance, then to open war, then to the Declaration of Independence. The central question is not merely about taxation but about self-government: whether a people must be controlled by the will of others without their free consent.

## Stories for the Heart

The following stories should be told in full, with detail, in the teacher's own voice:

1. **Biographies: Washington, Franklin, Samuel Adams, Patrick Henry, Thomas Paine, and Thomas Jefferson** — who they were and what they stood for *before* independence. Introduce them as real people, not icons.
2. **The Boston Massacre** (March 5, 1770) — the confrontation, the shots, Crispus Attucks. The atmosphere of fear and fury. Then: John Adams agrees to defend the redcoats. Why?
3. **John Adams's defense of the redcoats** — the most morally courageous act in early American politics. Adams believed even hated men deserved a fair trial. He was right, and he knew it would cost him.
4. **The Boston Tea Party** (December 16, 1773) — Samuel Adams, the Sons of Liberty, the disguise, the chests of tea, the harbor stained brown. A political act, not a mob.
5. **Patrick Henry's "Give Me Liberty" speech** — Virginia Convention, March 1775. Read the climax aloud dramatically. Let the students sit with it.
6. **Paul Revere's Ride** — April 18–19, 1775. The signal in the Old North Church, the moonlit ride, the road to Lexington. Longfellow's poem is worth reading aloud in full.
7. **Minutemen at Lexington and Concord** — "Don't fire until you see the whites of their eyes." The ragged militia facing professional soldiers. The shot heard 'round the world. The British retreating to Boston under fire.
8. **Siege of Fort Ticonderoga** — Ethan Allen and the Green Mountain Boys; Henry Knox drags the cannon 300 miles through winter to Boston.
9. **Battle of Bunker Hill** — The Patriots lose the hill but show they can fight. The Redcoats lose 226 killed, over 800 wounded. The cost of taking a hill from armed Americans is established.
10. **Liberation of Boston** — Knox's cannon on Dorchester Heights. The British evacuate. Washington's first great victory.
11. **John Adams nominates Washington** to command the Continental Army. Why Adams? Why Washington?
12. **Letters of John and Abigail Adams** — Abigail's famous "Remember the Ladies" letter and John's reply. The conversation of a marriage under the weight of revolution.

## Questions for the American Mind

Use these for oral discussion on story days. Not every question needs to be asked every day — choose 2–3 per session.

- Why was it good that the colonists had been allowed so much self-government for so long?
- What changed in 1763? Why did Britain start paying attention to the colonies after the French and Indian War?
- What is a tax? What is it for? Why were the colonists upset about *these* taxes specifically?
- What is the difference between a Patriot/Revolutionary and a Tory/Loyalist? Is it easy to know which side is right when you're living through it?
- Why were the two approaches to resistance different? Which do you think was wiser — deliberation or destruction?
- Why was John Adams's defense of the redcoats heroic?
- What were the Intolerable Acts? Why did the colonists call them "intolerable"?
- How did Paul Revere and the Sons of Liberty warn the colonists before Lexington and Concord?
- What was the "shot heard 'round the world"? Why is it called that?
- Even though the Patriots lost the Battle of Bunker Hill, why did it strengthen their will to fight?
- What did the Second Continental Congress decide to do?
- What did Thomas Paine say in *Common Sense*? Did it change people's minds?

## Day-by-Day Lesson Plan

### Class 1 — The World in 1763

**Focus:** Set the stage. Why did 1763 change everything?

**Teacher Narration (15–20 min):**
For over 150 years, the British colonists of North America governed themselves. This was not a gift — it was an accident, mostly. England was too busy with its own troubles (civil wars, Glorious Revolution, wars with France) to pay close attention to the colonies. The colonists had been left alone, and in that space, they had built something: elected assemblies, local courts, town meetings. They were Englishmen who had developed the habit of self-rule.

Then the French and Indian War ended in 1763. Britain won — but the victory cost a fortune. The national debt had doubled. Now there were new western territories to defend against restless Native peoples. Parliament looked at the colonies and thought: *they should help pay for this.* After all, the war was partly fought to protect *them.*

Bring out a map of North America in 1763. Show students what changed: French territory transferred to Britain, the Proclamation Line drawn at the Appalachian Mountains. The King said: colonists may not settle west of this line. This was an attempt to prevent costly conflict with Native peoples. The colonists were furious. They had just helped win this land.

**Discussion:** *"Why might the colonists have felt the Proclamation of 1763 was unfair, even if the King had a practical reason for it?"*

**Copywork/Dictation preview (assign for Day 4):**
- Form IA: "No taxation without representation."
- Form IIA: "Taxation without representation is tyranny." — attributed to James Otis

### Class 2 — Parliament Acts, Colonists React

**Focus:** Stamp Act through Tea Act; two kinds of resistance

**Teacher Narration (20–25 min):**
Parliament began passing tax laws. Walk through them with the students:

- **Stamp Act (1765):** Tax on all printed materials — newspapers, pamphlets, legal documents, playing cards. Every sheet had to carry a stamp. Colonists were furious. The boycotts began. "No taxation without representation!" — the slogan of the resistance.
- **Townshend Acts (1767):** New taxes on imported goods: glass, paper, paint, tea. The boycotts continued.
- **Tea Act (1773):** A trick — Parliament actually *lowered* the price of tea by letting the East India Company sell directly to the colonies, cutting out American merchants. But it still required paying the hated tea tax. The colonists saw through it.

Explain the two types of response: some colonists petitioned, wrote pamphlets, held meetings, organized boycotts — working within the system to change it. Others like the Sons of Liberty took to the streets, intimidated tax collectors, and destroyed property. Samuel Adams walked the line between both worlds.

**Key Idea — Representation:** The colonists' core argument was not simply about money. It was about consent. The English tradition held that a man could not be taxed without the consent of his representatives. But the colonists had no representatives in Parliament. Parliament had no right to tax them at all — or so they argued. "Taxation without representation is tyranny."

**Discussion:** *"What is a boycott? Can refusing to buy something be a powerful act? Why or why not?"*

### Class 3 — The Boston Massacre

**Focus:** Story of the Massacre; John Adams's defense

**Teacher Narration:**
British soldiers had been quartered in Boston since 1768. The townspeople resented them. On the evening of March 5, 1770, a crowd gathered around a small group of redcoats at the Customs House. People threw rocks, ice, and insults. Someone — no one knows who — fired. When the smoke cleared, five colonists lay dead. Crispus Attucks, a freed slave and sailor, was among the first to fall.

Paul Revere published an engraving of the scene — inaccurate in several details, perfectly designed as propaganda. It showed innocent colonists being slaughtered. Outrage swept the colonies.

Then: John Adams — a man who hated the British policies as much as anyone — agreed to defend the soldiers. He believed that every man, even an enemy, deserved a fair trial. "Facts are stubborn things," he told the jury, "and whatever may be our wishes, our inclinations, or the dictates of our passions, they cannot alter the state of facts." Six soldiers were acquitted. Two were convicted of manslaughter.

**Story for the Heart:** Tell this story in full, with detail. The courage it took. The political cost Adams paid. The principle he held.

**Two accounts of one night — a corroboration exercise:** Show Revere's engraving. Ask the sourcing questions: *Who made this? When? Why?* (Revere, weeks after the event, to stir outrage.) Then compare it with what the trial evidence showed. The engraving shows soldiers firing in an ordered line at peaceful citizens on a clear evening; the trial established a chaotic night scene, a threatening crowd, and no order to fire. Introduce the words **bias** and **corroborate**: Revere's engraving is a real primary source, made by a man who was really there in Boston — and it is also biased. Checking one account against another is how historians find out what actually happened. Adams said it in the courtroom: "Facts are stubborn things."

> **Oral Narration Prompt (both forms):**
> "Tell back the story of the Boston Massacre twice: first the way Paul Revere's engraving shows it, then the way the evidence at the trial showed it. Why are the two accounts different?"

### Class 4 — The Boston Tea Party and Intolerable Acts

**Focus:** Tea Party story; Intolerable Acts; First Continental Congress

**Teacher Narration:**
On the night of December 16, 1773, about 116 men — many of them Sons of Liberty, some disguised as Mohawk Indians (a disguise meant to mock, not to deceive) — boarded three ships in Boston Harbor. They worked through the night, breaking open 342 chests of tea and dumping them into the harbor. Not a single other item on the ships was touched. The tea ruined — a political act, carefully controlled.

Parliament's response was swift and severe: the **Intolerable Acts** (1774):
1. Boston Port Act — closed Boston Harbor until the tea was paid for
2. Massachusetts Government Act — stripped Massachusetts of self-government
3. Administration of Justice Act — British officials could be tried in Britain, not the colonies
4. Quartering Act — colonists must house British soldiers
5. Quebec Act — extended Quebec (Catholic) territory, alarming Protestant colonists

These five acts showed the colonists what tyranny looked like in detail.

The **First Continental Congress** met in Philadelphia in September 1774 — twelve colonies sending delegates. They agreed to stop importing British goods and to meet again if the situation did not improve. They were not yet talking about independence. But they were talking — together — for the first time.

### Class 5 — Paul Revere's Ride and Lexington and Concord

**Focus:** The midnight ride; first shots; "the shot heard 'round the world"

**Teacher Narration:**
Read aloud Longfellow's "Paul Revere's Ride" in full. Take your time. Then narrate the actual events:

April 18, 1775: British General Gage sent 700 Redcoats to seize colonial weapons stored at Concord. The Sons of Liberty had spies in Boston. Paul Revere and William Dawes rode out to warn the countryside. Lanterns hung in the Old North Church steeple: *two if by sea* — the British were crossing the Charles River.

April 19, dawn: At Lexington, 77 Minutemen faced the column of 700. Someone fired. Eight colonists died. The British marched on to Concord, found the weapons mostly gone (warned ahead of time), and began to destroy what remained.

The march back to Boston became a rout. Thousands of colonial militiamen lined the roads, firing from behind stone walls and trees. The Redcoats, used to open-field European warfare, were bewildered and bloodied. They reached Boston having lost 250 killed or wounded.

Ralph Waldo Emerson called the first shot at Concord "the shot heard 'round the world." He was right. The war had begun.

### Class 6 — Fort Ticonderoga and Bunker Hill

**Focus:** Stories 8–11; Washington takes command

**Teacher Narration:**
Tell the following stories in sequence:

**Ethan Allen and Fort Ticonderoga (May 10, 1775):** Allen and his Green Mountain Boys surprised the British garrison at dawn. Allen demanded surrender "in the name of the Great Jehovah and the Continental Congress!" The cannon at Ticonderoga were the prize. Henry Knox, a 25-year-old bookseller turned artillery officer, hauled 59 cannon 300 miles through the winter, across frozen Lake George, over the Berkshire Mountains, to Boston.

**Second Continental Congress (May 1775):** The colonies met again. This time the situation was different — a war was already underway. John Adams nominated George Washington of Virginia to command the army. His reasoning: Virginia was the largest colony; Washington had military experience; he looked like a general and acted like one.

**Battle of Bunker Hill (June 17, 1775):** Patriots occupied Breed's Hill near Boston overnight. The British assaulted uphill three times. "Don't fire until you see the whites of their eyes!" The Patriots ran out of ammunition on the third assault and were driven off. The British took the hill — and lost 226 dead, 828 wounded. The lesson was learned: Americans can fight.

**Liberation of Boston (March 1776):** Knox's cannon appeared on Dorchester Heights overnight. The British woke to find them staring down at the harbor. Gage evacuated Boston by sea. Washington's first great victory: not a single shot fired.

### Class 7 — Thomas Paine and Common Sense

**Focus:** The case for independence; why *Common Sense* mattered

**Teacher Narration:**
In January 1776, a newly arrived English immigrant named Thomas Paine published a 47-page pamphlet called *Common Sense*. Within three months, 100,000 copies had been sold — in a country of 2.5 million people. It was the bestselling work in 18th-century America.

Paine made three arguments: (1) Monarchy is absurd. Why should any man's birth determine his right to rule over others? (2) Britain has not "protected" the colonies — it has used them. (3) Independence is not just possible — it is necessary. There is no going back.

Read aloud the key passages from *Common Sense*. Discuss: Had people been talking about independence before Paine? Why did Paine's pamphlet change minds when petitions had not?

**Discussion:** *"Did the colonists have a right to declare independence? Why or why not? What would they need to say to convince the world their cause was just?"*

## Answer Key Notes

*(Full answers in U2_AnswerKey.md)*

**Self-government:** The ability of a people to choose, through elected representatives, the laws that govern them. The colonists had practiced this for 150 years through their colonial assemblies.

**Salutary neglect:** Britain's practical policy of loosely overseeing the colonies for most of the 1600s–early 1700s, allowing them to develop habits of self-governance. It ended in 1763.

**Crispus Attucks:** Freed slave and sailor; among the first killed at the Boston Massacre; considered by many the first American to die in the cause of independence.

**John Adams:** Boston lawyer; defended the redcoats at the Boston Massacre trial; nominated Washington to command the Continental Army; nominated Jefferson to draft the Declaration; later second president.

**"Shot heard 'round the world":** The first shot fired at the Battles of Lexington and Concord, April 19, 1775. Called this by Ralph Waldo Emerson because it began the first successful democratic revolution in modern history, inspiring others worldwide.

**Common Sense:** Pamphlet by Thomas Paine, January 1776; argued that monarchy was absurd, that Britain exploited the colonies, and that independence was both necessary and possible; sold 100,000 copies in three months.

**Intolerable Acts:** Five acts of Parliament (1774) in response to the Boston Tea Party; closed Boston Harbor, stripped Massachusetts of self-government, allowed quartering of soldiers, and more. United the colonies against Britain.

**Second Continental Congress:** Met May 1775; appointed Washington as commander; voted for independence July 2, 1776; signed the Declaration July 4, 1776.
