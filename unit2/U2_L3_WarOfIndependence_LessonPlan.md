# *The New World Made Ready* — Unit 2, Lesson 3
## The War of Independence
### 1776–1783 | 9–10 Classes

---

## Lesson Objective

Students learn about the major figures, common soldiers, strategy, and specific battles of the American War of Independence. They understand the immense risk the Founders and ordinary soldiers took, how the war was nearly lost and then won, and why Washington's character was as decisive as any battle. They encounter women and African Americans whose contributions shaped the war's outcome.

## Stories for the Heart

Tell each of these in full, in your own voice, with detail and drama. These are the stories students will carry forward.

**1. The Fates of the Signers**
Many of the men who signed the Declaration of Independence paid dearly for it. Their homes were burned, their families scattered, their fortunes spent. A few died in British prisons. When they signed, they knew they were committing treason. They signed anyway. Ask students: What does it cost to stand for something true?

**2. Paine Reads *The Crisis* to Washington's Army**
December 1776. The Continental Army is retreating through New Jersey in the cold, exhausted and demoralized. Washington has Thomas Paine's newly written pamphlet read aloud to his men by the campfires: *"These are the times that try men's souls. The summer soldier and the sunshine patriot will, in this crisis, shrink from the service of their country; but he that stands it now, deserves the love and thanks of man and woman."* Three days later, Washington orders the crossing of the Delaware.

**3. Washington's Crossing of the Delaware**
Christmas night, 1776. The Delaware River is choked with ice. The plan: cross by midnight, march nine miles to Trenton, and surprise the Hessian mercenaries before dawn. Everything goes wrong with the timing — but Washington crosses anyway, in the dark and the cold, with men who have no shoes, leaving bloody footprints in the snow. They reach Trenton at dawn. The Hessians, sleeping off their Christmas celebrations, are caught completely off guard. The Americans capture nearly a thousand soldiers without losing a man. The news travels fast: the revolution is not dead.

**4. Nathan Hale**
Before the army's retreat from New York, Washington needed a spy. Captain Nathan Hale volunteered. He was captured by the British, given no trial, and sentenced to hang the next morning. His last words before the noose: *"I only regret that I have but one life to lose for my country."* He was 21 years old.

**5. Valley Forge**
Winter 1777–78. The Continental Army camps at Valley Forge, Pennsylvania. Two thousand soldiers die of cold and disease. Men sleep in mud, lack shoes, lack food, lack blankets. Washington writes to Congress begging for supplies and receives very little. Into this misery arrives Baron Friedrich von Steuben, a Prussian officer who speaks almost no English. He drills Washington's ragged army into something resembling real soldiers — teaching them to load, fire, and march in formation. He curses them in three languages when they fail, and they love him for it. They leave Valley Forge a different army.

**6. Women and the War**
The war did not happen only on battlefields. Tell at least two of these stories:
- **Abigail Adams** wrote to her husband John while he was at Congress, managing the farm alone, raising their children, and sending sharp counsel: *"Remember the ladies."* Her letters are among the finest of the period.
- **Martha Washington** spent each winter encampment with her husband, organizing relief for soldiers, nursing the sick, boosting morale.
- **Margaret Corbin** took over her husband's cannon at Fort Washington after he was killed beside her. She was shot three times and survived.
- **Molly Pitcher** (Mary Ludwig Hays) carried water to soldiers at Monmouth and, when her husband collapsed, took his place at the cannon.

**7. Phillis Wheatley and African Americans in the War**
Phillis Wheatley was born in Africa, enslaved, and brought to Boston as a child. She taught herself to read Latin and Greek and became the first African American published poet. She wrote poetry in support of the American cause and was received by George Washington himself. Meanwhile, approximately five thousand African Americans served in the Continental Army across the war; by some accounts, nearly a quarter of the American forces at the Battle of Yorktown were Black soldiers. By the end of the war, every northern state except New York and New Jersey had explicitly outlawed slavery. The Declaration's principles were already beginning to work.

**8. Battle of Saratoga and the French Alliance**
Fall 1777. British General John Burgoyne marches south from Canada to cut the colonies in two — and meets American farmers with muskets at Freeman's Farm and Bemis Heights near Saratoga, New York. The British are surrounded. Burgoyne surrenders his entire army of 6,000 men. In Paris, Benjamin Franklin has been waiting for exactly this moment. France, which has quietly been supplying the Americans, now enters the war openly as an ally. Spain and the Netherlands follow. Britain is no longer fighting a colonial rebellion — it is fighting a world war.

**9. Benedict Arnold — Betrayal**
Benedict Arnold was one of Washington's best generals — brave, bold, brilliant in battle. He was instrumental at Saratoga. But Arnold felt passed over for promotion, slighted by Congress, and tempted by his Loyalist wife's connections. In 1780 he secretly agreed to hand the fortress at West Point to the British for money and a British officer's commission. The plot was discovered when the British spy John André was captured with papers in his boot. Arnold fled to the British lines. He spent the rest of his life in exile, despised by both sides. Washington, who had trusted him, was devastated. Ask students: What can we learn from Arnold's story? What does bitterness do to a man?

**10. Yorktown and Cornwallis Surrenders**
Fall 1781. Washington and French General Rochambeau march south in secret while the French fleet blocks the sea. British General Cornwallis is trapped at Yorktown, Virginia, on a narrow peninsula. After three weeks of siege, Cornwallis surrenders his army of 8,000 men. When the British band plays at the surrender, they choose a song called *"The World Turned Upside Down."* It has been. The war is effectively over.

**11. Washington Dissolves the Newburgh Conspiracy**
1783. The war is won but peace has not yet been signed. Washington's officers are camped at Newburgh, New York — unpaid, resentful, ready to march on Congress. Anonymous letters circulate calling for the army to seize power. Washington appears before his officers unannounced. He reads a letter appealing to their reason — then stops, squints at the page, and reaches slowly into his pocket for a pair of spectacles. The room goes quiet. "Gentlemen, you will permit me to put on my spectacles, for I have not only grown gray but almost blind in the service of my country." Several officers begin to weep. The conspiracy is over before it begins. This is why Washington is called the American Cincinnatus — after the Roman general who, given supreme power in a crisis, laid it down when the crisis passed.

## Day-by-Day Lesson Plan

### DAY 1 — Context: The Odds Against Them

**Focus:** The state of the Continental Army in 1776; why Britain was favored to win

**Teacher Narration:**
Open with the question: *Who was supposed to win this war?* Walk students through Britain's advantages — the most powerful navy in the world, professional soldiers, money, the Hessian mercenaries. Then walk through what the Americans had: determination, knowledge of the terrain, short supply lines, and a reason to fight. The British were fighting for empire; the Americans were fighting for their homes.

Describe how soldiers fought in this era: Brown Bess muskets, volley fire, formations. Why did armies stand in lines and fire at each other? Because a musket ball fired alone was nearly useless — volleys of hundreds gave you a chance. Guerrilla tactics — firing from behind trees, ambushing supply lines — were considered uncivilized by European standards but were used effectively by American militias. Washington had to balance both: fighting European-style when he needed to look like a real army, guerrilla-style when he needed to survive.

Cover the Battle of New York (summer 1776) — Washington's army nearly destroyed, the retreat through Manhattan and New Jersey. The army is melting away. Enlistments expire. Men go home. Then read aloud the opening of Paine's *The Crisis*.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What advantages did the British have at the start of the war? What advantages did the Americans have?"
*Form IIA:* "Why didn't Washington fight the war the same way the British did? What was his overall strategy for winning?"

---

### DAY 2 — Story Day: Crossing the Delaware and Trenton

**Focus:** The crisis of December 1776; Washington's gamble

**Narration:** Tell Story for the Heart #2 (Paine reads *The Crisis*) and #3 (Crossing the Delaware) in full. Use the painting *Washington Crossing the Delaware* (Emanuel Leutze) — show it, describe it, let students study it. Then describe the battle itself: surprise, victory, the army's lifted spirits.

Include Nathan Hale (Story #4) — brief, but powerful.

**Read Aloud:**
*"These are the times that try men's souls..."* — Thomas Paine, *The Crisis*, No. 1 (first paragraph)

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "Why was the crossing of the Delaware River dangerous? What happened when the Americans got to Trenton?"
*Form IIA:* "Thomas Paine wrote that 'the summer soldier and the sunshine patriot will shrink from the service of their country.' What did he mean? Can you think of a time when that temptation would have been real for someone in Washington's army?"

---

### DAY 3 — Story Day: Valley Forge and Von Steuben

**Focus:** Suffering, endurance, and the transformation of the Continental Army

**Narration:** Tell Story for the Heart #5 (Valley Forge) in full. Describe the conditions specifically — mud, cold, no shoes, men sleeping in huts with no firewood. Then introduce von Steuben: who he was, why he came, what he did. The army that marched out of Valley Forge in spring 1778 could stand and fight.

Include at least one of the women's stories (Story #6) — Martha Washington or Abigail Adams works best here.

**Student Living Book:**
*Meet George Washington*, pp. 55–63 — read together or assign.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What happened to Washington's army at Valley Forge? Who was Baron von Steuben and how did he help?"
*Form IIA:* "Washington's men suffered terribly at Valley Forge. What kept them there? What does that tell us about the difference between an army that fights for pay and one that fights for a cause?"

### DAY 5 — Story Day: Saratoga and the French Alliance

**Focus:** The turning point; how the war became international

**Narration:** Tell Story for the Heart #8 (Saratoga) in full. Describe what Burgoyne expected — a quick march south — and what he met instead: American militia, thick forests, short supplies. Tell the story of the American farmers who came out to fight — not professional soldiers, men with hunting rifles and something to defend. Burgoyne's surrender was the single greatest British defeat since the war began.

Then describe what changed: Franklin in Paris, the French decision, Spain, the Netherlands. Britain now faces enemies on every front.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "Why was the Battle of Saratoga important? Who decided to help America after Saratoga?"
*Form IIA:* "Why did France decide to help the Americans after Saratoga and not before? What were they waiting for? What does this tell us about how nations decide to take sides?"

---

### DAY 6 — Story Day: Betrayal and Yorktown

**Focus:** Benedict Arnold; the trap at Yorktown; the war ends

**Narration:** Tell Story for the Heart #9 (Benedict Arnold) — give Arnold's genuine accomplishments before the betrayal; the story is more powerful if students understand who he was. Then tell #10 (Yorktown) with its satisfying completeness: the trap, the siege, the surrender, the music.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What did Benedict Arnold do that was wrong? What happened at Yorktown?"
*Form IIA:* "Benedict Arnold is one of history's most famous traitors. But before his betrayal, he was one of America's best generals. What does his story tell us about the relationship between bitterness, ambition, and character? What might have happened differently if he had made other choices?"

---

### DAY 7 — Primary Source + Phillis Wheatley

**Focus:** Washington's Resignation; Wheatley; African Americans in the war

**Read Aloud:**
An excerpt from Washington's Resignation Speech (December 23, 1783). Washington appears before Congress, bows, hands back his commission, and rides home to Mount Vernon. He had the power to become a king — and he walked away. Cincinnatus.

Then read aloud a passage from Phillis Wheatley's "Liberty and Peace." Discuss: here is a woman who was enslaved, who taught herself to read and write, who celebrated American liberty — while still living under the contradiction of that liberty. Ask Form IIA students: What does Wheatley's life tell us about the power of ideas, even when they are not yet fully lived out?

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "What did Washington do after the war was over? Why was that remarkable?"
*Form IIA:* "Washington is called the 'American Cincinnatus.' Who was Cincinnatus? What does it mean to be compared to him? Why is it so rare for someone to give up power voluntarily?"

---

### DAY 8 — Story Day: The Newburgh Conspiracy

**Focus:** The final test of Washington's character

**Narration:** Tell Story for the Heart #11 (Newburgh) in full. Set the scene carefully: the officers have every reason to be angry. Congress has failed them. Their pay is months overdue. Washington's response is not a speech about duty — it is a personal moment, a man reaching for his glasses, and then a single sentence that does more than any argument could. This is what character looks like in a crisis.

**Discussion Questions:**
*Form IA:* "Why were Washington's officers angry at the end of the war? What did Washington do?"
*Form IIA:* "Washington didn't argue with his officers — he appealed to something deeper. What was that? Why did reaching for his spectacles matter more than any argument he could have made?"

## Questions for the American Mind

*Use these for oral discussion — whole family together. Both forms hear the same questions; depth of expected response differs.*

- Who was favored to win the War of Independence, and why?

- How did soldiers fight each other? What was a volley? What was guerrilla warfare?
- What happened at the crossing of the Delaware River? Why did it matter so much?
- How did Washington encourage his men at Valley Forge? What kept them there?
- Who was Baron von Steuben and what did he contribute?
- What happened at the Battle of Saratoga? Why did it bring France into the war?
- How did the Americans defeat the British at Yorktown?
- Why did the Americans win the War of Independence? *(Think: leadership, allies, cause, geography, enemy mistakes)*
- Why did Washington's officers want to overthrow the government? How did Washington stop them?
- What does it mean to call someone an "American Cincinnatus"?

## A Note on Providence

This lesson is rich with what the historical sources themselves call providential moments: Washington's survival in battle (he was shot at repeatedly across the war; one account from the French and Indian War found four bullet holes in his coat and two horses shot from under him); the fog at Brooklyn Heights that allowed the army to escape the British; the unlikely French alliance; the weather at Yorktown that prevented British rescue. These themes need not be imposed — let Washington's own writings speak. He attributed his survival and the American victory directly to divine Providence, and said so plainly.
