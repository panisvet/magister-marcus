## How to Use This Document

**Oral narration (Form IA primary; both forms use on narration days):**
The teacher reads the prompt. The student narrates aloud without notes. The teacher listens without interrupting. After the student finishes, the teacher may ask one clarifying question: *"You mentioned X — can you tell me a little more about that?"* Do not correct or add facts during narration. Narration itself is the learning.

**Written narration (Form IIA primary):**
The student writes independently. Form IIA target: 5–8 complete sentences forming a coherent paragraph. Content, not length, is the goal — but students should practice sustained, organized prose.

**Form IA oral narration assessment standard:**
- 1–3 complete sentences
- Accurate recall of the main story or idea
- Student's own words (not the teacher's phrases repeated back)

**Form IIA written narration assessment standard:**
- Full paragraph (5–8 sentences)
- Logical sequence (beginning, middle, point)
- Specific names, events, or details included
- Student's own words throughout

## Lesson 1 — Self-Government or Tyranny (1763–1776)

### Stories for the Heart Narration Prompts

**Form IA — Oral (choose one per narration session):**

1. "Tell me the story of the Boston Massacre. Who was involved, what happened, and why did it matter?"

2. "Tell me the story of the Boston Tea Party. Why did the colonists do it? What happened? What did Britain do next?"

3. "Tell the story of Paul Revere's Ride. Where was he going and why? What happened when the British reached Lexington?"

4. "Who were the Minutemen? Tell me what happened at Lexington and Concord."

5. "What was Common Sense? Who wrote it, and what did it say?"

6. "Who was John Adams? Tell me one story about him that shows what kind of man he was."

7. "Tell me about the Liberation of Boston. How did Henry Knox's cannon help?"

**Form IIA — Written (choose one per narration session):**

1. "Why did the colonists believe Britain was acting as a tyrant after 1763? Explain in a paragraph using at least three specific events or laws as evidence."

2. "Who was John Adams, and what does his decision to defend the British soldiers at the Boston Massacre trial reveal about his character? Why was that decision remarkable?"

3. "What was the central argument Thomas Paine made in *Common Sense*? What made this pamphlet so powerful, and why did it change people's minds when petitions had not?"

4. "Explain the difference between a Patriot and a Loyalist. What did each side believe? Is it easy to know which side is right when you are living through it?"

5. "What were the Battles of Lexington and Concord? What was the 'shot heard 'round the world,' and why is it called that?"

## Lesson 2 — The Declaration of Independence (1776)

### Stories for the Heart Narration Prompts

**Form IA — Oral (choose one):**

1. "Tell me who wrote the Declaration of Independence. Tell me one thing about Thomas Jefferson."

2. "What happened on July 4, 1776? Tell the story of the day the Declaration was adopted."

3. "Tell me the story of the first public reading of the Declaration — what happened, where it was, and how the crowd responded."

4. "Why was signing the Declaration dangerous? What were the men risking by signing it?"

5. "What does the Declaration of Independence say about rights? Tell me in your own words."

**Form IIA — Written (choose one):**

1. "What is the Declaration of Independence about? Explain its argument about natural rights, government, and what happens when government fails. Use specific phrases from the document."

2. "Jefferson wrote 'all men are created equal' while enslaving other human beings. Is that a contradiction? How have later Americans — Lincoln, Frederick Douglass, Martin Luther King Jr. — responded to it?"

3. "Why did John Adams nominate Jefferson to write the Declaration instead of writing it himself? What does this tell you about both men?"

4. "The Declaration says government gets its power from the 'consent of the governed.' What does that mean? Why is it a radical idea?"

5. "What does the final sentence of the Declaration — the pledge of 'Lives, Fortunes, and sacred Honor' — reveal about the men who signed it?"

## Lesson 3 — The War of Independence (1776–1783)

### Stories for the Heart Narration Prompts

**Form IA — Oral (choose one):**

1. "Tell the story of Washington's Crossing of the Delaware. What was happening before it, what happened during it, and why did it matter?"

2. "What was Valley Forge? Tell me what happened there and how the army survived."

3. "Tell the story of the Battle of Saratoga. What happened, and why was it a turning point in the war?"

4. "What was the Newburgh Conspiracy? Tell the story of how Washington handled it."

5. "What did Washington do after the war ended? Tell me the story of his resignation."

6. "Who was Phillis Wheatley? Tell me what makes her story remarkable."

7. "Tell me about Betsy Ross and the first American flag. What do the stars and stripes represent?"

**Form IIA — Written (choose one):**

1. "Why did the Americans win the War of Independence? Give at least three reasons and explain each one in your paragraph."

2. "Tell the story of the Newburgh Conspiracy and Washington's response. What does this reveal about Washington's character, and why does it matter for a republic that the general gave up his power?"

3. "What was the significance of the Battle of Saratoga — not just as a military victory, but as a turning point in the whole war? What did it change?"

4. "What role did African Americans and women play in the War of Independence? Use at least two specific people or examples."

5. "What is the 'American Cincinnatus'? Who was Cincinnatus, and why did people compare Washington to him?"

6. "Describe Valley Forge. What happened there, and what does this episode reveal about the character of the Continental Army and its commander?"

## Lesson 4 — The United States Constitution (1783–1789)

### Stories for the Heart Narration Prompts

**Form IA — Oral (choose one):**

1. "Tell me Benjamin Franklin's story about the rising sun on Washington's chair. What did it mean to him?"

2. "Tell me the story of Franklin leaving Independence Hall and what he said to the woman in the crowd."

3. "What is the Constitution? Tell me in your own words what it does."

4. "What are the three branches of government? Tell me what each one does."

5. "What is the Bill of Rights? Tell me two rights it protects and why they matter."

**Form IIA — Written (choose one):**

1. "Why did the Founders design a government with three separate branches? What problem were they trying to solve, and how did the Constitution address it?"

2. "What is the Bill of Rights, and why was it added to the Constitution? Explain what it protects and why those protections were so important to the Anti-Federalists."

3. "How are the Declaration of Independence and the Constitution connected? What does each document do that the other does not?"

4. "Madison wrote 'If men were angels, no government would be necessary.' Explain what he meant, and explain how the structure of the Constitution reflects this idea."

5. "What does Franklin mean when he says 'A republic, if you can keep it'? What does it take to keep a republic? Use evidence from what you have learned in Unit 2."

## Unit 2 Review Narration — End of Unit

These prompts draw across all four lessons. Use before the final test, or as a cumulative narration assignment.

**Form IA — Oral (choose one):**
- "Tell me the most important story from Unit 2. Why did you choose it?"
- "Tell me about George Washington. What did he do in the war, and what did he do when the war was over?"
- "What is the most important idea you learned in Unit 2? Explain it in your own words."

**Form IIA — Written (choose one):**
- "Unit 2 covers the founding of the United States. In a paragraph, explain: (1) what the Americans were fighting for, (2) how they won, and (3) what kind of government they built. What is the thread that connects all three?"
- "Why does the Declaration of Independence still matter today? How have Americans used it to argue for justice since 1776?"
- "What kind of man was George Washington? Use at least three episodes from Unit 2 to support your answer."
