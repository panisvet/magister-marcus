## Unit 2 Enduring Ideas

Students who complete this unit will carry these ideas forward:

1. The United States was not founded on racial origin or family privilege but on ideas asserted to be true of all people at all times — the equal natural rights of each person.
2. America was founded on the conviction that government should be controlled by the people and limited to securing each person's natural rights and the common good.
3. Ordinary Americans — farmers, merchants, tradespeople — sacrificed security and life to defend the truth that people must govern themselves, not be governed without their consent.
4. The Declaration of Independence did not merely announce independence; it created a nation and stated its founding principles. Americans should measure their public life against it.
5. The Constitution is a carefully wrought document. Its structure — separating powers, creating checks and balances, protecting rights — reflects a sober view of human nature and a commitment to lasting liberty.

## A Note on Providence

This unit gives students rich material for noticing the quieter notes of Providence in the historical record: Washington's repeated near-misses with death, the unlikely victory at Yorktown, Franklin's observation about the rising sun on Washington's chair. These themes arise naturally from the sources. They need not be imposed — only noticed.

## Lesson Structure

Each week follows the same rhythm established in Unit 1:

| Day | Focus |
|-----|-------|
| **Day 1** | Context or Geography Lesson — the situation entering the period |
| **Day 2** | Core History Narrative — story-driven lesson with discussion |
| **Day 3** | Primary Source, Biography, or Document Study |
| **Day 4** | Narration, copywork, and notebooking |

**Every lesson includes:**
- Teacher narration with Stories for the Heart
- Questions for the American Mind (oral discussion)
- Narration prompts for both forms (oral or written)
- Copywork passage (Form IA) or dictation passage (Form IIA)
- One notebooking page (illustration + short narration)

## A Note on Difficult History

This unit addresses the contradiction at the heart of the founding: men who wrote "all men are created equal" while holding other men in slavery. Students should encounter this honestly. The Hillsdale framework is clear: the contradiction is genuine, students should rightly grapple with it, and the Declaration's principles of equality ultimately provided the ground on which later Americans — Lincoln, Douglass, King — stood to demand that the nation live up to its own words. Present the contradiction. Trust the students.

## How to Use Both Forms Together

The whole family hears the same stories. On Days 1–3, deliver the lesson to both students together. On Day 4, assign each student his own narration form (oral for Form IA, written paragraph for Form IIA). For formal assessments, assign each student his appropriate quiz or test form.

Discussion questions naturally differentiate: after reading or narrating together, ask Form IA students simpler questions ("Who was Paul Revere? What did he do?") and Form IIA students richer ones ("Why did John Adams's defense of the redcoats require courage? What does it tell us about him?"). Both questions point to the same truth — they just ask for different depth.
