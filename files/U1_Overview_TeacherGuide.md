## A Family History & Civics Curriculum
### Unit 1 — The British Colonies of North America, 1492–1763
**Teacher Overview & Guide**

## About This Curriculum

**The New World Made Ready** is a two-year family history and civics study for upper-elementary students (grades 3–6), designed for families learning together across multiple ages. It follows the four-unit arc of the Hillsdale K-12 American History framework, expanding each unit into a full semester of rich, literature-based study with differentiated student materials.

**Two-Year Scope:**
- **Year 1:** Unit 1 (British Colonies, 1492–1763) · Unit 2 (American Founding, 1763–1789)
- **Year 2:** Unit 3 (Early Republic, 1789–1848) · Unit 4 (The Civil War Era, 1848–1877)

**Pacing:** 4 lessons per week · ~9 weeks per unit · 36 weeks per school year

**Two Student Forms:**
- **Form IA** — For younger or developing writers (grades 3–4 range). Questions answered in 1–3 complete sentences. Matching and fill-in-the-blank assessments. Shorter notebooking responses.
- **Form IIA** — For stronger writers (grades 5–6 range). Questions answered in organized paragraph responses. Short essay assessments. Full notebooking pages with thesis-level prompts.

*Both forms cover identical content. Families may use one form for all students or assign forms individually.*

## Unit 1 Enduring Ideas

Students who complete this unit will carry these ideas forward through all subsequent study:

1. America's varied geography — its rivers, mountains, plains, and sheltered coasts — shaped the people who settled here and made their flourishing possible.
2. The discovery, exploration, and settlement of the Western Hemisphere was one of the most consequential series of events in human history, setting in motion changes that are still unfolding.
3. The contact between native North American and European civilizations brought both gifts and wounds to both peoples. Honest history requires holding both truths at once.
4. The British colonies of North America were unlike any other settlements in the world. Their particular freedoms — of land ownership, of religion, of self-governance — gradually shaped the colonists into something new: the American.
5. The freedom the colonists enjoyed was not an accident. It grew from English legal tradition, providential circumstance, and the hard choices of ordinary people who braved genuine risk in pursuit of something better.

## A Note on Providence

This curriculum holds a *quietly Christian* frame — not devotional, not sectarian, but shaped by the conviction that history is not merely accidental. Teachers may draw out, where naturally present in the sources, the sense that something larger was at work in the founding of this nation: the Pilgrims' confidence that God went before them, Winthrop's vision of "a city upon a hill," Washington's repeated acknowledgment of "the hand of Providence." These themes arise from the historical record itself and need not be imposed. They are simply noticed — and honored.

## Lesson Structure

Each week follows a predictable rhythm, which reduces friction and builds the habit of historical thinking:

| Day | Focus |
|-----|-------|
| **Day 1** | Geography or Context Lesson — map work, land and peoples |
| **Day 2** | Core History Narrative — story-driven lesson with Questions for the Mind |
| **Day 3** | Primary Source, Biography, or Supplemental Study |
| **Day 4** | Notebooking, Worksheet, or Review |

**Every lesson includes:**
- Teacher narrative notes (what to tell, what to emphasize)
- Discussion questions at two levels (Form IA and IIA)
- A "Story for the Heart" — a narrative moment to dwell in
- A "Key to the Lesson" — the one idea that must land
- Connections to civics vocabulary and U.S. Civics Test questions

## Civics Integration

This unit introduces the following civics vocabulary and concepts, drawn from U.S. Civics Test preparation:

- Self-government and representative assemblies
- Rule of law (Magna Carta → Mayflower Compact → colonial legislatures)
- Religious freedom as a founding motivation
- Who lived in America before Europeans arrived (Q.74)
- Why colonists came to America (Q.73)
- What group was taken and sold as slaves (Q.75)
- Benjamin Franklin's accomplishments (Q.85)
- George Washington's accomplishments (Q.86)

Civics terms are flagged throughout lesson notes with a **⚖️** symbol.

## Geography Notes

Geography lessons in this curriculum require blank or outline maps of:
- North America (physical features: mountains, rivers, plains, coastlines)
- The 13 Original Colonies (labeled)
- Colonial regions (New England, Middle, Southern)
- French and British territorial claims, 1754–1763

**Recommended public-domain map sources:**
- **Library of Congress Geography & Map Division:** [loc.gov/maps](https://loc.gov/maps) — searchable historical maps, freely downloadable
- **David Rumsey Map Collection:** [davidrumsey.com](https://davidrumsey.com) — high-resolution historical maps, free for educational use
- **Perry-Castañeda Library Map Collection (UT Austin):** [lib.utexas.edu/maps](https://lib.utexas.edu/maps) — clean outline maps of U.S. regions
- **National Atlas / USGS Historical Maps:** public domain, downloadable as PDFs

**For clean labeled outline maps suitable for student printing:** Grok (xAI image generation) or a similar image tool can produce a clean line-art outline map from a prompt such as: *"Black and white outline map of the thirteen British colonies of North America, labeled, suitable for student coloring, no background, clean lines."* These are recommended for the student map activities in Lesson 1 and Lesson 3.

## How to Use This Guide with Older Siblings or Co-ops

This curriculum is designed for the whole family to hear the same story. Discussion questions naturally differentiate: ask Form IA questions of younger children and Form IIA questions of older ones during the same lesson. For formal assessment, assign each child their appropriate form.

In a co-op setting, the teacher delivers the Day 1–3 lessons to the whole group; Days 4 written work is assigned and completed at home.

## A Note on Difficult History

This unit does not flinch from hard things. Students will encounter:
- The genuine dangers and hardships of exploration and settlement
- The Starving Time at Jamestown
- The displacement and suffering of Native American peoples
- The origins and horror of the Atlantic slave trade and Middle Passage

These are not embellishments — they are the truth of the story, and treating students as capable of handling honest history respects their growing moral intelligence. The goal is neither to horrify nor to sanitize, but to help students understand why events happened, what they cost, and what they reveal about human nature — including the best and worst of it.

The Hillsdale principle applies here: *"They will encounter a mixed picture. At times, they will see cooperation, care, and mutual respect; at other times, they will see all the duplicity and injustice that human nature is capable of. They will see these traits exhibited by all parties at various moments."*
