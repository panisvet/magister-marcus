Spelling & Word Study

Charlotte Mason method — spelling is learned from good books through studied dictation, not cold tests. The child studies a passage, spells the hard words correctly first, then writes; you prevent every error. These pages give the rules and patterns behind the words, so a parent can name why a word is spelled as it is.

Year 3 — Rules & Patterns

Short vowels in C-V-C words.
Examples: cat, bed, pig, hot, sun
Watch for: two-letter sight words break the pattern: to, do, go.
Consonant digraphs & blends make one sound.
Examples: sh (ship), ch (chin), th (this), ck (duck), ng (sing); bl, st, tr, gr
Watch for: ‘ck’ only after a short vowel — back, not bair.
Silent (magic) e makes the vowel say its name.
Examples: cap→cape, kit→kite, hop→hope, cub→cube
Watch for: have, give, love, come — the e does not lengthen.
Vowel teams — the first vowel usually ‘talks’.
Examples: ai (rain), ee (feet), ea (leaf), oa (boat), ay (day)
Watch for: bread, head (short e); great, break (long a); said.
Soft c and g before e, i, y.
Examples: city, cent, cycle; gem, giant, gym
Watch for: get, give, girl keep the hard g.
Plurals: add -s; add -es after s, x, z, ch, sh.
Examples: cats, dogs; boxes, wishes, buzzes
Watch for: baby→babies (consonant + y → ies); boys, days (vowel + y → just s).
The 1-1-1 doubling rule (one syllable, one vowel, one final consonant).
Examples: hop→hopping, run→running, sad→sadder
Watch for: x never doubles: fix→fixing; two vowels don’t: rain→raining.
Drop silent e before a vowel suffix; keep it before a consonant suffix.
Examples: make→making, hope→hoping; hope→hopeful, nice→nicely
Watch for: true→truly, argue→argument (a few drop it anyway).

Words to picture (studied, not sounded out)

said, was, one, two, come, some, they, because, friend, could, would, should, own, people, again.

Year 6 — Advanced Rules & Exceptions

i before e, except after c — or when it says /ay/.
Examples: believe, field, piece; receive, ceiling; neighbor, weigh
Watch for: seize, weird, foreign, height, either, protein — learn these by sight.
Doubling in longer words: double only when the final syllable is stressed.
Examples: be-GIN→beginning, re-FER→referred, for-GET→forgetting
Watch for: OP-en→opening, VIS-it→visiting (stress not on the last syllable → no double).
Suffix changes: change y→i; drop silent e; keep e after soft c/g before -ous/-able.
Examples: happy→happiness, beauty→beautiful; create→creation; courage→courageous, notice→noticeable
Watch for: keep y after a vowel (enjoy→enjoyment); day→daily is an exception.
-able vs -ible.
Examples: -able on full base words: comfort→comfortable, depend→dependable; -ible on roots & after soft c/g: visible, legible, edible, terrible
Watch for: after a soft c/g, -ible keeps the softness: forcible, eligible.
Endings that sound like ‘shun’: -tion, -sion, -cian.
Examples: action, education; tension, decision; musician, physician
Watch for: -cian usually means a person; -sion often follows d or s (decide→decision).
Prefixes never change the root — so double letters happen.
Examples: mis+spell=misspell, un+necessary=unnecessary, ir+regular=irregular, dis+satisfy=dissatisfy
Watch for: also over+run=overrun; the root keeps every letter.
Silent letters, often from Latin/Greek roots.
Examples: doubt, debt (Latin dubitare, debitum); sign, design (signum); knee, gnaw, write (old English); rhythm, chorus (Greek)
Watch for: knowing the root explains the silent letter — debt keeps the b of debitum.
Homophones — spelling shows the meaning.
Examples: their / there / they’re; its / it’s; principal / principle; stationary / stationery
Watch for: ‘stationEry’ = lettErs (paper); ‘principAl’ = my pAl (the person).

Year 6 — Latin & Greek Derivatives (Word Study)

This strand runs beside your Via Latina course. Each week take one root; the student collects English words built from it, and — the payoff for spelling — learns to spell long words by their parts (man·u·script, in·terr·upt), and to guess meanings from the root.
