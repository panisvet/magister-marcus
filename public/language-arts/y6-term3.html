Simile & Metaphor

When a writer departs from the plain way of speaking to assert or assume some striking relation between things, the expression is a figure of speech. The first service of such imagery is to the thought itself — it makes the thought clearer and stronger; the beauty comes second, as Reed & Kellogg say, the way a brooch may adorn as well as do duty to the dress.

A simile points the likeness out, usually with like or as: your copywork says Silas “seemed to weave, like the spider, from pure impulse.” A metaphor assumes the likeness and carries the word straight over: call Guinea fowls “fretting housewives,” or say with Shakespeare, “Night’s candles are burnt out” — no like, no as, the stars simply are candles for the length of the sentence.

Definition. “A Simile is a figure of speech in which we point out or assert a likeness between things otherwise unlike.” “A Metaphor is a figure of speech in which, assuming the likeness between two things, we apply to one of them the term that denotes the other.”

a. In the copywork, what is compared to what? Which word signals the simile?

b. Turn it into a metaphor — say the sentence so that Silas is the spider. What changed?

c. “The setting of a great hope is like the setting of the sun.” Name the figure and the two things.

d. “Night’s candles are burnt out.” What are the candles? Why is this not a simile?

e. Find one comparison in this week’s Silas Marner passage and classify it.

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Higher Lessons in English (1896), Lessons 84 and 150.

Personification

Personification raises things above their proper plane of being. Reed & Kellogg mark three lifts: a mere thing raised to the plane of an animal — “The sea licks your feet, its huge flanks purr pleasantly for you”; an animal raised to the plane of a person — “So talked the spirited, sly Snake”; a thing raised all the way to personhood — “Earth fills her lap with pleasures of her own.” Things take on dignity as they rise in the scale of being.

Crane opens The Red Badge on this figure. In your copywork the cold “passed reluctantly from the earth” — reluctance is a human feeling — and the fogs are “retiring,” like sentries going off duty, while the army lies “resting” on the hills as one great creature. One quiet adverb can carry the whole figure.

Definition. “A Personification is a figure of speech in which things are raised to a plane of being above their own — to or toward that of persons.”

a. In the copywork, which words give the cold and the fogs human ways?

b. “The sea licks your feet, its huge flanks purr pleasantly for you.” To what plane is the sea raised?

c. “Earth fills her lap with pleasures of her own.” Point to every personifying word.

d. In this week’s forest passage, find one more personification and read it aloud.

e. Write one sentence giving the campfire or the wind a human act or feeling.

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Higher Lessons in English (1896), Lessons 47 and 150.

Contrast & Antithesis

Plutarch’s whole design is contrast: he wrote his Lives in pairs, a Greek against a Roman, so that each might be seen by the light of the other. When a writer compresses such a contrast into one sentence, setting opposed things over against each other limb for limb, the figure is antithesis — and each part, say Reed & Kellogg, shines with its own light and with the light reflected from the other.

Mark the balance in these: “Wealth may seek us, but wisdom must be sought.” “The race is not to the swift, nor the battle to the strong.” Your copywork weighs two men’s faults in one scale: the one… easiness and good-nature; the other… pride and severity. Sentences built on antithesis are our best examples of balanced sentences.

Definition. “An Antithesis is a figure of speech in which things mutually opposed in some particular are set over against each other.” It gives great force to the thought.

a. In the copywork, what is set against what? Name both opposed pairs.

b. “Wealth may seek us, but wisdom must be sought.” Which words balance which?

c. “The race is not to the swift, nor the battle to the strong.” Find the matched limbs.

d. Brutus: “Not that I loved Caesar less, but that I loved Rome more.” Show the balance word by word.

e. Write one balanced sentence comparing your two Plutarch heroes.

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Higher Lessons in English (1896), Lessons 77 and 150.

Definition & Example Structure

You have memorized Reed & Kellogg definitions all year; now look at how they are built. “A Pronoun is a word used for a noun”: first the term, then its class (a word), then the mark that parts it from every other word (used for a noun). The grammar underneath is your oldest pattern — term as S, a linking verb, and the class with its marks as PN. A definition is a predicate-noun sentence with its boots polished.

Then examples make the definition visible. Your copywork defines by appositives: a crown of laurel, the emblem of victory, and the reward given to those who had served their country well — two APP phrases doing a definition’s work in the middle of a story. When you must explain a term in your own writing, do it in this order: name, class, mark, example.

Definition. A Definition names the term, gives its class, and adds the marks that part it from all others of that class. An Example shows the definition at work.

a. Test the build of “A Clause is a part of a sentence containing a subject and its predicate.” Name the term, the class, the distinguishing mark.

b. In the copywork, find the two appositives that define the crown; tag each APP.

c. Mark S, V, and PN: “A triumph was a grand procession in which a victorious general rode through Rome.”

d. Define gladiator in the same shape; add one example sentence.

e. Pick a term from this week’s reading — consul, legion, forum — and give definition plus example.

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Graded Lessons in English (1889), Lessons 4 and 77.

Rhetorical Question, Tricolon, Repetition

Brutus asks your copywork question of a whole crowd: “Who is here so base, that would be a bondman?” He wants no hand raised. A rhetorical question is asked for effect, not for an answer — it forces every hearer to answer inwardly, which is stronger than being told.

A tricolon sets three parallel members in a row: “Friends, Romans, countrymen.” Reed & Kellogg’s rule of energy applies inside it: put the strongest last, so the series climbs. And repetition, which is a fault when it is needless, becomes power when every return strengthens the thought — hear Webster close in on the murderer: “No eye has seen him, no ear has heard him… It is accomplished. The deed is done.”

Definition. A Rhetorical Question is asked for effect, no answer being expected. A Tricolon is a series of three parallel members, strongest last. Repetition repeats a word or thought so that each return adds force.

a. In the copywork, what answer does Brutus expect? What does the question do to the crowd?

b. “Friends, Romans, countrymen, lend me your ears.” Why this order of the three?

c. Webster: “No eye has seen him, no ear has heard him… It is accomplished. The deed is done.” Name every device you hear.

d. In the psalm you are reciting, find two lines that say one thing twice — why is the repetition not needless?

e. Write a tricolon of your own about this week, strongest item last.

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Higher Lessons in English (1896), Lessons 84 and 150.

Review — Mark All Figures & Constructions

Lay out the term’s kit: simile, metaphor, personification, antithesis, rhetorical question, tricolon, repetition — and beneath them the constructions that carry them: the balanced sentence, the S–V–PN definition, the appositive that defines. The drill is always the same three steps: read the passage aloud, name the figure, and prove it by pointing to the signal — the like or as, the carried-over word, the human touch, the matched limbs, the unanswered question.

Definition. Recite: a simile points out a likeness; a metaphor assumes it; personification raises things toward persons; antithesis sets opposites over against each other; a rhetorical question expects no answer; a tricolon marches three abreast; repetition returns to strengthen.

a. “He seemed to weave, like the spider, from pure impulse.” Name and prove it.

b. “The cold passed reluctantly from the earth.” Name and prove it.

c. “Not that I loved Caesar less, but that I loved Rome more.” Two devices — name both.

d. “Who is here so base, that would be a bondman?” Name it.

e. Choose one paragraph from the term’s books; mark every figure you find, and diagram its plainest sentence.

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Higher Lessons in English (1896), Lesson 150 (review).
