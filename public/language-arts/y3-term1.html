Week 1 · Lesson

Dr. Verb Proves What a Sentence Is

Far away in Grammar-land, where all the words live, old Judge Grammar once asked his court to show him what a sentence is. Serjeant Parsing called three words forward. “Sun,” said Mr. Noun. “Bright,” said Mr. Adjective. “The,” said little Article. Serjeant Parsing put them together every way he could—the bright sun, the sun bright—but they would not make a sentence, no matter how he shuffled them.

Then Dr. Verb spoke up. “When you say the bright sun, you still have not made a sentence, for you have not said what it does. But say the sun is bright, and then you tell a fact about the sun, and you have made a sentence fit to set before the king.” A sentence, you see, does two things: it names someone or something, and then it tells something about them. Naming part, telling part—one complete thought.

Look at your copywork this week: Once upon a time there were two princes who were twins. The naming part is two princes; the telling part says there were—they lived, they existed, once upon a time. It could stand all alone on a page and still make sense. That is how you know it is a sentence.

The rule. A sentence is one complete thought: it names someone or something, then tells about them—and it can stand all alone.

a. Listen: The bright sun. Sentence, or only pretty words? Why?

b. Listen: The sun is bright. Sentence or not? What does it tell?

c. I say a naming part—two princes. You add a telling part out loud.

d. Make a whole sentence about Perseus from this week’s reading: name him, then tell one thing he did.

Adapted from M. L. Nesbitt, Grammar-land (1877), ch. 8.

Mr. Noun and His Naming Words

The first word-gentleman called before Judge Grammar was Mr. Noun—a stout, rich fellow, very well dressed. “What is your name?” asked Serjeant Parsing. “Name,” answered Mr. Noun. “Do not trifle, sir!” said the Judge. But Mr. Noun had answered truly: “My name is Name, for noun means name. The name of everything belongs to me.”

“The name of everything you can see, or touch, or taste, or smell, or hear, belongs to me,” he went on. Desk, pen, window, thunder, smoke—all his. And more besides: things you can only feel in your heart, like love, kindness, and happiness, are nouns too, for they are names of real things, even though your fingers cannot touch them.

Try it on your copywork: There was no living thing around them, not a fly, not a moss upon the rocks. Mr. Noun owns four words there—thing, fly, moss, rocks. Each one names something. Take the nouns away and the sentence would have nothing left to talk about.

The rule. A noun is a naming word. It names a person, a place, or a thing—even a thing you can only feel, like kindness.

a. Look around the room and name five things. Every name you say is a noun.

b. Name a noun you can hear but never see. (Mr. Noun suggests thunder.)

c. Name a noun you feel in your heart when someone shares with you.

d. Say the copywork sentence aloud and stop at each of the four nouns.

Adapted from M. L. Nesbitt, Grammar-land (1877), ch. 1.

Mr. Noun’s Particular Names

Mr. Noun told the court he owns more than the names of things. “The names of persons belong to me too—every person in the world, from the Queen on her throne to the raggedest beggar-boy in the street. And not people only, but all pet dogs, cats, birds, and rabbits: Fido, Tabby, Bright-eye, Tiny.”

Then he said something to remember: “I am very particular about such names. I call them proper nouns, and expect them always to be written with a capital letter.” A common noun names any one of a kind—boy, city, warrior. A proper noun names one particular person or place, and it wears a capital letter like a crown.

Your copywork shows both kinds: The name of this great warrior was Julius Caesar. He was a Roman. Warrior is common—the world is full of warriors. But Julius Caesar names one man only, and Roman comes from the name of one city, Rome; so they hold their capitals proudly.

The rule. A proper noun names one particular person, place, or pet, and it always begins with a capital letter.

a. Say your own name, and the name of your street or town. Which letter is the capital?

b. I say a common noun, you answer with a proper one: a warrior… a city… a river… a queen.

c. Which words in the copywork wear capitals, and why does warrior not get one?

d. Give a proper name to an imaginary pet, the way Mr. Noun would—and say which letter you would write big.

Adapted from M. L. Nesbitt, Grammar-land (1877), ch. 1.

Dr. Verb, the Busiest Word in the Land

Next before the Judge came Dr. Verb, bustling up with an air of great importance. “My lord, my name is Verb. I am called Verb because verb means word, and the verb is the most important word—the word, in fact—in every sentence.” Mr. Noun was quite cross to hear it. Was not the name of everything his?

“I know that,” said Dr. Verb. “But do you suppose that when people have simply named a thing they have made a sentence? Not a bit of it. You must say whether it is or has or does anything: Ice is cold. Puss has a tail. Blackbirds sing.” Every word that tells of being, having, or doing belongs to Dr. Verb.

Find him in your copywork: A fine young Working-bee left his hive, one lovely summer’s morning, to gather honey from the flowers. What did the bee do? He left—there is the verb, the doing-word that sets the whole sentence in motion.

The rule. A verb is a telling word. It tells what someone or something does, has, or is. Without a verb there is no sentence.

a. Act out three verbs—hop, stretch, whisper—and name each one as you do it.

b. Find the verb: The bee gathers honey. The lamb sleeps. Perseus is brave.

c. Make one sentence with is, one with has, one with a doing-word, about anything in the garden.

d. What did the Working-bee do in the copywork? Say the verb by itself.

Adapted from M. L. Nesbitt, Grammar-land (1877), ch. 8.

Three Little Marks at the End

Every sentence begins with a capital letter, and every sentence ends with a little mark that tells your voice what to do. A sentence that simply tells something ends with a period (.)—your voice settles down and rests. A sentence that asks something ends with a question mark (?)—your voice bends upward, waiting for an answer. And a sentence full of strong feeling ends with an exclamation mark (!)—your voice rings out.

An old schoolbook liked to turn this into a game: the teacher would read a sentence with no mark at all, and the children had to give it the right one. Does he want to call us to him—that asks, so it earns a question mark. These Saint Bernard dogs are very intelligent—that tells, so a period. Watch out—that is a cry, so an exclamation mark.

Your copywork this week holds two of the three marks in one breath: What shall I do to be saved? — Life! life! Eternal life! Hear how the question climbs, and how the answer rings like a bell, three times.

The rule. A telling sentence ends with a period. A question ends with a question mark. A strong feeling ends with an exclamation mark.

a. I read a sentence; you call out its mark: Where is the shepherd… The sheep are in the meadow… The wolf is coming…

b. Say the copywork aloud so a listener could hear the question mark and the exclamation marks.

c. Ask me a question about this week’s story; I must answer with a telling sentence.

d. Say one sentence so full of feeling that it clearly deserves an exclamation mark.

Adapted from H. Jeschke, Beginners’ Book in Language (1912).

A Fat Hippopotamus Is Not a Sentence

The court wished to test Dr. Verb once more, so Serjeant Parsing asked Mr. Noun for another word. “Hippopotamus,” said Mr. Noun. Mr. Adjective gave fat, and little Article gave a. Serjeant Parsing put them together: a fat hippopotamus. “It does not sound wrong,” said Mr. Noun hopefully.

“Not wrong, of course,” answered Dr. Verb, “but you have not made a sentence.” A fat hippopotamus is only a piece—a fragment. It names something, but it never tells. Your ear is left waiting: a fat hippopotamus… did what? Say A fat hippopotamus wallowed in the river, and at last the thought is complete.

Now listen to your copywork: There was once a little princess whose father was king over a great country full of mountains and valleys. Long as it is, it is one complete thought—it names a princess and tells that she lived, once upon a time. Length is not the test. A fragment is missing its naming part or its telling part, however grand it sounds.

The rule. A fragment is only a piece of a sentence—it is missing its naming part or its telling part. Finish the thought before you stop.

a. Fragment or sentence? A fat hippopotamus. If a fragment, finish it aloud.

b. Fragment or sentence? The goblins hid. How do you know?

c. Fragment or sentence? Up the mountain in the dark. Give it a naming part and a telling part.

d. Say the copywork sentence and show me its naming part and its telling part.

Adapted from M. L. Nesbitt, Grammar-land (1877), ch. 8.

Judge Grammar Holds Court: A Review

In six weeks you have met Grammar-land’s chief citizens. Mr. Noun owns every name—princess, thunder, kindness—and his proper nouns wear capital letters. Dr. Verb owns every telling word—is, has, sings—and without him no sentence can be made.

A sentence is one complete thought: a naming part and a telling part that could stand alone. The bright sun is a fragment; the sun is bright is a sentence. Every sentence ends with its own mark: period for telling, question mark for asking, exclamation mark for strong feeling.

The rule. Name it, tell about it, end it with the right mark—that is a sentence.

a. Quick-fire: name two nouns from this week’s tale, one of them proper.

b. Give me a verb for each: the knight… the sea… the little lamb…

c. Sentence or fragment? Over the hills and far away. Fix it aloud.

d. Say a telling sentence, a question, and an exclamation about the tale—your voice showing each mark.

Mr. Adjective, the Chatterbox

The next Part-of-Speech called before Judge Grammar was Mr. Adjective. “He is the greatest chatterbox and the veriest gossip that ever lived,” said Serjeant Parsing. “You never in all your life knew any one who could say so much about one thing. Mr. Noun cannot mention a word but Mr. Adjective is ready to tell all about it—whether it is little or big, blue or green, good or bad.”

When pussy comes into the room, Mr. Adjective whispers that she is a “dear, sweet, soft, warm, little pet.” Five whispers, and every one of them hangs upon the noun pet. That is his whole business: an adjective describes a noun. It answers which one? what kind? how many?

He has been whispering in your copywork too: Also the rain made a great trampling noise on the roof. What kind of noise? A great, trampling noise—two adjectives that let you hear the rain stamping overhead like a crowd of little boots.

The rule. An adjective describes a noun. It answers which one? what kind? how many?

a. Describe a cat with three adjectives, as Mr. Adjective would—then a dragon.

b. Find the two adjectives in the copywork sentence. Which noun do they describe?

c. I name a noun—porridge, castle, goblin—you whisper one fitting adjective for each.

d. How many? Say a sentence using a counting adjective, like seven or many.

Adapted from M. L. Nesbitt, Grammar-land (1877), ch. 5.

Mr. Adjective Chooses His Words Well

Mr. Adjective was offended when Serjeant Parsing accused him of calling people names. “I do not call people names,” he said indignantly. “I qualify them.” To qualify means to tell exactly what kind. And there lies a secret: some adjectives work hard, and some are lazy.

Lazy adjectives—nice, big, good—could describe almost anything, so they paint almost nothing. A nice day, a nice pudding, a nice pony: what do you actually see? Nothing clear. But a frosty day, a steaming pudding, a shaggy pony—now a picture springs up before your eyes. A strong adjective is exact; it fits one thing snugly, like a key in its own lock.

Your copywork chooses its words well: Soon afterwards, however, he went singing upwards into the bright, blue sky. Not a nice sky—a bright, blue sky. You can see the very color and the shine of it.

The rule. A strong adjective is exact and paints a clear picture. A weak one—nice, big, good—is vague and paints nothing.

a. Trade the weak word for a strong one: a nice cake… a big dog… a good knight.

b. Which paints the clearer picture: a bad storm, or a howling storm? Why?

c. Describe today’s sky without using nice, big, or pretty.

d. Say the copywork aloud; then try it with nice instead of bright, blue, and tell me what was lost.

Adapted from M. L. Nesbitt, Grammar-land (1877), ch. 5.

Adverb Sticks Close to Dr. Verb

One day Dr. Verb was heard grumbling about a companion who follows him everywhere. “He sticks on to me so tight sometimes that we look like one instead of two. Besides, he is always teasing by asking why, and when, and how everything is done.” That companion is Adverb, and when we were at school, said Dr. Verb, the boys called him Adverb because he was always adding his words on to mine.

An adverb adds to a verb. It tells how a thing is done (slowly, bravely), when it is done (soon, never), or where (here, upwards). Mr. Adjective complained of him too: “Adverb will take my words, right good adjectives, stick a ly on to them, and call them his adverbs—bright becomes brightly, bad becomes badly.”

Watch him at work in your copywork: But Athené stood before him and spoke gently, and bid him have no fear. How did she speak? Gently. One small adverb, and you can hear the very tone of the goddess’s voice.

The rule. An adverb tells how, when, or where—most often about a verb. Many adverbs end in -ly.

a. Walk across the room slowly, then proudly, then silently—naming each adverb.

b. Turn these adjectives into adverbs aloud: brave… sweet… quick… gentle.

c. Find the adverb in the copywork. Which question does it answer—how, when, or where?

d. Add a when adverb and a where adverb to: The lark sang.

Adapted from M. L. Nesbitt, Grammar-land (1877), ch. 12.

Two Describers, Two Masters

Adverb, it turns out, teases Mr. Adjective just as he teases Dr. Verb. “He is always putting very, quite, more, most, and words of that sort before my adjectives,” said Mr. Adjective, “and exaggerating them: very beautiful, quite charming, most provoking.” So the two describers each keep to their own master: adjectives belong to nouns, adverbs to verbs—and sometimes an adverb props up an adjective too.

Here is the way to tell them apart. Ask what the word describes. A brave knight—brave tells about the knight, a noun, so it is an adjective. The knight fought bravely—bravely tells about the fighting, a verb, so it is an adverb.

Your copywork keeps both servants busy: They knew that it would be very difficult to take his strong fortress. Strong describes the fortress—adjective. Very leans on difficult, stretching it taller—that is Adverb, exaggerating as usual.

The rule. Adjectives describe nouns; adverbs describe verbs—and little adverbs like very can strengthen an adjective.

a. Adjective or adverb? The gentle princess… she smiled gently. Tell how you know.

b. In the copywork, which word does strong describe? Which word does very lean upon?

c. Say a sentence with loud; now say one with loudly.

d. Exaggerate like Adverb: put very or quite into a sentence about your supper.

Adapted from M. L. Nesbitt, Grammar-land (1877), ch. 12.

The Court Rests: Term Review

See how far you have walked in Grammar-land this term. Mr. Noun names everything, and his proper nouns wear capitals. Dr. Verb tells what things do, have, and are. Mr. Adjective describes nouns; Adverb adds how, when, and where to verbs. And every complete thought ends with its own mark.

Your copywork gathers several friends in one breath: Little Lamb, who made thee? Dost thou know who made thee? There is a noun (Lamb), an adjective (Little), a verb (made)—and both sentences ask, so both end with question marks.

The rule. Nouns name; verbs tell; adjectives describe nouns; adverbs describe verbs; every sentence is one complete thought with an end mark.

a. In Little Lamb, who made thee?—point out the noun, the adjective, and the verb aloud.

b. Quick-fire: give me one proper noun, one strong adjective, one -ly adverb.

c. Fragment or sentence? Beside the still waters. Finish it.

d. Recite Psalm 23, and afterward name one noun and one verb you heard yourself say.

Adapted from M. L. Nesbitt, Grammar-land (1877).

Term Review — A Describing Paragraph

Read: Blake, “The Lamb” or “The Tyger.” · Memory: recite Psalm 23 whole.

Day 1 Read the poem twice; narrate what it pictures.   Day 2 Copywork of a couplet.   Day 3 Build a description aloud (you scribe).   Day 4 Child writes his best 3–4 sentences.   Day 5 Read it back with delight; recite.

Copywork

Little Lamb, who made thee? Dost thou know who made thee?

Write

Describe one thing so a reader can picture it — use at least two exact adjectives and one adverb. Say it aloud first.
