Prepared Dictation, Copywork & Scribing Guide

Charlotte Mason "studied dictation" · leveled for both students

Two students, opposite ends of the ladder. The transcription work is scaffolded the same way for both — short, studied, error-prevented — but the texts are age-matched: the 8-year-old copies from early living books; the 11-year-old takes dictation from Year 6 literature. For a struggling speller you control difficulty by length and pre-taught words, not by babying the text.

  • 8-year-old — copywork only this year. Bank B below. (Gentle one-line dictation optional late in the year, only if copywork is effortless.)
  • 11-year-old — prepared dictation as the spelling engine. Bank A below, drawn from the Year 6 reading spine.

The core principle

In studied dictation the child never writes a word wrong. He studies the passage first; you remove it, dictate slowly, and prevent every error the instant it's about to happen. Only correct spelling ever reaches the page, so that is what gets reinforced. This is the opposite of a cold spelling test, and it is exactly why it suits a struggling speller.

The weekly routine (11-year-old — 10–15 min, 2–3 days)

Day 1 — Study (~5 min). Read the passage aloud together. The child finds the tricky words; you add any he misses (pre-listed below). For each: look → say the letters → cover → picture it → write once on scrap → check. This is the whole spelling lesson — no separate list.

Day 2 — Dictate (~5–7 min). Passage out of sight. Dictate one phrase at a time, slowly, repeating each phrase once. Prevent, don't correct — the moment he hesitates or starts a wrong letter, show the word again before it's written wrong. A prevented error is a success. Read the finished piece back with warmth.

Day 3 (optional). Re-do only the 1–2 words that needed prevention.

Dosage. Start at one sentence. Grow to two only when a single sentence comes out clean two weeks running. A clean short passage beats a messy long one every time.

Spelling page. The 2–3 target words per passage are the week's spelling focus, grouped by pattern. Worksheet pattern: trace → cover-and-write → use-in-a-sentence (you may scribe the sentence).

Bank A — the 11-year-old (prepared dictation, Year 6 texts)

Work down the list over the term; repeat any tier until clean. All passages are public domain; two are by the workbook's own authors, and one doubles as memory work.

1. Dickens, A Christmas Carol (warm-up)

Marley was dead: to begin with.
  • Targets: dead, begin · feature: the colon. Short entry into a grown-up book.

2. Dickens, A Christmas Carol

Old Marley was as dead as a door-nail.
  • Targets: door-nail (hyphen), dead · feature: a simile (Term 5 tie-in).

3. Proverbs 3:5 (KJV) (doubles as memory work)

Trust in the Lord with all thine heart; and lean not unto thine own understanding.
  • Targets: understanding, thine, heart · feature: the semicolon.

4. George Eliot, Silas Marner (opening phrase) — workbook author

In the days when the spinning-wheels hummed busily in the farmhouses…
  • Targets: spinning-wheels, hummed, busily, farmhouses · feature: -ing; -ea- /e/; compound words. Same author as the workbook's Narration I.

5. R. L. Stevenson, Kidnapped (opening)

I will begin the story of my adventures with a certain morning early in the month of June.
  • Targets: adventures, certain, June · feature: soft c; multi-syllable ad-ven-tures.

6. Bunyan, Pilgrim's Progress (opening — longest)

As I walked through the wilderness of this world, I lighted on a certain place where was a den, and laid me down in that place to sleep.
  • Targets: wilderness, certain, lighted · feature: -ilder-; the -ough/-alk family (walked, through).
End-of-year bridge: once dictation is comfortable, take one sentence from the same Lewis or Tolkien passage he'll meet in the workbook (Description VI / Description III) and study its spelling in advance — the workbook passage then arrives as an old friend.

Bank B — the 8-year-old (copywork; gentle dictation only if ready)

Short, warm, early living books. Copy in best hand; read back with delight. These are the passages from your prep course — right on level for an 8-year-old.

1. Stevenson, "The Swing" — How do you like to go up in a swing, up in the air so blue? · words: swing, blue, air.

2. Aesop, "The Crow and the Pitcher" — A crow, half dead with thirst, came upon a pitcher of water. · words: thirst, pitcher, water.

3. Psalm 23:1–2 (KJV) — The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want. He maketh me to lie down in green pastures. · words: shepherd, pastures, shall.

4. Sewell, Black Beauty — The first place that I can well remember was a large pleasant meadow with a pond of clear water in it. · words: remember, pleasant, meadow.

If, late in the year, copywork is effortless, you may try one of these as a single studied-dictation sentence — same routine, one phrase at a time, prevent every error.

Scribing policy — where to write for them

Their bottleneck is transcription (handwriting + spelling), not thinking. Don't let mechanics gate composition. Scribe wherever the point is the idea; reserve the child's own pen for the places writing is the skill being trained.

In the prep / grammar course (both students):

  • Scribe: all oral narration, all discussion-question answers, any "build a paragraph aloud" work.
  • Child writes himself: the daily copywork line; (11yo) his dictation; and the single imitation/description sentence.

In the target workbook (the 11-year-old, later):

  • Scribe: the mark-up and analysis — Exercise A (mark parts/figures), B (sentence analysis), C/E (constructions, contrast, definition), D (verb lists). He points and tells; you record.
  • Child writes himself: the imitation (Exercise D/F) only — and let him say it aloud and revise orally before writing the final version.

Rule of thumb: he composes and analyzes orally; he writes only copywork, dictation, and his imitation. Hand more back as his hand and spelling strengthen — there's no deadline.
