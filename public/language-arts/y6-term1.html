The Noun at Work — the Five Jobs a Noun Can Do

Read: Bulfinch, Age of Fable — Prometheus & Pandora.

You already know a noun names a person, place, thing, or idea. But inside a sentence, every noun also has a job. Learn these five jobs and you can take almost any sentence apart. Each job has a short tag we will use all year.

TagThe jobWhat it doesFind it by asking…Example (noun in bold)
SSubjectthe noun the sentence is about; it does the verb"Who or what [verb]s?"Prometheus gave fire.
DODirect objectthe noun that receives the actionafter the verb: "[verb] what/whom?"Prometheus gave fire.
OPObject of a prepositionthe noun right after a position word (in, on, to, by, for, through)"in / to / for what?"a gift for men
PNPredicate nounafter a linking verb (is, was, became, seemed), a noun that renames the subject"The subject is a ___?"Prometheus was a Titan.
APPAppositivea noun set right beside another to rename it — usually inside commas"Which noun does this rename?"Prometheus, a wise Titan, …

How to find them — in order

  1. Find the verb. Ask "who or what does it?" — that noun is the S.
  2. After the verb, ask "what?" or "whom?" — that noun is the DO.
  3. Spot any little position words (prepositions). The noun after each is an OP.
  4. Is the verb a linking verb (is, was, became)? The noun that renames the subject is a PN.
  5. Is a noun set beside another (often between commas) just to rename it? That's an APP.

Worked example

Prometheus, a wise Titan, gave fire to men.

Prometheus = S (who gave?) · a wise Titan = APP (renames Prometheus, in commas) · gave = verb · fire = DO (gave what?) · to men = OP (noun after "to").

And with a linking verb: "Prometheus was a Titan." → Titan = PN (it renames the subject).

Teach it inductively (Day 2). Read the worked sentence aloud, then ask the five questions above and let your student discover each job before you name its tag. Name the job first ("this noun receives the action"), then attach the short tag ("we call that the direct object — DO"). Don't drill the letters cold. Keep it oral; you scribe. This lesson stays in the booklet as his reference all year.

Pronouns & Antecedents

Try retelling Perseus without pronouns and you will hear the trouble at once: “Perseus took Perseus’s sword, and Perseus set out on Perseus’s voyage.” Language keeps a small stock of stand-in words — I, you, he, she, it, we, they — to spare us that clatter. Reed & Kellogg open with a schoolroom scene: a boy confessing does not announce his own name; he simply says I whispered.

The noun a pronoun stands for is its antecedent — the word that “goes before.” In this week’s copywork, Polydectes sent him: the antecedent of him is Perseus. And because a pronoun does any job a noun can do, your function tags still apply: He conquered (S); the gods aided him (DO); the shield hung beside her (OP).

One courtesy the reader is owed: every pronoun must point plainly to one antecedent. If two men stand in a sentence, he must not wobble between them.

Definition. “A Pronoun is a word used for a noun.” The word for which a pronoun stands is called its antecedent.

a. In the copywork sentence, point to him and name its antecedent aloud. Then find the pronoun in “who had laid waste the country” and tell what it stands for.

b. Tag each pronoun’s job (S, DO, or OP): They are conquered. · Minerva warned him. · The nymphs kept the sandals for her.

c. Rescue this sentence with pronouns: “Athena guided Perseus, and Athena warned Perseus.”

d. Fix the wobble — who is he? “Perseus told the king that he must sail.” Say it two clear ways.

e. Diagram “You will be rewarded” on paper: subject line, upright bar, predicate line.

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Graded Lessons in English (1889), Lessons 19 and 72.

Verbs: Action vs. Linking

Reed & Kellogg sort all verbs by what they assert. Plants grow asserts an action. God is asserts being — nothing more. George sleeps asserts a state of being, the condition George is in. Whatever asserts is a verb, and only verbs assert; that is why every predicate must be or contain one.

Action verbs often pass their action along to a direct object: in the copywork, Theseus bored holes — bored what? holes, the DO. Linking verbs (is, was, became, seemed) assert no action at all; they connect the subject to a word that renames or describes it. “Theseus was a prince” — prince renames Theseus, a PN.

Remember that one verb may be two, three, or four words: is singing, will be sung, might have been sung.

Definition. “A Verb is a word that asserts action, being, or state of being.”

a. In the copywork, find the verbs adds and bored. Which has a direct object? Name and tag it.

b. Say A (action) or L (linking): The hero sailed at dawn. · The monster was terrible. · The people became free.

c. “Theseus was the son of Aegeus.” Point to the linking verb; tag the noun that renames the subject.

d. Supply verbs aloud: “Ships ——.” Give three of action and one of being.

e. Diagram “Laws are obeyed” on paper or the board.

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Graded Lessons in English (1889), Lessons 16 and 39.

Adjectives & Articles

Write boys on a slate and pile up words that fit before it: small, large, white, straight, five, some. Each modifies the noun by adding an idea — of size, color, form, or number. Words that do this work for nouns and pronouns are adjectives. This week’s copywork carries two exact ones: a long white beard; and the little possessive its is doing adjective work too.

The smallest adjectives — the, an or a — some grammarians set apart as articles. An and a are one old word meaning one: keep an before vowel sounds, a before consonant sounds. So it is an heir (the h is silent) but a unit (it opens with a y sound). The ear, not the letter, decides.

Definition. “An Adjective is a word used to modify a noun or a pronoun.” The little words the and an or a are called articles.

a. In the copywork sentence, point to every adjective and touch the noun it modifies.

b. Count the adjectives: “That dilapidated old wooden building has fallen.”

c. Correct aloud: a apple · an brickbat · a hour · an university · a honest answer.

d. Prefix three exact adjectives each to desert and camel — sights from this week’s chapter.

e. Diagram “A fearful storm was raging,” hanging each adjective on a slanting line under its noun.

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Graded Lessons in English (1889), Lessons 22 and 23.

Adverbs — Manner, Time, Place

The ship sails gracefully; the ship sails immediately; the ship sails homeward. One word tells the manner, one the time, one the place of sailing — and all three belong to a single class. An adverb may also modify an adjective or another adverb: in “She decided too hastily,” hastily tells how she decided, and too tells how hastily.

Sort adverbs by the question each answers: When? — time. Where? — place. In what way? — manner. To what extent? — degree. In the copywork, Plutarch’s Theseus “immediately fell upon those that lay in ambuscade” — one adverb of time, and the whole speed of the ambush is in it. Hunt adverbs as you hunt adjectives — find the word being modified first, then put the question to it.

Definition. “An Adverb is a word used to modify a verb, an adjective, or an adverb.” Adverbs of time answer When?; of place, Where?; of manner, In what way?; of degree, To what extent?

a. In the copywork, which question does immediately answer? What verb does it modify?

b. Sort into the four classes: soon, hither, freely, intensely, daily, homeward.

c. “You should listen more attentively.” What does more modify? What does attentively modify?

d. One word, two masters: “An intensely painful operation was performed” and “The patient suffered intensely.” Tell what intensely modifies in each.

e. Diagram “The birds chirp merrily.”

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Graded Lessons in English (1889), Lessons 27 and 75.

Prepositions & Their Objects

Take Reed & Kellogg’s squirrel: The squirrel ran up a tree. Now swap the little word: around the tree, behind, down, into, over, through, to, under, from. The squirrel and the tree never move, yet the whole relation between the running and the tree changes nine times. Words that show such relations are prepositions.

Every preposition takes a noun or pronoun after it — its object, your OP tag. Test it by asking the preposition’s question: through what? — the mountain. In the copywork, the giants’ breath comes up through the mountain (OP mountain) and men call it the eruption of the volcano (OP volcano).

Prepositions are the smallest words with the longest reach: each drags a noun behind it and hands the pair to some other word as a modifier.

Definition. “A Preposition is a word that introduces a phrase modifier, and shows the relation, in sense, of its principal word to the word modified.”

a. In the copywork, name both prepositions and tag each object OP.

b. Say the squirrel sentence nine ways, using a different preposition each time.

c. Find preposition and OP: “Columbus was born at Genoa.” · “The forces of Hannibal were routed by Scipio.”

d. Build one sentence each around: through the fields · among the Titans · at the forge.

e. Diagram “Flowers preach to us”: the preposition slants down from the verb, and us rides its shelf.

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Graded Lessons in English (1889), Lesson 34.

Prepositional Phrases

A group of words can act as a single modifier. “The dew of the morning has passed away” means the morning dew — the phrase does an adjective’s work. “We shall go to that place” means we shall go there — that phrase does an adverb’s work. All the words of a phrase are taken together to perform one distinct office.

This week’s copywork from Julius Caesar is a riverbed of phrases: a tide in the affairs of men, taken at the flood, leads on to fortune. In a Reed & Kellogg diagram each phrase hangs below the base line: a slanting stroke for the preposition, a shelf for its object — you can see at a glance which word the whole phrase serves.

Definition. “A Phrase is a group of words denoting related ideas but not expressing a thought.” A phrase modifying a noun is equivalent to an adjective; one modifying the predicate is equivalent to an adverb.

a. List every prepositional phrase in the copywork lines; tag each OP.

b. Exchange phrase for word: a citizen of America → an —— citizen; we walked toward home → we walked ——.

c. “The finest trout in the lake are generally caught in the deepest water.” Which phrase works as an adjective, and which as an adverb?

d. Diagram the trout sentence on the board — it is the classic.

e. Build two sentences: one with a phrase modifying the subject, one with a phrase modifying the predicate.

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Graded Lessons in English (1889), Lesson 31.

Coordinating Conjunctions

Conjunctions are the joiners. And, but, and or connect equals: two words (“Lightning and electricity were identified by Franklin”), two phrases (“to Saratoga and to Niagara”), or two whole clauses (“He must increase, but I must decrease”). Because they join parts of the same rank, Reed & Kellogg call them co-ordinate. Mark the rank well: and never makes one part serve another; it only sets equal beside equal.

Your copywork joins two complete statements: The month was December, and winter reigned over all the regions east of the Mediterranean. Each half could stand alone; the comma before and marks the seam. (Notice, too, that December renames the subject after was — a PN.)

Definition. “A Conjunction is a word used to connect words, phrases, or clauses.” “Co-ordinate Conjunctions are such as connect words, phrases, or clauses of the same rank.”

a. In the copywork, what two things does and join? Name the subject and verb of each clause.

b. “The small but courageous band was finally overpowered.” What does but connect here?

c. “Vapors rise from the ocean and fall upon the land.” Does and join words, phrases, or clauses?

d. Compose three sentences: and joining two subjects; or joining two phrases; but joining two clauses — with the comma.

e. Diagram “War has ceased, and peace has come”: two base lines, a dotted line between for and.

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Graded Lessons in English (1889), Lessons 36 and 76.

Subordinating Conjunctions

Not all joiners join equals. In Antony’s line — “If you have tears, prepare to shed them now” — the word if fastens the clause you have tears onto the command as a modifier expressing condition. In “I will go, because you need me,” because attaches a clause expressing cause. The attached clause serves the main one; the ranks are unequal, so these are subordinate conjunctions.

Your copywork works the same way: When Aethra was delivered of a son cannot stand alone — it hangs on the main statement and tells the time of it. Keep a pocket list: when, while, because, if, although, until, that. Meet one of these, and expect a clause that leans. Read your Plutarch with an ear for them — scarcely a sentence goes by without one clause hung upon another.

Definition. “Subordinate Conjunctions are such as connect clauses of different rank” — they join a dependent clause to a clause of higher rank.

a. Read the copywork’s when-clause alone. Can it stand by itself? What does it tell about the naming of Theseus?

b. “I will go, because you need me.” Name the conjunction. Does its clause express time, cause, or condition?

c. Finish with a main clause: “—— while we sleep.” · “—— before winter comes.”

d. Sort into co-ordinate and subordinate: and, when, but, because, or, if.

e. Compose one if-sentence about Theseus and the black sail.

Adapted from Reed & Kellogg, Graded Lessons in English (1889), Lessons 59 and 76.

Appositive & Participial Phrases

“Webster, the statesman, was born in New Hampshire.” The noun statesman explains which Webster is meant; both words name the same man. Reed & Kellogg call this an explanatory modifier — your APP tag — and it usually sits between commas. Your copywork has one: Gesius, the keeper.

A participle is the verb form that only assumes an action instead of asserting it. Peter turned asserts; Peter, turning, said merely assumes the turning while said does the asserting. With its own objects and modifiers, a participle builds a participial phrase that describes a noun: “Truth, crushed to earth, will rise again.”

Both phrases are quiet renamers and describers — they add without asserting, which is why strong writers lean on them.

Definition. An Appositive is a noun set beside another noun to explain it, both naming the same person or thing. A Participle is “the form of the verb that merely assumes the act, being, or state.”

a. In the copywork, find the appositive; tag it APP and name the noun it renames.

b. “Milton, the great English poet, became blind.” Find the appositive.

c. “The general, riding to the front, led the attack.” Find the participial phrase and the noun it describes.

d. “Washington, having crossed the Delaware, attacked the Hessians.” A participle may be two or three words — find it.

e. Diagram “Truth, crushed to earth, will rise again” — the participle’s line bends, half slanting, half level.

Parse a Sentence Fully

This week you rehearse the whole ritual. To analyze is to take a sentence apart in order: name it a sentence and say why; give the subject; give the predicate; give any complement (DO, PN); then account for every modifier — word, phrase, and appositive. To parse is to take each word and name its class, for “naming the class to which a word belongs is the first step in parsing.”

Antony’s opening is a fine test. Friends, Romans, countrymen are independent words of address — set off by commas and standing outside the sentence’s frame, like interjections. The subject of lend is you, understood; ears is the DO; me tells to whom. In the second line, to bury Caesar tells why he comes.

Go slowly; the value is in the ritual itself. One sentence fully analyzed is worth ten skimmed.

Definition. “The Analysis of a sentence is the separation of it into its parts.” “Naming the class to which a word belongs is the first step in parsing.”

a. Full oral analysis: “Antony beheaded Cicero.” Tag S and DO.

b. “Caesar crossed the Rubicon.” Analyze; then parse each word by class.

c. “Brutus is an honourable man.” Name the linking verb; tag the PN.

d. Analyze the copywork lines, noting the words of address and the understood subject.

e. Diagram “Antony beheaded Cicero” on the board.

Term Review — Find Every Phrase

Gather the term in one hand. Eight classes of words — noun, pronoun, verb, adjective, adverb, preposition, conjunction, interjection — and three kinds of phrase: the prepositional (through the mountain), the appositive (Gesius, the keeper), and the participial (riding to the front). Every phrase, whatever its size, performs one office: it describes like an adjective, tells like an adverb, or renames like an appositive.

Definition. “A Phrase is a group of words denoting related ideas but not expressing a thought.” Before beginning, recite the eight part-of-speech definitions from memory.

a. “The steeples of the village pierced through the dense fog.” Find both phrases; give the office of each.

b. “The general, riding to the front, led the attack.” What kind of phrase?

c. “Gesius, the keeper, told his tale methodically.” What kind? Tag it.

d. “There is a tide in the affairs of men, which, taken at the flood, leads on to fortune.” Count the phrases; name each kind.

e. From this week’s reading find one phrase of each kind; diagram your favorite.
