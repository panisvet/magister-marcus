WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 1
Sorting Without Names
Before you can name a thing, you must learn to see how it differs.
Lesson 1.1
NAME
DATE
WEEK 1
Teacher PrepGather 12–15 varied natural objects or clear photos: a fern frond, a moss clump, a mushroom, a pine cone, a snail shell, a feather, a beetle, a leaf, a lichen-covered twig, a seed head. Do not label anything.
Lesson
Last year we asked what things ARE. This year we ask how they are ORDERED. Spread the objects out. Ask both students to sort them into groups — any groups — and then defend the rule they used. Then re-sort using a different rule. The point is not to be right; it is to discover that sorting always depends on which features you decide matter.
Hands-OnEach student sorts the collection twice, using two different rules (e.g. once by 'plant vs not-plant', once by 'soft vs hard'). Record both sorting rules and which objects were hardest to place. Those hard-to-place objects are exactly where classification gets interesting.
Vocabulary
classifygroupfeaturesort
Narration
Form IA · Oral
What rule did you use to sort? Which object was hardest to place, and why?
Form IIA · Written
Describe both of your sorting rules. Explain why the same object can belong to different groups depending on the rule. Which features do you think SHOULD matter most, and why?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw three of your objects and write the group you put each one in.
Form IIA
Draw your two sorting schemes as branching diagrams. Write a paragraph: what makes one sorting rule better than another for a scientist?
Watch for  Resist the urge to teach the 'correct' kingdoms yet. The whole unit lands better if students first feel the problem: nature does not come pre-labelled.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Ask the older student: is a mushroom more like a plant or more like an animal? Hold the question open — it is answered in Lesson 1.4.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 1
The Man Who Named the World
Carl Linnaeus gave every living thing two names — and a place.
Lesson 1.2
NAME
DATE
WEEK 1
Lesson
Read about Carl Linnaeus (1707–1778), the Swedish naturalist who devised the system of two-part Latin names still used today. Before Linnaeus, the same plant had a dozen long descriptive names in different countries. He gave each living thing a genus and a species — like a surname and a first name — so that a botanist in Sweden and one in Japan could know they meant the same creature.
Naming PracticeWrite your own two-part names for three household pets or garden creatures, surname (group) first: e.g. Felis domesticus. Then look up the real binomial name for one of them and compare.
Read
📖 Britannica Kids: Carolus Linnaeus
Vocabulary
Linnaeusbinomialgenusspeciestaxonomy
Narration
Form IA · Oral
Why did Linnaeus give every living thing two names instead of one?
Form IIA · Written
Narrate the problem Linnaeus solved. Explain how a two-part name works and why using Latin — a language no country 'owns' — was a wise choice.
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Copywork: 'Homo sapiens' — the name Linnaeus gave to human beings. Write what it means: 'wise man.'
Form IIA
Copywork the Linnaean ranks in order: Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species. Write one sentence on why the order runs from broadest to narrowest.
Watch for  Students often think the species name comes first. Reinforce: genus (the group) is capitalised and comes first; species is lowercase and comes second; both are italicised.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Linnaeus placed humans among the animals — bold for his day. Discuss: what does it mean that we can be both classified WITH the animals and yet be the ones DOING the classifying?
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 1
Building a Dichotomous Key
A key is a ladder of either/or questions that leads to a name.
Lesson 1.3
NAME
DATE
WEEK 2
Teacher PrepYou will need 6 leaves of clearly different shapes, or 6 shells, or 6 seeds.
Lesson
A dichotomous key sorts by asking one yes/no question at a time — 'dichotomous' means 'divided in two.' Each answer sends you down one of two branches until only one creature remains. Naturalists use keys every day to identify what they find.
Make a KeyLay out your 6 specimens. Write a key: Question 1 splits all 6 into two piles (e.g. 'edges smooth?' yes/no). Each pile gets its own next question, until every specimen sits alone at the end of a branch. Trade keys with your sibling and test whether theirs correctly identifies your specimens.
Vocabulary
dichotomous keybranchcoupletidentify
Narration
Form IA · Oral
How does asking one question at a time help you find the right name?
Form IIA · Written
Explain how a dichotomous key works. Why must each question have exactly two answers? What happens if a specimen fits neither?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw your key as a branching tree with your 6 specimens at the ends.
Form IIA
Paste or draw your key. Write a paragraph evaluating it: where did it work smoothly, and where did a specimen not fit cleanly? What does that tell you about real classification?
Watch for  The classic error is a first question that leaves an uneven or overlapping split. A good key's questions are unambiguous — the answer is never 'sort of.'
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Have the older student build a key that a stranger could use to identify the trees in your own yard.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 1
More Than Two Kingdoms
Not plant, not animal — and yet alive.
Lesson 1.4
NAME
DATE
WEEK 3
Lesson
For a long time people sorted all life into just two kingdoms: plants and animals. But a mushroom makes no food from sunlight as a plant does, nor does it hunt and move as an animal does — it belongs to its own kingdom, the fungi. And the tiniest living things, revealed only by the microscope, fit neither. Scientists now recognise several kingdoms, and above them the three great domains: Bacteria, Archaea, and Eukarya.
Sorting RevisitedReturn to your objects from Lesson 1.1. Now sort them into: plant, animal, fungus, or 'too small to see clearly.' Notice how the mushroom and the lichen forced you to invent new groups — exactly as scientists had to.
Read
📖 The Fairy-Land of Science — Buckley
Vocabulary
kingdomfungidomainEukaryaBacteria
Narration
Form IA · Oral
Why is a mushroom not a plant? Name the four kingdoms you sorted into.
Form IIA · Written
Explain why two kingdoms were not enough. Describe how a fungus differs from both a plant and an animal in how it gets its food.
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw one plant, one animal, one fungus. Label each with its kingdom.
Form IIA
Make a chart of the kingdoms you have met, with one distinguishing feature and one example for each. Add the three domains above them.
Watch for  Keep 'domain' and 'kingdom' distinct: domains are the three broadest branches of life; kingdoms sit inside them.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Introduce the idea that classification changes as we learn more — it is a map of our understanding, not a fixed decree. Ask: does the world change when scientists re-draw the map, or does our seeing change?
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 1
A Backyard Census
Classify what actually lives where you live.
Lesson 1.5
NAME
DATE
WEEK 4
Teacher PrepChoose a 1-metre square of yard, garden, or a single tree. Bring the hand lens.
Lesson
Real classification begins at home. Spend 30 quiet minutes cataloguing every living thing in one small square of ground — plants, insects, fungi, mosses, birds overhead. You are doing exactly what Linnaeus did on his expeditions, only in your own garden.
Field CensusList every distinct living thing you can find in your square. For each, assign the broadest group you are sure of (plant / animal / fungus / other). Count how many distinct kinds you found — this is the 'diversity' of your square.
Vocabulary
diversitycensushabitatspecimen
Narration
Form IA · Oral
How many different living kinds did you find? Which surprised you?
Form IIA · Written
Report your census. Which kingdom had the most kinds? Propose a reason. What might you have missed because it was too small, too fast, or hidden?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw your square and label five living things you found.
Form IIA
Draw a map of your square. Tally the kinds by kingdom. Write a paragraph on why even a tiny patch of ground holds more order than it first appears to.
Watch for  This lesson pairs with the year-long nature notebook. Encourage a return visit to the same square in a later season to see how the census changes.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Have the older student research how many species Linnaeus personally named (roughly 12,000) and reflect on the patience such work required.
