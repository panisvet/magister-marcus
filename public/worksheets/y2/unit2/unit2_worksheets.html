WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 2
A New Visible World
There is nothing so small as to escape our inquiry.
Lesson 2.1
NAME
DATE
WEEK 6
Teacher PrepA hand lens for each student; if you own a microscope, prepare it. Otherwise a strong magnifier and a smartphone macro lens work.
Lesson
Robert Hooke wrote that the microscope revealed 'a new visible World discovered to the understanding.' Before any reading, look closely: examine a torn edge of paper, a strand of your own hair, a grain of salt, the skin of an onion, a housefly's wing. The point is astonishment — the ordinary is not ordinary once magnified.
Hands-OnExamine five ordinary objects under the greatest magnification you have. For each, draw what you see and write one thing that was invisible to the naked eye. Save the onion skin — you will meet Hooke's discovery in it next lesson.
Vocabulary
microscopemagnifylensspecimen
Narration
Form IA · Oral
What did the magnifier show you that your eyes alone could not?
Form IIA · Written
Describe the most surprising thing you magnified. Why does Hooke call the microscope's revelation 'a new visible world'? What does it suggest about how much of creation lies below our ordinary sight?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw two magnified objects. Write one word for how each looked: rough? patterned? alive?
Form IIA
Draw three magnified objects with careful detail. Write a paragraph: how does seeing small change how large the world feels?
Watch for  Wonder first, vocabulary second. If a microscope is unavailable, the macro camera on a phone genuinely suffices for this unit.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Have the older student read Hooke's own preface language (Micrographia, opening pages) and note his argument that instruments 'repair' the weakness of our senses.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 2
Hooke Names the Cell
He looked at cork, and saw little rooms.
Lesson 2.2
NAME
DATE
WEEK 6
Lesson
In 1665 Robert Hooke sliced a thin sheet of cork, put it under his microscope, and saw it was full of tiny empty compartments. They reminded him of the small rooms — cells — where monks lived. He borrowed the word, and it stuck: every living thing is built of cells. Read the relevant passage of Micrographia (or a summary for the younger student).
See What Hooke SawPeel the thin, transparent inner skin from an onion layer. Lay it flat and examine it under magnification. You should see the same orderly compartments Hooke saw in cork. You are repeating one of the most famous observations in the history of science.
Read
📖 Micrographia — Robert Hooke
Vocabulary
cellcorkcompartmenttissue
Narration
Form IA · Oral
What was Hooke looking at when he first used the word 'cell'? Why did he choose that word?
Form IIA · Written
Narrate Hooke's discovery. Explain why naming the compartments 'cells' was more than a label — how did having a word change what scientists could then investigate?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw the cells you saw in the onion skin, like little rooms in a wall.
Form IIA
Draw your onion cells. Copywork Hooke: 'there is a new visible world discovered to the understanding.' Write what he meant.
Watch for  Hooke saw only the empty cell WALLS of dead cork — not living contents. That distinction sets up Leeuwenhoek's living 'animalcules' next.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Discuss how a single well-chosen word (cell) organised centuries of later biology. Words are tools of thought.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 2
Leeuwenhoek's Little Animals
A cloth-merchant found a whole world swimming in a drop of water.
Lesson 2.3
NAME
DATE
WEEK 7
Lesson
Antonie van Leeuwenhoek was a Dutch draper, not a trained scientist. He ground his own tiny, powerful lenses and pointed them at pond water, rain water, even the scrapings from his own teeth. To his astonishment, he found them teeming with living things he called 'animalcules' — the first person ever to see bacteria and single-celled creatures.
Pond-Water SafariCollect a jar of pond, puddle, or vase water (the greener the better). Examine a drop under your best magnification. Look for anything that MOVES on its own. Even at low power you may catch darting specks — Leeuwenhoek's animalcules.
Read
📖 Britannica Kids: Anton van Leeuwenhoek📖 Marvels of Pond-life (Slack) — Project Gutenberg
Vocabulary
animalculemicroorganismprotozoabacteria
Narration
Form IA · Oral
What did Leeuwenhoek find in a drop of pond water? What did he call them?
Form IIA · Written
Narrate Leeuwenhoek's discovery. He was not a university scientist — what qualities let a cloth-merchant see what the learned had missed? What does that suggest about who can do science?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw what you saw moving in your water drop, even if it was only a speck.
Form IIA
Draw your pond-water observations. Write a paragraph comparing Hooke's dead cork cells with Leeuwenhoek's living animalcules — what did each man add to the story of the cell?
Watch for  Manage expectations: a home microscope may show only motion and shape, not detail. The motion alone is the point — it is unmistakably alive.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Leeuwenhoek kept his lens-grinding method secret his whole life. Discuss the tension between the scientific duty to share and the human wish to keep an advantage.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 2
Inside the Cell
Every cell is a tiny, busy household.
Lesson 2.4
NAME
DATE
WEEK 8
Lesson
Since Hooke and Leeuwenhoek, we have learned what fills a living cell. Every cell has a boundary (the membrane), a jelly-like inside (cytoplasm), and — in plants and animals — a control centre (the nucleus). Plant cells add a stiff outer wall and green chloroplasts where food is made from light. Think of a cell as a small household: walls, an inside, a manager, and (in plants) a kitchen.
Model a CellBuild or draw a plant cell and an animal cell side by side. Label membrane, cytoplasm, nucleus, and (plant only) cell wall and chloroplasts. A cheap model: jelly or pudding in a bag (cytoplasm in a membrane) with a grape for a nucleus.
Vocabulary
membranecytoplasmnucleuscell wallchloroplast
Narration
Form IA · Oral
Name three parts of a cell and what each one does.
Form IIA · Written
Explain the main parts of a cell and their jobs. Give two differences between a plant cell and an animal cell, and explain why a plant needs each extra part.
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw a plant cell and an animal cell. Label three parts of each.
Form IIA
Draw and fully label both cell types. Write a paragraph using the 'household' comparison, then note where the comparison breaks down — no model is perfect.
Watch for  Don't overload with organelles at this level. Membrane, cytoplasm, nucleus, wall, chloroplast are plenty; deeper detail comes in later years.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Introduce that chloroplasts (plant food-making) connect directly to Unit 3's photosynthesis — the cell is where the plant's chemistry actually happens.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 2
Cheek, Leaf, and Thread
Three specimens, three lessons in what cells look like.
Lesson 2.5
NAME
DATE
WEEK 9
Teacher PrepIf you have a microscope with slides: a cheek-swab slide (animal), a thin leaf-skin peel (plant), and a cotton thread. Otherwise use the best available magnification and prepared-slide photos.
Lesson
Now that you know what to look for, examine real cells with intent. A cheek swab shows loose animal cells with no wall. A leaf peel shows plant cells packed in tidy walls, some with green dots. A cotton thread shows the empty walls of dead plant cells — cotton is pure cellulose.
Compare ThreeExamine (or study prepared images of) the three specimens. For each, note: are the cells packed tight or loose? Do they have stiff walls? Any green? Record which is plant and which is animal, and the evidence for each call.
Vocabulary
cellulosestructurefunctionpacked
Narration
Form IA · Oral
How could you tell a plant cell from an animal cell just by looking?
Form IIA · Written
Describe the visible differences between your specimens. Which single feature was the most reliable clue to 'plant vs animal,' and why?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw the three specimens. Circle the one that is an animal cell.
Form IIA
Draw all three with labels. Write a short paragraph: how does structure follow function — why do plant cells have walls and animal cells do not?
Watch for  No microscope? High-quality prepared-slide photos are widely available and this comparison works well as guided image study.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Cotton, wood, and paper are all cellulose cell walls — the leftover architecture of once-living plant cells. Ask the student to find three cellulose objects in the house.
