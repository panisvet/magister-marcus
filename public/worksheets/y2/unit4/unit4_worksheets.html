WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 4
Light Travels Straight
You cannot see light itself — only what it touches.
Lesson 4.1
NAME
DATE
WEEK 16
Teacher PrepA darkened room, a small bright flashlight, a mirror, a comb or slotted card.
Lesson
Light is so ordinary we forget to ask what it is. Begin with what it DOES. In a dark room, shine a flashlight and notice you cannot see the beam itself — only the objects it lands on, and any dust it passes through. Shine it through the slots of a comb and you get straight rays. Light travels in straight lines until something stops or bends it.
Hands-OnDarken the room. Shine the flashlight through a comb onto paper to make straight rays. Then place a mirror in a ray's path and watch it bounce off at a matching angle. Trace the rays you see with a pencil on the paper.
Vocabulary
raybeamreflectstraight-line
Narration
Form IA · Oral
Why can't you see the flashlight's beam in clean air, but you can where there's dust?
Form IIA · Written
Describe your observations. Light travels in straight lines — how do the comb-rays and the mirror bounce both demonstrate this? Where did the light bend, and where did it merely bounce?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw your flashlight, the comb, and the straight rays hitting the paper.
Form IIA
Draw your ray diagram, including the mirror bounce. Label the incoming ray and the reflected ray. Write a sentence: what did the dust let you see that clean air did not?
Watch for  The 'you can't see light itself' point is genuinely surprising to children and worth dwelling on — we see BY light, not light.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Tyndall himself studied why the sky is blue — light scattered by tiny particles. Preview: the same dust that reveals the beam is why sunsets are red.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 4
Reflection — The Law of the Bounce
The angle in equals the angle out.
Lesson 4.2
NAME
DATE
WEEK 16
Teacher PrepA mirror, a protractor, the flashlight-and-comb setup.
Lesson
When light hits a smooth surface it bounces off in a predictable way: the angle at which it arrives equals the angle at which it leaves. This one simple law explains mirrors, why you see your reflection, and how a periscope lets a submarine see above water.
Measure the AngleShine a single ray at a mirror lying on paper. Draw a line straight up from the point where the ray hits (the 'normal'). Measure the angle of the incoming ray and the outgoing ray from that line. They should match. Try three different incoming angles.
Read
📖 Six Lectures on Light — John Tyndall
Vocabulary
reflectionangle of incidenceangle of reflectionnormal
Narration
Form IA · Oral
When light hits a mirror, how does the bounce-out angle compare to the come-in angle?
Form IIA · Written
State the law of reflection and explain what the 'normal' line is. Why does a rough surface (like paper) not give a clear reflection even though the same law applies to every point?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw a ray hitting a mirror and bouncing off. Show both angles are equal.
Form IIA
Record your three measured angle-pairs in a small table. Write a paragraph explaining reflection and one everyday device that depends on it.
Watch for  Introduce 'the normal' (the line perpendicular to the surface) carefully — angles are measured from it, not from the surface.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Ask why a still pond gives a clear reflection but a rippled one does not — same law, scrambled surface.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 4
Refraction — The Broken Pencil
Light bends when it changes speed.
Lesson 4.3
NAME
DATE
WEEK 17
Teacher PrepA clear glass of water, a pencil, a coin, a bowl.
Lesson
Put a pencil in a glass of water and it looks broken at the surface. Look at a coin at the bottom of a bowl and it seems shifted. Light bends — refracts — when it passes from one clear substance into another, because it changes speed. This bending is the secret behind every lens: eyeglasses, telescopes, cameras, and your own eye.
Hands-On1) Stand a pencil in a glass of water and view from the side — note the 'break.' 2) Place a coin in an empty bowl, back away until it just disappears behind the rim, then have someone slowly pour water in without moving the bowl. The coin reappears — the light from it is bent up to your eye.
Vocabulary
refractionbendlensmedium
Narration
Form IA · Oral
Why does a pencil look broken in a glass of water?
Form IIA · Written
Explain refraction using the coin-in-the-bowl demonstration. What does it mean to say light 'changes speed' when it enters water, and why does that make it bend?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw the pencil looking broken in the glass.
Form IIA
Draw both demonstrations with light-ray lines showing where the bending happens. Write a paragraph connecting refraction to how a lens works.
Watch for  The reappearing-coin demo is striking and reliable — practise the disappearance point before doing it with the student.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Tyndall discusses how Newton and others explained refraction. Have the older student read a short passage and note how competing theories of light both tried to explain the same bending.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 4
The Prism and the Rainbow
White light is every colour hiding together.
Lesson 4.4
NAME
DATE
WEEK 18
Teacher PrepA glass prism (or a clear glass of water on a sunny windowsill), white paper.
Lesson
Isaac Newton let a beam of white sunlight pass through a glass prism and split it into a band of colours — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet. He proved that white light is not plain at all: it is all the colours mixed together, and the prism simply bends each colour by a slightly different amount, spreading them out. A rainbow is the sky doing the same thing with raindrops.
Make a SpectrumOn a sunny day, let light pass through a prism (or a glass of water at the edge of a sunny table) onto white paper. Find the band of colours. Note their order — it is always the same. Then try to recombine them or spin a colour wheel to make white again.
Read
📖 Six Lectures on Light — John Tyndall
Vocabulary
prismspectrumrefractionwavelength
Narration
Form IA · Oral
What did Newton discover when he passed white light through a prism?
Form IIA · Written
Explain Newton's prism experiment and what it proved about white light. Why does a prism spread the colours out instead of keeping them mixed?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw a prism splitting white light into a rainbow. Colour the band in order.
Form IIA
Draw the prism spectrum labelled with the colour order. Write a paragraph: how is a rainbow in the sky the same physics as your prism?
Watch for  Colour order is fixed (red bends least, violet most). If a prism is unavailable, a glass of water on a sunny sill or a CD's surface both make usable spectra.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Each colour is light of a different wavelength; the prism bends short wavelengths (violet) most. Introduce that our eyes only see a narrow band of a much wider spectrum.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 4
How the Eye Catches Light
The eye is a living lens.
Lesson 4.5
NAME
DATE
WEEK 19
Lesson
Everything you have learned this unit meets in your own eye. Light passes through a clear front, is bent by the lens (refraction!), and lands on the retina at the back, where it forms a tiny upside-down picture. The eye is a camera made of living tissue. This connects directly to the vision lesson from Y1's human-body unit — now you understand the physics behind it.
The Pinhole CameraMake a simple pinhole viewer: a small hole in one end of a box or tube, wax paper or thin white paper at the other. Point the hole at a bright window. A dim, upside-down image of the scene appears on the paper — exactly what happens at the back of your eye.
Read
📖 Six Lectures on Light — John Tyndall
Vocabulary
lensretinapupilimagefocus
Narration
Form IA · Oral
How is your eye like a camera? What lands on the back of it?
Form IIA · Written
Trace the path of light through the eye, using the words refraction, lens, and retina. Why is the image on the retina upside-down, and how does the brain manage this?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw an eye with light coming in and an upside-down picture at the back.
Form IIA
Draw a labelled eye diagram (front, lens, retina) beside your pinhole-camera diagram. Write a paragraph on how the two work by the same principle.
Watch for  The upside-down image genuinely appears in a good pinhole viewer — build one in advance to confirm your hole size and darkness.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Eyeglasses correct focus by bending light before it reaches the eye's own lens. Have the older student explain, in refraction terms, what a lens for a far-sighted person must do.
