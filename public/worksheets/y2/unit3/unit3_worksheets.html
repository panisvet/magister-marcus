WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 3
The Drinking Stem
A plant is mostly a system for moving water.
Lesson 3.1
NAME
DATE
WEEK 11
Teacher PrepA white carnation or celery stalk with leaves; a cup; food colouring. Start the day before if you can.
Lesson
Last year you learned that plants make food from light. This year: how does a plant move water from its roots all the way to a leaf at the top of a tree, with no heart and no pump? Set a white flower or a celery stalk in coloured water and watch. Within hours the colour climbs the stem and reaches the petals or leaves — carried by tiny tubes.
Hands-OnPlace a white carnation or split celery stalk in strongly coloured water. Observe every few hours and again the next day. Then cut the stem crosswise and find the coloured dots or lines — those are the vascular tubes (xylem) that carried the water up.
Vocabulary
vascularxylemstemtransport
Narration
Form IA · Oral
How did the colour get to the top of the flower? What carried it?
Form IIA · Written
Describe what you observed. A tree has no pump, yet lifts water 30 metres. Propose how tiny tubes plus the pull of evaporating leaves might accomplish this.
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw the flower before and after. Draw the coloured dots in the cut stem.
Form IIA
Draw the stem cross-section with the vascular bundles labelled. Write a paragraph explaining the journey of one water molecule from cup to petal.
Watch for  The reveal is the cut cross-section — do not skip it. Seeing the coloured tubes makes 'vascular' concrete.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Introduce transpiration: leaves lose water to the air, and that loss pulls more water up behind it — like sipping through a straw the length of a tree.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 3
Vascular and Non-Vascular
Some plants have plumbing; the simplest do not.
Lesson 3.2
NAME
DATE
WEEK 11
Lesson
The tubes you found are why most plants can grow tall: vascular plants (trees, flowers, ferns) have xylem to carry water up and phloem to carry food down. But the simplest plants — mosses and liverworts — have no such plumbing. They must stay small and low, soaking up water directly, which is why moss grows as a thin damp carpet and never as a tree.
Find Both KindsGo outside and find one clearly vascular plant (with a real stem and veined leaves) and one non-vascular plant (a moss cushion on a rock or wall). Feel the difference: one is structured and upright; the other is soft, low, and damp.
Read
📖 The Fairy-Land of Science — Buckley
Vocabulary
vascularnon-vascularphloemmosstranspiration
Narration
Form IA · Oral
Why can a tree grow tall but a moss cannot?
Form IIA · Written
Explain the difference between vascular and non-vascular plants. Connect a plant's plumbing to how big it can grow and where it must live.
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw a tall vascular plant and a low moss. Label which has tubes.
Form IIA
Draw both plants. Write a paragraph: how does a plant's internal structure set the limits of its size and habitat?
Watch for  Students conflate 'moss' with 'small plant.' Emphasise the real distinction is internal plumbing, not size.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Ferns are vascular but reproduce by spores, not seeds — a useful bridge to the reproduction lesson.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 3
Photosynthesis, Up Close
Light in, sugar out — the reaction that feeds the world.
Lesson 3.3
NAME
DATE
WEEK 12
Lesson
You learned last year THAT plants make food from light. Now the how: inside the green chloroplasts you met in Unit 2, a leaf takes in carbon dioxide from the air and water from the roots, and — using the energy of sunlight — builds sugar, releasing oxygen as its by-product. Nearly every breath you take and every meal you eat traces back to this one reaction.
The Bubbling LeafSubmerge a fresh green leaf (or sprig of pondweed) in a clear jar of water in bright sunlight. Watch for tiny bubbles forming on the leaf surface — that is oxygen, the visible by-product of photosynthesis happening in real time.
Vocabulary
photosynthesischlorophyllcarbon dioxideoxygenglucose
Narration
Form IA · Oral
What three things does a leaf need to make food? What does it give off?
Form IIA · Written
Explain photosynthesis in your own words: the inputs, the energy source, the product, and the by-product. Why is the oxygen 'waste' to the plant but life to you?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw a leaf with arrows: sunlight, water, air going in; sugar and bubbles coming out.
Form IIA
Draw a labelled diagram of photosynthesis. Write the word-equation (carbon dioxide + water + light → sugar + oxygen) and a paragraph on why this single reaction underlies almost all life.
Watch for  Keep the chemistry at word-equation level; the balanced formula belongs to a later year. The bubbling-leaf demo is the memorable anchor.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Photosynthesis and the candle-flame combustion from Y1 Unit 6 are near-mirror images: one stores light-energy in sugar, the other releases it. Have the older student diagram the two as opposites.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 3
Dissecting a Flower
A flower is a plant's plan for the next generation.
Lesson 3.4
NAME
DATE
WEEK 13
Teacher PrepA large, simple flower per student — lily, tulip, or alstroemeria work well. Tweezers, tape, a sheet of paper.
Lesson
A flower is not decoration; it is reproduction. Take one apart carefully and you will find the same organs in almost every flowering plant: petals to attract, stamens that make pollen (the male part), and a central pistil that receives pollen and holds the future seeds (the female part).
Flower DissectionGently remove each part of your flower in turn and tape it to paper in order: sepals, petals, stamens (find the powdery pollen), pistil. Slice the base of the pistil open to find the tiny ovules — the future seeds. Label every part.
Vocabulary
petalstamenpistilpollenovule
Narration
Form IA · Oral
Which part of the flower makes pollen? Which part becomes the seeds?
Form IIA · Written
Name the flower's parts and each one's role in reproduction. Explain how a flower's beauty and scent serve a practical purpose.
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw your dissected flower with the parts labelled: petal, stamen, pistil.
Form IIA
Draw a fully labelled flower diagram. Write a paragraph on how a flower is an elegant solution to the problem of a rooted, unmoving organism needing to reproduce.
Watch for  Some flowers are single-sex; choose a complete flower (lily/tulip) so all parts are present in one specimen.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Connect to the bees and pollinators studied in Y1 Unit 1 — the flower's colour and scent are advertisements aimed at exactly those insects.
WONDERS OF THE CREATED WORLD · YEAR TWO · UNIT 3
Seeds, Spores, and Cones
Three different answers to one question: how to begin again.
Lesson 3.5
NAME
DATE
WEEK 14
Lesson
Not every plant makes seeds in flowers. Ferns and mosses scatter dust-like spores. Pines and firs hold their seeds in cones. Flowering plants wrap seeds inside fruit. Three strategies, one goal — carrying life forward to the next generation. Examine examples of each you have gathered.
Compare Three StrategiesLay out a fruit with seeds, a pine cone (tap it over dark paper to release winged seeds), and the underside of a fern frond (find the brown spore dots). Compare how each protects and disperses its offspring.
Read
📖 The Fairy-Land of Science — Buckley
Vocabulary
seedsporeconefruitdispersal
Narration
Form IA · Oral
Name three different ways plants make new plants.
Form IIA · Written
Compare seeds, spores, and cones as reproductive strategies. What are the advantages of wrapping a seed in fruit versus scattering many tiny spores?
Nature Journal & Copywork
Form IA
Draw a seed, a spore-dot fern, and a cone. Label each.
Form IIA
Draw all three strategies. Write a paragraph: why might a plant that makes millions of tiny spores and one that makes a few well-protected seeds both succeed?
Watch for  This lesson deliberately deepens Y1 Unit 2's seed-dispersal work — invite the student to recall it.
Dig deeper (Form IIA)  Have the older student classify each strategy by plant group (ferns=spores, conifers=cones, flowering plants=seeds-in-fruit) — tying reproduction back to Unit 1's classification.
